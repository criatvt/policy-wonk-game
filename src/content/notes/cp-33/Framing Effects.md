# Framing Effects

Framing is the selection of which aspects of an issue to highlight and which to leave in the background. A tax increase can be framed as "necessary to fund schools" (foregrounding the use) or as "another burden on families" (foregrounding the cost). The same numerical change produces different public responses depending on which frame is operative when audiences encounter it.

Framing isn't manipulation in any sinister sense — every communication frames, because every communication has to select what to put in the foreground and what to leave out. The question is whether the framing is examined or unconscious. Examined framing is honest about its choices; unconscious framing absorbs the choices from the surrounding discourse.

For policy communicators, the practical work is to *choose* the frame rather than absorb it. A frame that emphasises the benefits the policy is intended to deliver tends to support the policy. A frame that emphasises the costs tends to undermine it. Both can be technically accurate; both leave parts of the picture out. The political contest over framing is often what determines policy outcomes more than the underlying substantive merits.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy communicators who pick their frame deliberately, and stick with it across communication cycles, tend to shape how the public understands the policy. Those who let the frame be set by critics tend to find themselves explaining the policy on someone else's terms.
