# Misinformation

Misinformation about policy is now a routine operational problem. False claims about what a policy does, what it costs, who benefits, and what alternatives exist circulate through both organic and deliberate channels. Some of the misinformation comes from honest misunderstandings; some from sceptical critics pushing harder claims than the evidence supports; some from bad-faith actors deliberately spreading falsehoods. The mix varies but the operational problem is similar across them.

The standard response is *prompt correction with accurate alternatives*. The correction needs to come quickly — delays let the misinformation spread further and become harder to displace. It needs to be clear about what is wrong and what is right. It typically needs an *alternative explanation* that lets the audience understand the underlying situation correctly, not just a denial of the false claim. Audiences left with "the claim was wrong but here's nothing about what's actually true" are not well served.

The hard part is that *repeating false claims even to deny them* can reinforce them. The audience's memory often retains the claim but not the denial. The right correction technique avoids prominent repetition of the false claim, leading instead with the accurate statement. "The new policy provides X" works better than "Despite claims that the policy does Y, it actually provides X" — the former lodges the accurate version without the false version.

Underlying concerns matter. Misinformation often spreads because it taps into real concerns that the audience holds. Correction that addresses both the false claim and the underlying concern is more durable than correction that only addresses the surface claim.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes operating in contested public environments need rapid-response capacity for misinformation. The investment is technical (monitoring channels, prepared response templates, fast-decision frameworks) and political (deciding which claims warrant response and which don't). Without it, false claims set the public understanding and accurate communication can't catch up.
