# News Cycles and Corrections

A robust finding from political-psychology research is that accurate factual corrections often fail to change minds about politically charged issues — and sometimes strengthen the false beliefs they were trying to correct. The mechanism is *identity-protective cognition*: political beliefs are tied to group identity, and factual challenges to those beliefs feel like personal or social threats. The audience responds by defending the belief rather than updating it.

This is the so-called *backfire effect*. Subsequent research has refined the original strong claim — backfire is real but not universal, and depends on how the correction is delivered — but the underlying dynamic of identity-protective resistance is well-documented. Telling a political partisan that their preferred candidate made a false claim does not, on average, reduce the partisan's support for that candidate. Telling a vaccine sceptic that vaccines are safe does not, on average, increase vaccination intention. Telling a climate sceptic about scientific consensus does not, on average, increase support for climate policy.

The implication for policy communicators is that the standard model of "correct the falsehood; truth will out" doesn't work well for identity-linked beliefs. Effective communication on identity-charged topics requires different approaches: connecting the new information to values the audience already holds; using messengers the audience already trusts; framing the issue in ways that don't trigger identity defence; delivering corrections in non-confrontational settings; and allowing time for gradual updating rather than expecting immediate belief revision.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes attempting to shift public understanding on identity-charged issues — vaccines, climate, gun policy, immigration — typically fail through direct factual confrontation. Programmes that work indirectly through trusted messengers and value-congruent framings tend to make slower but real progress. Recognising the structure of the problem is what makes the slower path strategic rather than defeatist.
