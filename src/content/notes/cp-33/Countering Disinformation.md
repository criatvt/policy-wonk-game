# Countering Disinformation

Disinformation — the deliberate production and spread of falsehoods designed to deceive — is a different operational problem from honest misunderstanding. The producers are working actively to make the false claim persuasive; they typically use vivid framing, emotional appeals, identity hooks, and platform optimisation; and they often coordinate across channels to amplify each message. Countering it requires more sophisticated responses than simple correction would suggest.

The backfire effect makes the standard approaches risky. Direct correction can entrench the audience's belief in the false claim, particularly when the claim is identity-linked. Aggressive labelling of content as "disinformation" can produce backlash from audiences that suspect motivated suppression. Centralised fact-checking has limited reach into the audiences most exposed to disinformation.

The mitigation strategies that show some effectiveness combine several elements. *Pre-bunking* — exposing audiences to weakened versions of likely disinformation before they encounter the real version — can build resistance ahead of time. *Trusted messengers from within the affected community* — local leaders, religious figures, community organisations — can correct disinformation in ways central authorities can't. *Inoculation through media literacy* — teaching audiences to recognise the techniques of disinformation — helps audiences resist it across many specific claims. *Patience and repetition* — correction over time through trusted channels — gradually shifts understanding even when single corrections don't.

Quick reactive correction has its place too, particularly when disinformation about an immediate policy implementation would cause real harm (vaccine campaigns, emergency public-safety information). But the broader work of building resilient information environments is slower and more structural than reactive correction alone.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes facing organised disinformation campaigns need the full toolkit: rapid response, trusted-messenger partnerships, pre-bunking when possible, media literacy investment, and institutional patience. Programmes that rely only on reactive correction tend to be overwhelmed; programmes that invest in the broader architecture have more chance of sustained credibility.
