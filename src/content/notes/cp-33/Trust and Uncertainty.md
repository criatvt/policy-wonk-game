# Trust and Uncertainty

A consistent finding in the policy-communication literature is that audiences trust communicators who are honest about uncertainty more than communicators who project false certainty. Performative certainty is brittle — when the uncertainty inevitably surfaces, the credibility collapse is substantial. Honest uncertainty is durable — audiences accept that genuinely uncertain situations call for caveated guidance.

The COVID-era policy communication provides extensive material on both sides. Governments and public-health agencies that maintained the line that "the science is settled" on contested or rapidly evolving questions lost credibility when the evolving evidence overturned earlier guidance. Mask policy, school-reopening policy, transmission via surfaces, the value of various interventions — all of these saw rapid evidence shifts that contradicted earlier communications. Communicators who had been honest about uncertainty earlier ("we think this, here's what we know, here's what we'll watch for, we may update") retained trust through the shifts. Communicators who had projected certainty lost it.

The hard part is that audiences also want guidance. "We don't know" alone is not useful when people need to make decisions. The discipline is to give the best available guidance while being honest about the confidence behind it, and to update visibly as new evidence comes in. "Based on what we know now, we recommend X. We don't yet have data on Y. We expect more clarity in [timeframe]" is more useful than either pretended certainty or pure agnosticism.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes operating under genuine uncertainty — pandemic response, financial-stability work, novel-technology regulation — depend on this discipline. The communications style of acknowledged uncertainty plus best-current-guidance is what allows policy to operate effectively in situations where the underlying facts are still being learned.
