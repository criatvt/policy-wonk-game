# Policy Paradox and Narratives

The deeper insight Deborah Stone packages in the term "policy paradox" is that most consequential policy choices involve real trade-offs between values that are each defensible on their own terms. Freedom and security. Liberty and equality. Growth and environment. Privacy and accountability. Efficiency and resilience. Each pair contains goods that competing constituencies legitimately want, and any policy choice has to balance them rather than maximise one.

For policy communication, this poses a recurring difficulty. The political rhetoric of single-value framings ("we will deliver freedom") is more emotionally satisfying than the rhetoric of trade-offs ("we will balance freedom against security, accepting some loss of each"). The single-value rhetoric wins applause in the moment; the trade-off rhetoric is more honest about what the policy actually does. Audiences that absorb the single-value framing feel betrayed when they discover that the policy didn't deliver the single value undiluted.

The skill of policy communication around paradoxes is to acknowledge the trade-offs honestly without losing the audience. Concretely: name the values that are in tension; explain why this particular balance is being struck; acknowledge what each constituency is giving up; explain how the trade-off can be revisited if conditions change. Audiences treated this way generally respect the honesty even when they would have preferred a different balance.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy communication that papers over trade-offs tends to produce backlash when the unmentioned trade-offs surface in implementation. Policy communication that names the trade-offs has more durable political support because audiences cannot accuse it of having concealed what the policy actually did.
