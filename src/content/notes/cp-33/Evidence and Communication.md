# Evidence and Motivated Reasoning

Motivated reasoning is the psychological pattern by which people interpret evidence selectively, applying tougher scrutiny to evidence that disconfirms their prior beliefs and looser scrutiny to evidence that confirms them. The bias operates unconsciously and is not the same as bad faith; the people doing it sincerely believe they are evaluating the evidence neutrally.

The mechanism applies across many domains. A doctor with prescribing preferences may dismiss safety studies that conflict with those preferences while accepting confirming studies. A policymaker with ideological commitments may scrutinise opposing analysis more harshly than supporting analysis. Citizens evaluating policy claims tend to find confirming evidence more compelling than disconfirming evidence on the same issue.

For policy communicators, the implication is that providing more evidence doesn't reliably change minds. The audience absorbing the new evidence is not a neutral receiver; it is an active interpreter who will work harder to fit the evidence into existing beliefs. Effective communication has to anticipate this and adapt accordingly.

Specific tactics include: providing evidence through trusted intermediaries who the audience does not pre-suspect; framing evidence in terms compatible with the audience's existing values; offering alternative explanations that let the audience update without feeling they made a mistake; acknowledging the underlying concerns that motivated the original belief, even when contradicting the specific factual claim; and accepting that belief revision often takes time and repeated exposure rather than single-instance correction.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes that rely on "showing the evidence" without engaging the motivated-reasoning dynamics typically disappoint. Programmes that anticipate motivated reasoning and design around it have more realistic expectations and better outcomes. The dynamic is universal, including in audiences and analysts who consider themselves objective.
