# Policy Communication

This module is about the working discipline of explaining policy to people who don't already know it. The audiences are multiple — citizens, media, civil society, international partners, internal stakeholders — and each requires a slightly different message. The channels are multiple — official statements, press briefings, social media, broadcast, long-form journalism, public consultations — and each operates on different timescales and incentives. The work of policy communication is the daily craft of moving the right message through the right channel to the right audience at the right time.

The texts in this module include Deborah Stone's analysis of policy narratives in *Policy Paradox*, Indian-context essays on policy communication in an age of information overload, and the broader literature on persuasion, framing, and the social-media transformation of the communication environment. The unifying theme is that communication is not a thin layer added on top of policy after the substantive work is done; it is part of the policy work itself, and policy that is poorly communicated tends to fail regardless of its substantive merits.

Topics in this module:

- **Framing and narrative** — how problems get defined rhetorically, why narratives outperform statistics, what Deborah Stone's "policy paradox" framework reveals.
- **Trust, credibility, and legitimacy** — what makes policy communication believable, what destroys credibility, and how the bond between government and citizens gets maintained or lost.
- **Channels and audiences** — segmenting audiences, working with media, the changing politics of social-media platforms.
- **Misinformation and the backfire effect** — why correcting falsehoods is harder than it should be, and what works when direct correction fails.
- **Crisis communication, implementation communication, and feedback** — what to do when policy is moving fast or facing pushback, and how to use communication as a learning loop.
- **Stakeholder engagement and consultation** — what genuine public consultation looks like and how to tell it apart from performative versions.
