# Communication Channels

The set of channels available for policy communication has expanded considerably from the broadcast-era dominance of major newspapers and television. The contemporary mix includes: traditional media (newspapers, TV, radio), official government channels (websites, press releases, public-information advertisements), social media (multiple platforms with very different demographics), direct mail and SMS, community channels (local-language radio, community groups, religious networks), influencer channels (specific commentators, content creators, podcasters), and direct public engagement (consultations, town halls).

Each channel has characteristic strengths and characteristic audiences. Traditional media reach audiences who still consume news through formal channels — typically older, more politically engaged. Social-media platforms reach audiences who don't consume traditional news, with sharp demographic differences across platforms. Community channels reach specific communities through trusted local intermediaries — often more effectively than central broadcasting for the segments they reach. Direct channels (SMS, postal mail) reach specific named beneficiaries with action-required information.

Effective policy communication uses *multiple channels in coordination*, not one channel exclusively. The same core message goes through different channels in formats appropriate to each. The redundancy is part of the design: each channel covers some audience that the others miss, and the combined coverage approaches comprehensiveness in a way no single channel can.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes that rely on a single channel — typically traditional media — systematically under-reach the audiences who don't consume that channel. Programmes that use multi-channel strategies tend to achieve substantially better coverage, even though they require more communications work and more careful coordination across channels.
