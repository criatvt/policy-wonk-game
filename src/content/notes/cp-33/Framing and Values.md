# Framing and Values

A policy frame is more persuasive when it connects the policy to values the audience already holds than when it asks the audience to adopt new values. A climate-policy frame that emphasises "protecting our grandchildren's future" resonates with audiences that value family legacy. The same policy framed as "preventing economic loss" resonates with audiences that value prosperity. The same policy framed as "stewardship of God's creation" resonates with religious audiences for whom this is a binding value.

Effective communicators learn to read the values of their audiences and frame the policy in terms that align with those values. The same policy can carry multiple frames simultaneously, each aimed at a different audience segment. The skill is in matching the frame to the audience without becoming inconsistent across audiences in ways that look manipulative when surfaced.

The discipline gets harder when the audience holds values that conflict with the policy. A free-trade policy is harder to communicate to audiences that value local employment as a primary good than to audiences that value consumer welfare. The right move is usually not to dismiss the audience's values but to acknowledge them honestly while explaining the trade-offs the policy is making.

## Why this matters in policy

Policies with substantial public-acceptance challenges typically have value-conflict issues underlying them. Pure technical framing rarely fixes these. Acknowledging the value conflicts and arguing within them, rather than around them, tends to produce more durable policy support.
