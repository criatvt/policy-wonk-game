# Transparency

Transparency in policy communication is open access to information about how decisions are made, what evidence supports them, what alternatives were considered, and how the policy is performing in practice. Done well, transparency strengthens trust because the audience can verify rather than having to take the government's word for things. Done badly, transparency becomes a selective release of favourable information that emphasises rather than offsets the asymmetric-information problem.

The components of substantive transparency include: *process transparency* (how decisions were made, who was consulted, what the analytical work looked like); *decision transparency* (what was decided and on what reasoning); *performance transparency* (how the policy is performing against its stated objectives over time); and *failure transparency* (honest acknowledgement when things aren't working).

The hardest component is failure transparency. The political instinct is to hide bad news. The credibility consequence of hidden bad news, once discovered, is much worse than the credibility consequence of acknowledged failure. Governments that acknowledge failures and explain what they're doing about them generally retain more credibility than governments that hide them — even though the hidden failures may look smaller in the short run.

Transparency has limits. National security information, personal data, commercially sensitive material, and deliberative discussions all have legitimate reasons for protection. Substantive transparency operates within these limits while pushing the default toward openness rather than secrecy.

## Why this matters in policy

The investment in transparency infrastructure — public dashboards, performance reporting, accessible data, regular disclosure — is unglamorous but valuable. It builds credibility that the rest of policy communication can lean on. The absence of it tends to make every subsequent communication harder, because audiences have no independent way to verify the claims.
