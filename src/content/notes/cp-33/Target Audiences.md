# Target Audiences

Policy communication serves multiple audiences simultaneously, each with different needs. *Citizens* need to know how the policy affects their lives — what is changing, what they need to do, when, and why. *Media* need enough substance to write usable stories and enough context to write accurate ones. *Civil society* — NGOs, advocacy groups, professional associations — need detailed information to inform their constituencies and to engage in subsequent policy debates. *International partners* need clarity on commitments and expected behaviour. *Internal stakeholders* — bureaucrats implementing the policy, allied agencies, sub-national governments — need detailed operational understanding.

Each audience operates on different timescales and through different channels. Citizens get most of their information through media and direct government communication. Media work on news-cycle timescales. Civil society organisations process information through their own analytical frameworks. International partners typically need formal diplomatic communication. Internal stakeholders need administrative detail.

Effective communication tailors the message and the channel to each audience. The same policy might be explained in a press release, a public-information advertisement, a technical briefing for civil-society groups, a diplomatic communique to partners, and an operational circular for implementing agencies — each with different content, language, and emphasis. The same decision, several different communications.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy that uses one message for all audiences typically misses most of them. Citizens find it too technical; media find it too thin on substance; civil society finds it too generic; international partners find it imprecise. Audience-segmented communication is more work but reaches each audience with what they actually need.
