# Narrative Change

Changing a narrative that an audience already holds is much harder than introducing a narrative into a space where none was operative. Established narratives carry emotional weight, identity content, and accumulated reinforcement that any single counter-message struggles to dislodge. But change is possible, and the working patterns are reasonably well-understood.

Effective narrative change typically combines several elements. *Consistent counter-narratives*: a single message changes few minds, but the same message repeated across many channels over time gradually shifts the audience's frame. *New evidence*: data, examples, or events that don't fit the old narrative create cognitive friction that opens space for new framings. *Trusted messengers*: messages from speakers the audience already trusts get absorbed more readily than messages from outsiders. *Appeals to shared values*: connecting the new narrative to values the audience already holds reduces the resistance that comes from feeling asked to change identity.

Aggressive argument — direct contradiction of the old narrative — typically backfires. It puts the audience in defensive posture and strengthens the identity attachment to the original story. Patient narrative change works gradually and indirectly, building bridges between the audience's existing frame and the new framing rather than demanding immediate abandonment.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes that need to shift entrenched public understanding — anti-smoking campaigns, climate-policy support, vaccine confidence — typically work over decades, with patient narrative-change strategies and trusted messengers, rather than through aggressive corrections. The slower path is much more effective than the fast one.
