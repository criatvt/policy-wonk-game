# Credibility and Trust

Credibility in policy communication is a stock variable that accumulates slowly through consistent performance and depletes rapidly through inconsistency, broken commitments, or visible deception. A government department that has been honest about uncertainties and has delivered on its commitments gets the benefit of the doubt when it announces something new. A department that has overpromised, shifted reasoning, and underdelivered gets discounted in advance.

The components of credibility are several. *Consistency*: the same answer to the same question over time, even when politically inconvenient. *Honesty about uncertainty*: explicit acknowledgement of what is and isn't known, rather than performative certainty about contested issues. *Transparency*: visible reasoning, accessible data, and openness about how decisions were made. *Follow-through*: doing what was announced, or explaining honestly why the original plan was changed.

Each of these is easy to compromise in the short run for tactical advantage and expensive to rebuild once compromised. A single inconsistency can cost months of subsequent credibility. A single overpromised programme can taint a government's reputation for years. The asymmetry — slow accumulation, fast depletion — is what makes credibility management a discipline rather than a slogan.

## Truth and Trust

A subtle tension in policy communication is between *factual accuracy* and *narrative legitimacy*. Sometimes the most accurate thing a government can say contradicts a deeply held public belief, and the contradiction — even when fully correct — damages the government's standing more than it advances the cause of accuracy. The dilemma is real and is not resolvable by formula.

Take a hypothetical: a government has strong evidence that a widely held public belief about a particular health practice is incorrect. The straightforward move is to publish the evidence and explain that the belief was wrong. But the public has held this belief for a long time, has built behaviours around it, and treats it as part of community identity. The direct correction may produce a backlash that not only fails to shift the belief but erodes the government's broader credibility.

The alternative is more gradual. Acknowledge what the original belief got right (the underlying concern that motivated it). Introduce the new evidence in a frame that respects the audience's values. Use trusted messengers from within the community rather than outside experts. Build the bridge from the old understanding to the new rather than demanding immediate abandonment. The accuracy ends up no different; the path is different and produces less collateral damage.

This is not an argument for deception. It is an argument that *how* you communicate truth matters as much as *whether* you communicate it. The same accurate content delivered in different ways can produce qualitatively different audience responses.

### Why this matters in policy

Practitioners who treat communication as just "telling people the facts" run into the predictable backfire effects of identity-threatened audiences. Practitioners who treat communication as a craft — same facts, careful presentation, sensitivity to how audiences process information — produce better outcomes without sacrificing accuracy.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes that rely on public cooperation — tax compliance, public-health behaviours, regulatory compliance — depend on credibility in a load-bearing way. When credibility is high, the same programme costs less to enforce and produces better outcomes. When credibility is low, enforcement costs rise and outcomes deteriorate. The investment in credibility is therefore an investment in operational capacity, not a separate communications matter.
