# International Policy Communication

Policy communication directed at international audiences operates under a different set of constraints than domestic communication. Language differences require translation that preserves nuance. Cultural contexts differ in ways that affect how the same message is received. Trust in government varies across countries — some audiences default to scepticism, some to acceptance. Media landscapes differ, with different outlets carrying different weight in different countries. Historical relationships colour reception of any message — historical allies receive messages differently from historical adversaries.

The discipline for effective international communication is to *adapt rather than translate*. A direct translation of a domestic policy statement often lands awkwardly because the implicit context — historical references, idiomatic phrasing, assumed background knowledge — doesn't transfer. An adapted version that conveys the same substance in terms appropriate to the foreign audience lands more effectively.

International communication also faces *competing voices*. The government's own foreign-ministry communications are one channel; other countries' communications about the same issue are another; the international press is a third; local commentators in the target country form their own views. The government's voice has to operate in this multi-voice environment rather than commanding the conversation.

The technical infrastructure for international communication — multilingual staffing, translation services, regional media expertise, cultural-context advisors — is a substantial investment. Foreign ministries develop this expertise over decades. Other government departments, when communicating internationally, often need to partner with the foreign ministry to access the expertise efficiently.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes whose international communication is treated as a translation exercise — same message, different language — tend to produce flat results in foreign audiences. Programmes that invest in adaptation, regional expertise, and partnership with credible local intermediaries produce communications that actually land. The investment is part of the broader work of operating effectively in an interconnected world.
