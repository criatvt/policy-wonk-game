# Consultation Design

The structural feature that distinguishes genuine consultation from performative consultation is not the scale of input gathered but whether the input can materially alter the resulting decision. A consultation that produces 5,000 written submissions and a vibrant series of public meetings, but produces a final policy identical to the draft, is performative regardless of how many people were heard. A consultation that produces 100 submissions and visibly changes specific provisions of the draft is substantive.

The design choices that determine which kind a consultation will be are recognisable. *Timing*: consultations that happen before options are foreclosed allow real input; consultations that happen after the decision has effectively been made cannot. *Scope*: consultations that admit input on the policy's framing and substance allow real engagement; consultations that admit input only on the implementation details of a fixed approach allow only narrow engagement. *Feedback loops*: consultations that report back on how submissions affected the outcome build credibility for future processes; consultations that swallow input without visible response erode credibility. *Accountability*: consultations where decision-makers can be questioned on how they weighed input function differently from consultations that report only aggregate numbers.

Performative consultation imposes costs beyond the specific decision. It teaches stakeholders that participation doesn't matter, which reduces participation in future processes. It erodes the credibility of "consultation" as a category, even when subsequent consultations are designed substantively. It builds cynicism about democratic processes more broadly.

## Why this matters in policy

The investment in genuine consultation design — substantive timing, scope, feedback, and accountability — costs more than performative versions but pays off in both better immediate decisions and durable consultation infrastructure. The performative shortcut is locally cheaper but compounds in cost over time.
