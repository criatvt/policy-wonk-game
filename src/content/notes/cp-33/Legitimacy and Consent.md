# Legitimacy and Consent

Democratic legitimacy rests on the proposition that the people governed have, in some meaningful sense, consented to the government governing them. The consent operates through several channels — periodic elections, ongoing public debate, constitutional rules that constrain what the government may do — but underlying all of them is the assumption that the public is sufficiently informed about what government is doing to render meaningful consent.

Policy communication is therefore not just an instrumental matter of producing compliance with specific policies. It is part of the legitimacy infrastructure of democratic government. Citizens who can understand what government is doing, and why, are in a position to evaluate it and either consent or contest. Citizens who cannot understand are in a more passive position — they may consent by default, but their consent is thinner and less meaningful.

This is what makes opaque policy communication — jargon-laden, partial, evasive — politically corrosive over time. Each instance of opacity makes informed consent slightly harder. Cumulatively, opaque communication erodes the basis for democratic legitimacy and produces the cynicism that periodically reshapes political coalitions.

The communications discipline of explanatory clarity, honest acknowledgment of trade-offs, transparency about evidence, and openness about uncertainty is therefore also a discipline of democratic citizenship. Each piece of well-conducted policy communication is a small investment in the legitimacy of the broader system.

## Why this matters in policy

Practitioners who treat policy communication as just a tactical matter — getting through this announcement, deflecting this critique — produce communications that work in the short run but contribute to long-run legitimacy erosion. Practitioners who treat communication as part of the legitimacy infrastructure invest more in it and produce communications that hold up over years.
