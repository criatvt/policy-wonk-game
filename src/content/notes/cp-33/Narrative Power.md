# Narrative Power

A well-replicated finding in persuasion research is that personal stories and vivid examples are more persuasive than aggregate statistics, even when the statistics are far more representative of the population. The mechanism is cognitive: humans evolved to process individual experiences (in our ancestral past, all experience was individual) and have less native machinery for processing population-scale data. Statistics require translation; stories don't.

For policy communicators, this is a tool to be used carefully. A vivid story about a single beneficiary of a programme reaches audiences in a way the underlying statistics cannot. A single tragic case animates an issue more than the aggregate fatality data ever would. The persuasive power is real and is part of how policy debates actually get conducted.

The risk is that vivid stories also produce *selection effects*. The single case the storyteller chose to highlight may not be representative of the population. Audiences who absorb the story without the statistics may end up with badly distorted pictures of the underlying reality. The "welfare queen" archetype and the "hot coffee lawsuit" served their narrative purposes precisely by being unrepresentative.

The honest combination is narrative on top of data. The story provides the emotional engagement and the human stakes. The data provides the assurance that the story is representative of the population and not a cherry-picked exception. Each does what the other can't.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy communication that drops the data and runs only on stories ends up persuasive but easy to discredit. Policy communication that runs only on data is rigorous but politically inert. The combination is what holds up under scrutiny and reaches audiences emotionally — which is what good policy communication has to do at once.
