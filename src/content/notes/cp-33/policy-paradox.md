# Stone on Narratives as Political

Deborah Stone's deepest argument in *Policy Paradox* is that there is no neutral way to define a policy problem. Every narrative about a public issue selects which facts to make salient, designates protagonists and antagonists, attributes causation, and implies what counts as a legitimate solution. These choices are political because they distribute who benefits and who bears costs.

Consider poverty. A "personal-responsibility" narrative attributes poverty to individual choices, identifies poor families as responsible characters, locates causation in their behaviour, and implies solutions that ask them to change. A "structural" narrative attributes poverty to economic conditions, identifies poor families as victims of those conditions, locates causation in markets and policies, and implies solutions that reshape the economic structure. Both narratives describe the same observable poverty, but each implies very different policy responses, and each distributes political and economic power differently.

The political contest over which narrative becomes dominant in any given debate is therefore much of what policy fights actually are. Whoever sets the operative narrative has set most of the parameters of the resulting policy. Once "poverty is a result of bad choices" wins, redistribution and structural reform are off the table; once "poverty is a structural condition" wins, individual behaviour-change programmes look inadequate.

For policy communicators, the implication is that narratives are not just rhetorical packaging. They are part of the substantive political work. Treating them as decoration cedes the political contest to whoever does take them seriously.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy analysis that ignores narrative work tends to be politically toothless even when it is analytically sound. Policy analysis that engages narratives honestly — naming the contestant framings, examining their political implications, choosing a narrative deliberately — is much more likely to produce policy that actually gets adopted and sustained.
