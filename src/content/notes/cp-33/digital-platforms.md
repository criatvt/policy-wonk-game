# Why Social Media Amplifies Narrative

A common intuition is that the information abundance of the social-media era should reduce the importance of narrative — with more facts available, audiences could engage with the substance rather than the story. The empirical pattern has been the reverse. Social media has *amplified* the relative weight of narrative in shaping public understanding, in several mutually reinforcing ways.

*Algorithmic amplification* favours content with strong emotional engagement, which narratives produce far better than statistical analysis. The most engaging content is therefore typically narrative-led, while careful analytical content gets distributed less.

*Filter bubbles* organise audiences by ideological affinity, which means each audience is exposed predominantly to narratives that confirm its existing frames. The reinforcement is structural rather than incidental; the architecture of the platforms produces it.

*Virality* rewards the most compelling narrative, not the most accurate one. A simple, vivid, emotionally charged story spreads through algorithmic distribution faster than a complex, careful, accurate one. The platforms reward the spreader-prioritising content.

*Identity-protective cognition* operates more strongly when content is consumed in identity-affirming environments. Audiences encountering narratives that confirm their identity adopt them more readily; audiences encountering narratives that threaten their identity reject them more firmly. The platform architecture makes these encounters more sorted than they would be in a mixed-audience environment.

The cumulative effect is that policy debates increasingly take place through competing narratives rather than through shared facts. Stone's argument from the pre-social-media era — that narratives are politically constitutive — applies with more force, not less, in the contemporary environment.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy communicators who treat narrative as decoration find themselves outmanoeuvred by communicators who treat narrative as the substance. The pure-facts approach to public communication is more disadvantaged than ever; the discipline of choosing narratives deliberately and competing in the narrative space is correspondingly more important.
