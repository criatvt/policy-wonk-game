# Message Development

A clear policy message is short enough to be remembered, specific enough to be useful, and concrete enough to be acted on. The classic structure: what the policy does (in plain language); why it matters to this audience (linked to their concerns); what they need to do or know (concrete action or information). Compressed in this way, a policy that took 200 pages of regulation to draft can be communicated in two or three sentences that the affected audience can absorb and act on.

The discipline is hardest when the policy is genuinely complicated. The temptation is to convey all the complexity, which produces a message no one absorbs. The discipline is to identify the few things this particular audience needs to know first — the headline, the next step — and trust that they will seek more detail if they need it.

Good messages survive repetition. The same core message, repeated across multiple channels and over time, builds recognition and trust. Messages that change constantly with each communication cycle leave audiences confused about what the policy is supposed to be.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes whose messages are too complex or too inconsistent typically fail to reach the audiences they need. Programmes whose messages are clear and consistent get understood, even when the underlying policy is genuinely complicated.
