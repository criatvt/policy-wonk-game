# Narrative Framing

The most precise way to define framing, drawing on Deborah Stone, is as the *selection* of which aspects of an issue to make salient in the audience's mind. A frame doesn't change the underlying facts; it changes which facts are foregrounded and which are pushed to the background. The selection profoundly shapes how audiences interpret the issue and which solutions seem appropriate.

A road-safety issue can be framed as "drivers behaving recklessly" (foregrounds individual responsibility, suggests enforcement) or as "infrastructure failure" (foregrounds structural conditions, suggests engineering) or as "the cost of inadequate vehicle standards" (foregrounds manufacturing regulation). The accident statistics are the same; the frame determines which dimension comes to the front, which determines which interventions seem worth discussing.

Different audiences may receive the same issue through different operative frames depending on which news sources, social-media feeds, and community conversations they're connected to. The same underlying issue can produce qualitatively different policy debates in different communities depending on what frames they're operating inside.

For policy communicators, the frame is not a decoration to be added at the end; it is part of the policy's persuasive architecture. Choosing the frame is part of the work.

## Why this matters in policy

Public consultation processes that present pre-defined solutions are choosing among options selected by whoever framed the problem. Honest consultation surfaces the framing and asks the public to engage with multiple framings, not just the one the host institution prefers. Without that surfacing, the consultation is performing democracy more than practising it.
