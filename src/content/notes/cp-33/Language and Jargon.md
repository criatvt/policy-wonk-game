# Language and Jargon

Specialist jargon — the technical vocabulary developed within policy disciplines — has legitimate uses. It allows experts to communicate precisely with each other; it captures distinctions that everyday language obscures; it makes professional discourse efficient by avoiding repeated explanation. But the same jargon, used in communication with non-expert audiences, becomes exclusionary. Citizens are locked out of decisions that affect them because they can't follow the language in which the decisions are explained.

The discipline for public-facing policy communication is to use plain language by default and to reach for jargon only when the precision is necessary and an explanation can be provided. "Ad valorem tariffs of 12.5% with exemptions for goods on Schedule III" works for tax-policy specialists; "import tax of 12.5%, with some goods exempt" works for general audiences. The substantive content is the same; the accessibility is dramatically different.

The reverse trap is *oversimplification* — stripping the language down so much that the meaning is lost. Plain language is not the same as inaccurate language. "We are responding to inflation" is plain but uninformative; "we are raising interest rates to 7.5% to slow demand growth and bring inflation back to the 4% target" is plain and informative. The discipline is to find the accessible version that preserves the substantive content, not to lose the content for accessibility.

Translation between technical and public registers is part of the communicator's craft. Policymakers often need help from communications professionals to make the translation effective, because the experts who designed the policy are too deep in the technical register to easily produce the public register.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes whose public communications are jargon-heavy systematically under-reach the audiences they need. Programmes whose communications are plain-language but accurate reach broader audiences and produce more informed engagement. The investment in translation is worth more than it visibly costs.
