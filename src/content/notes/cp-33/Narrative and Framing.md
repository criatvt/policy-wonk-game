# Narrative and Framing

A policy narrative is a structured story that explains what the policy does and why it matters. The structure typically includes characters (real or representative people whose experiences illustrate the issue), a problem (what is going wrong), a cause (who or what is responsible), a solution (what the policy proposes), and a hopeful outcome (what life will look like if it works). The narrative gives the policy a shape that abstract description cannot.

Narratives outperform statistics in persuasion partly because they engage emotional as well as cognitive processing, partly because they are more memorable than raw numbers, and partly because they make the abstract concrete. A statistic that two million children are out of school is informative but distant; a narrative about one of those children — her aspirations, her circumstances, her family — makes the issue immediate.

The risk is that narratives select. Choosing one child's story to represent the two million inevitably emphasises some aspects and downplays others. A narrative built around a sympathetic protagonist may not capture the harder cases. Honest policy communication combines narratives with the underlying statistics so that the audience gets both the immediacy and the scale.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes that lean on narratives without supporting data tend to over-emphasise the dramatic at the cost of the typical. Programmes that lean on data without narratives tend to be technically respectable but politically inert. The combination — vivid narrative on top of solid statistical foundation — is what carries policy debates over time.
