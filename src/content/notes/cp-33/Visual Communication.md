# Visual Communication

Visual information — charts, graphs, maps, infographics, photographs, video — carries information differently from text. A well-designed chart can communicate a trend that would take paragraphs of text to describe. A map can show geographic patterns that text cannot easily convey. An infographic can summarise a complex programme in a single image. Visual content is also typically more memorable than text, which matters for ongoing policy communication where audiences need to retain what they've learned.

The discipline of visual communication has its own craft. Good charts pick the right type for the data — line charts for trends, bar charts for comparisons, maps for geographic distribution. They use consistent scales, clear labels, and minimal decoration. They make the headline finding visible without forcing the audience to study them. They avoid the well-known traps of misleading visualisations (truncated axes, distorting projections, cherry-picked comparisons) that look respectable but produce inaccurate impressions.

For policy communication, visual content is also a way to reach audiences who don't engage deeply with text. Social-media platforms favour visual content in their algorithms; mobile-first audiences scan rather than read; lower-literacy audiences engage with images more readily than with paragraphs. Each of these is a substantial portion of any modern audience.

The institutional investment in visual-communication capacity — designers, data-visualisation tools, established style guides, review processes — is generally undervalued in policy organisations relative to its impact. Programmes that invest tend to communicate more effectively across all the channels and audiences they need to reach.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes that produce strong visual communication artefacts — clear charts in their reports, well-designed infographics for their social media, accessible maps in their analyses — reach more audiences with more impact. Programmes that rely on text-only communication systematically under-reach the audiences that engage primarily through visual channels.
