# Narrative Structure: Characters and Plot

In Deborah Stone's analysis, every policy narrative carries structural elements that do specific persuasive work. *Characters* — protagonists and antagonists, victims and perpetrators — give the audience someone to root for and someone to blame. *Plot* — the sequence of events with causes and effects — explains how the situation arose and where it is heading. *Moral or lesson* — what the audience is supposed to take from the story — implies the solution and the action.

Each element carries political content. The choice of who is the protagonist (the struggling small farmer? the entrepreneurial start-up? the vulnerable consumer?) selects the audience's sympathies. The choice of who is the antagonist (corporate interests? incompetent regulators? foreign competitors?) directs the audience's anger. The plot's causal chain (this happened because of X, leading to Y) implies which interventions are responsive. The moral (the system needs reform, or the individuals need to behave better, or the regulators need more authority) maps onto specific policy choices.

This is why two analysts can look at the same situation and tell qualitatively different narrative stories about it. The same data — say, a rise in child malnutrition — supports a "parental responsibility" narrative (characters: parents and children, antagonist: bad parental choices), a "structural inequity" narrative (characters: families and children, antagonist: economic conditions and weak public services), and a "regulatory failure" narrative (characters: families and children, antagonist: ineffective government). Each narrative is internally coherent and each implies different policy.

## Why this matters in policy

Recognising the narrative structure of policy debates is part of reading them honestly. The contestant narratives implicate different solutions; the political fight over which narrative becomes dominant determines which solutions get pursued. Policy actors who notice this can choose their narratives deliberately rather than absorbing them from the surrounding discourse.
