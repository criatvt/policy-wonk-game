# Policy Instruments

Policy is the abstract goal — better health, cleaner air, lower poverty. Policy *instruments* are the concrete things government does to pursue the goal. The standard taxonomy distinguishes a handful of instrument families, each with characteristic strengths and characteristic limitations.

*Fiscal instruments* change the price of activities through taxes, subsidies, grants, levies, and transfers. They work well where actors are price-responsive and where the resulting incentive is large enough to drive behaviour. They produce continuous incentives for innovation and let actors find their own least-cost route to the desired outcome.

*Regulatory instruments* mandate or prohibit specific activities, technologies, or outcomes. They work well where compliance is verifiable and enforcement is credible. They produce more certain outcomes than fiscal instruments but at potentially higher cost and with weaker innovation incentives.

*Public provision* — the state directly supplies goods or services. It works where market provision is missing or inadequate (defence, basic education, public health, some infrastructure). It depends entirely on the state having the capacity to deliver, which the rest of this curriculum suggests is often the binding constraint.

*Information instruments* — labelling, disclosure, certification, ratings — change behaviour by changing what actors know. They work well where information was the missing piece and the actors who get it can act on it.

*Voluntary agreements and norms* — codes of conduct, voluntary standards, public-private compacts — operate through cooperation rather than coercion. They work well where interests align and where reputational stakes are real.

Most real policy regimes combine instruments rather than relying on any one. The mix matters at least as much as the choice.

## Why this matters in policy

Instrument choice is half of policy design. Analysts who reach for the same instrument out of habit (more regulation; more subsidies) miss opportunities to combine instruments that work better together than either does alone. The discipline of asking which instrument family is appropriate for which sub-problem, and how the families should interlock, is what produces well-designed policy.
