# Confronting Trade-Offs

The sixth step of Bardach's path — "confront the trade-offs" — is the place where the analysis stops being mechanical and becomes a discipline of honest comparison. No realistic policy alternative dominates the others on every criterion. The more efficient option is less equitable. The more equitable option is more expensive. The more flexible option is harder to implement consistently. The more powerful instrument creates more political resistance. Trade-offs are the normal condition, not the exception.

The analyst's job is to make the trade-offs explicit rather than to pretend they don't exist. A useful table format lays out alternatives along rows and criteria along columns, with each cell carrying a projected outcome (often as a range to mark uncertainty). The pattern that emerges shows which alternatives are good on which dimensions, and which conflicts the decision-maker will be choosing between. The recommendation then explicitly names the trade-off the analyst thinks is worth making and explains the value judgement involved.

This is uncomfortable for a reason: it requires the analyst to put the value judgement on the page. A recommendation that says "Option B trades 15% lower cost for 25% lower coverage of the poorest quintile — we recommend it because the budget constraint is binding and we judge the coverage loss tolerable given the alternatives" is much harder to write than "Option B is the best." But it is also much more useful, because it lets the decision-maker engage with the trade-off and either accept or override it.

The alternative — recommendations that hide the trade-offs in technical language — produces decisions that go wrong in predictable ways. Once the trade-off becomes visible later (because of unforeseen consequences, or political backlash, or a bad outcome) the buried disagreement surfaces, often acrimoniously.

## Why this matters in policy

The "no trade-off" recommendation is almost always a sign that the analysis is incomplete. Real policy choices typically involve real trade-offs. Forcing them onto the table makes the recommendation honest, makes the decision process accountable, and makes subsequent course-corrections easier when one of the unfavoured dimensions starts to bite.
