# Voluntary Agreements

Voluntary agreements occupy an interesting middle space in the instrument taxonomy. They involve regulated parties — usually firms or industries — committing publicly to achieve some outcome (emissions reductions, safety improvements, labour standards) without being legally required to do so. The state typically facilitates, monitors, and publicises performance, but does not formally compel.

The case for voluntary agreements is that they maximise the regulated parties' degree of freedom. Each can find its own way to meet the commitment, exploiting local cost advantages and knowledge that an external regulator could not access. They also reduce regulatory adversarial dynamics: the regulated parties are partners in the design and have a stake in being seen to comply. Where interests genuinely align — for instance, where industries want to forestall harder regulation by demonstrating self-regulation — voluntary agreements can produce surprisingly good outcomes.

The case against is the enforcement problem. Voluntary agreements depend on the goodwill, reputational concerns, and continuing alignment of interests of the parties. When any of these weakens, the agreement has limited bite. Free-rider problems are common: firms that comply pay costs that non-complying firms avoid, and over time the compliers either defect or fall behind commercially. Voluntary agreements rarely catch the worst-performing actors — exactly the ones policy most needs to reach.

The strongest evidence base on voluntary agreements suggests they work well in two cases: where the participants have aligned interests with the outcome (industry self-regulation under credible threat of formal regulation), and where they are paired with credible monitoring and the prospect of escalation to mandatory rules if voluntary action falls short.

## Why this matters in policy

Voluntary agreements as standalone instruments are usually weaker than their advocates claim. As parts of an instrument mix — paired with monitoring, public ratings, and the credible threat of mandatory regulation — they can extract more flexibility and cooperation than either pure regulation or pure laissez-faire. The design question is whether the credible threat is, in fact, credible.
