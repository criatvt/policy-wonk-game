# Smart Practices

A "smart practice" is Bardach's term for a policy approach that has worked in some context and might work in another. Sometimes these are called "best practices," which carries an unfortunate implication of universal applicability. Smart-practice analysis is more cautious: it asks not just what worked but *why* it worked, and whether the conditions that produced the success are present in the new context.

The temptation, particularly in policy work, is to copy the surface form of a successful programme — its name, structure, headline features — without copying the conditions that made it work. Brazil's Bolsa Família conditional cash transfer is widely studied and widely imitated. Some imitations have worked; others haven't. The difference is rarely about the conditional-cash-transfer mechanism itself; it's about whether the imitator had Brazil's banking infrastructure, civil registry, school-attendance monitoring system, and political consensus on poverty reduction. Without those preconditions, the cash transfers happen but don't produce the same outcomes.

The smart-practice discipline asks several questions. What was the problem the source programme was solving? What was the institutional context in which it operated? What were the key implementation features? Which features were essential to the outcome and which were incidental? What conditions in the source context made the essential features work? Are those conditions present in the new context, and if not, what would have to be built or adapted to compensate?

This process turns "we should adopt X" into "we should adopt X *if* conditions Y, Z are met, and otherwise should adapt the design to fit our conditions."

## Why this matters in policy

Bare imports of foreign programmes have a long record of underperforming, often expensively. Adaptive imports — taking the underlying insight and re-engineering the design for local conditions — have a better record. The smart-practice framing is what makes the adaptation work an explicit step rather than a discovery made after the imitation has already failed.
