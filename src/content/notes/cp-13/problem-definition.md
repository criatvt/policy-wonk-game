# Problem Definition

The first step of any policy analysis is to define what problem you are actually trying to solve. The step sounds procedural and harmless. It is in fact the most consequential and most politically loaded part of the work. The way the problem gets defined selects which facts are relevant, which actors are implicated, which solutions are even thinkable, and which trade-offs are visible. Different problem definitions of the same underlying situation produce different analyses and different recommendations using identical data.

Consider road congestion in a major city. Define the problem as "too many cars on the road" and the solutions are traffic restrictions, congestion charges, parking policy. Define it as "inadequate road capacity" and the solutions are flyovers, road-widening, new highways. Define it as "inadequate public transport" and the solutions are metro investment, bus rapid transit, last-mile connectivity. Define it as "poor land-use planning" and the solutions are mixed-use development, density reform, work-from-home policy. Same situation, four different problem definitions, four different policy worlds.

Deborah Stone's contribution is to make the political work of problem definition visible. Problems do not arrive pre-defined. They are framed by actors with stakes in particular definitions. Industry framing of pollution as "regulatory burden" produces different policy than environmental framing as "negative externality." Anti-poverty framing as "individual behaviour" produces different policy than framing as "structural inequality." The framing contest is upstream of the analysis, and the winner of that contest has often already won the policy.

Bardach offers practical discipline: define the problem in terms of the *condition* you are trying to change (more children learning to read; fewer people dying of road accidents) rather than in terms of *means* you are attached to (more schools; better policing). Definition-by-means smuggles the solution into the problem statement.

## Why this matters in policy

Analysts who accept the problem definition they are handed have already had much of their work done for them — by whoever did the handing. Analysts who interrogate the problem definition, name alternative framings, and articulate which framing they are using do harder work but produce more useful analysis. The political nature of definition does not go away; it is just made visible.
