# Policy Credibility

Policy credibility is the public's belief that the government can and will follow through on what it announces. It matters because much of policy works through expectations. A central bank's inflation target only anchors expectations if the public believes the bank will act to maintain it. A tax reform only changes behaviour if firms believe the new rules will stay in place. A subsidy reduction only triggers adjustment if beneficiaries believe the reduction is permanent. Without credibility, announcements become noise rather than signals, and the policy effect collapses.

The Indian demonetisation episode of November 2016 is the canonical Indian case for credibility damage. The policy was announced suddenly, with stated objectives that shifted repeatedly over the following months (anti-corruption, anti-black-money, digitisation, anti-counterfeiting, formalisation), with implementation chaos that contradicted the announced operational competence. The combination — inconsistent stated reasoning, projections that didn't bear out, visible execution failures — produced a sharp credibility cost that subsequent policies inherited.

The lesson is not specifically about demonetisation; it is about the structure of credibility. Credibility is a stock variable that builds slowly through consistent performance and depletes quickly through inconsistency, broken commitments, and visible execution failure. Once depleted, it is hard to rebuild because future announcements are discounted in advance. The next major reform faces a public that has learned to disbelieve until the implementation proves itself.

For policy actors, the upshot is that credibility is not just rhetoric to be managed; it is a strategic asset that should be spent carefully and rebuilt deliberately. Announcements that overpromise, projections that prove wrong, and execution failures that contradict competence all cost more than the immediate transaction.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy regimes with credibility — established central banks, mature regulatory agencies, long-running social-protection programmes — can rely on the public to respond to announcements as if they would be implemented. Policy regimes without credibility have to coerce or pay for behaviour that more credible regimes get for free. The work of building credibility is unglamorous and slow; the work of preserving it is part of the daily discipline of running a credible state.
