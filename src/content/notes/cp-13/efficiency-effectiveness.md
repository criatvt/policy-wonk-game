# Efficiency vs Effectiveness

Two words that sound similar but mean different things. *Efficiency* is the ratio of outputs to inputs: how much of the intended thing do you get per rupee, per hour, per unit of effort? *Effectiveness* is whether the intended thing is the *right* thing to be doing in the first place — whether the policy actually addresses the underlying problem rather than merely some proxy of it. A policy can be highly efficient at producing something that doesn't solve the real problem, and a policy can be inefficient at producing something that does.

The classic confusion is the metric-and-target trap. A school accountability system that measures student attendance with great efficiency may not be effective at improving learning. A police-staffing programme that produces high arrest rates efficiently may not be effective at reducing crime if the arrests are concentrated in the wrong places. A welfare delivery system that processes applications quickly and at low administrative cost may not be effective if it is excluding eligible beneficiaries through complicated paperwork.

The order of operations matters. *First* establish that the policy is effective — that it moves the underlying condition you care about. *Then* compare efficient ways of delivering it. Reversing the order produces highly optimised solutions to the wrong problem. This is what efficiency-without-effectiveness looks like in practice, and it is one of the more common failure modes of well-trained policy analysis.

Stone's deeper point is that "effectiveness" itself is contested. What the underlying problem is — and therefore what counts as effective intervention — depends on the problem definition, which is political. Efficient delivery of a poorly defined problem is not just a technical failure; it is the smooth execution of someone else's political framing.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes that optimise for measurable efficiency while leaving effectiveness unexamined tend to look good on dashboards and produce indifferent outcomes. Programmes that establish effectiveness first and then optimise for efficiency produce better outcomes but require harder upstream work — measuring what actually changes for citizens, not just what the bureaucracy processes.
