# Policy Analysis

This module is about the craft of policy analysis as a working discipline. The unifying texts are Eugene Bardach's *Eightfold Path* — a step-by-step procedure that takes an analyst from problem definition through to telling the story — and Deborah Stone's *Policy Paradox*, which adds the political and rhetorical dimensions that the more procedural literature tends to underplay.

The module's running tension is between policy analysis as *technique* (apply the framework, find the optimal answer) and policy analysis as *political activity* (problems are defined politically, evidence is selected politically, alternatives are framed politically). Both descriptions are partially true. The mature analyst learns to use the techniques while staying alert to the political moves embedded in them — particularly in problem definition, criteria selection, and the rhetorical packaging that determines whether the analysis gets used.

Indian case material grounds the abstract framework. Demonetisation provides a textbook case of policy credibility being destroyed by inconsistent reasoning and chaotic implementation. PAISA for municipalities illustrates how information infrastructure is upstream of accountability. The Delhi Metro debate shows how diffuse public benefits get under-counted against concentrated costs. *Tocsin for an Ancien Regime* makes the case that recurring policy failure usually signals institutional decay rather than bad design.

Topics in this module:

- **Stone on the goals of policy** — equity, efficiency, security, liberty, and the structural conflicts between them.
- **Bardach's Eightfold Path** — defining the problem, assembling evidence, constructing alternatives, selecting criteria, projecting outcomes, confronting trade-offs, deciding, and telling the story.
- **Problem definition and framing** — why how the problem is described pre-determines what counts as a solution.
- **Policy instruments and their trade-offs** — taxes vs standards, regulation vs incentives, the "top-down vs bottom-up" dilemma.
- **Credibility, implementation, and the limits of design** — demonetisation, the Delhi Metro debate, the lessons of repeated policy failure.
