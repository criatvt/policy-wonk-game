# Information Asymmetry and Policy Effectiveness

The textbook case for price-based policy instruments assumes that economic actors can readily learn the new prices, compute the implications for their behaviour, and respond efficiently. The empirical reality is that actors face substantial information costs at every step. Firms have to discover that the new tax applies to them, work out what activities are taxed, calculate which alternatives are cheaper, learn how to implement those alternatives, and absorb the disruption of switching. Each step takes time, expertise, and resources.

These information costs are why the theoretical effectiveness of price-based instruments often exceeds the actual effectiveness, especially in the short run. A carbon tax on paper produces 20% emissions reduction; the same tax in practice may produce 8% in the first three years and reach 18% only after seven years as actors gradually accumulate the information needed to respond fully.

The gap matters for policy design in several ways. It explains why pure taxes often disappoint advocates who underestimated transaction costs. It explains why supportive instruments — disclosure, R&D, training, demonstration — strengthen the response by reducing the information costs. It explains why pilot programmes that work in the controlled setting of a pilot don't always scale to national rollout where the information environment is less prepared. And it suggests that effectiveness should be evaluated over longer time horizons than the political cycle usually allows, because the response curve is slower than the textbook implies.

## Why this matters in policy

The information-cost lens is a useful corrective to over-optimism about price-based instruments and over-pessimism about regulatory ones. Both are sensitive to the information environment. Instruments that come bundled with information support — labels, public databases, advisory services, training — tend to perform closer to their theoretical effectiveness than instruments deployed into an information-poor environment.
