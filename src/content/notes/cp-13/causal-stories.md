# Causal Stories

A *causal story*, in Deborah Stone's terminology, is the narrative that explains why a problem exists, who or what is responsible for it, and therefore who should fix it. Different causal stories about the same situation produce very different policy worlds. Poverty caused by individual behaviour (laziness, poor decisions, addiction) calls for interventions on individuals: behavioural reform, conditional benefits, paternalistic safety nets. Poverty caused by structural conditions (labour-market failures, discrimination, broken institutions, inadequate education) calls for interventions on structures: minimum wages, anti-discrimination law, school investment, social-protection systems.

The contest over causal stories is much of what political argument actually is. Both sides agree the condition exists (people are poor). They disagree on the causal story, and the disagreement is consequential because the causal story selects the solution set. This is why political coalitions invest so heavily in causal-story work — picking the causes that point to their preferred solutions and contesting the causes that point to opponents' preferred solutions.

Stone identifies several recurring story types: *accidental* causes (no one is to blame, bad things happen), *intentional* causes (specific actors deliberately caused harm), *inadvertent* causes (well-meaning actions had bad side effects), and *mechanical* causes (impersonal systems produced the outcome). Each story type shifts responsibility differently and points to different responses. Skilled policy actors choose causal stories deliberately; less reflective actors absorb the stories on offer without noticing them.

For analysts, the upshot is that causal stories are not background to the analysis; they are part of what the analysis is shaping. Surface a single causal story uncritically and you have already committed to a particular policy direction. Mapping multiple causal stories explicitly, and noting which solutions each implies, is part of honest analysis.

## Why this matters in policy

The causal-story lens helps analysts notice when a policy debate is really an unresolved argument about causation. Programmes designed under one causal story can fail badly if the actual underlying causes are better described by another story. Honest analysis names the operative causal story, considers alternatives, and treats the choice between them as visible rather than hidden.
