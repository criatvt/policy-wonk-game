# Metaphor in Policy

Metaphors are not decorative; they are framing devices that quietly tell the audience what kind of thing the policy problem is and therefore what kind of response is appropriate. Calling drug abuse a "war" frames it as a struggle against an enemy, justifying mobilisation, intelligence operations, militarised enforcement, and emergency-style suspension of normal civil liberties. Calling it a "public-health crisis" frames it as a problem of treatment, prevention, and harm reduction, justifying medical infrastructure and education rather than incarceration. The same underlying phenomenon, two different metaphors, two very different policy worlds.

Stone catalogues several recurring metaphor families. *Disease and contagion* — implies urgent action, prevention, containment, the importance of moving fast before the problem spreads. Useful for mobilising attention; potentially distorting when the problem isn't really like disease (concepts like "viral" misinformation can lead to over-investment in epidemiological models for what is actually a sociological phenomenon). *War* — implies enemies, mobilisation, sacrifice, suspension of normal constraints. Useful for political mobilisation but tends to produce harsh policy responses. *Market* — implies voluntary exchange, individual responsibility, efficient allocation. Useful where market analogies actually hold; potentially distorting where they don't (markets in education, markets in healthcare).

The metaphor a policy debate operates inside shapes which considerations seem relevant and which seem off-topic. A policy actor who establishes the metaphor early has substantial influence over the debate that follows. The analyst's discipline is to notice which metaphor is operating, name it explicitly, and consider whether it is the right one for the situation.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy debates conducted inside the wrong metaphor tend to produce policies that fit the metaphor rather than the underlying problem. Drug-war framings tend to produce militarised responses regardless of what the underlying drug problem actually requires. Market framings of education tend to produce voucher systems regardless of whether market mechanisms actually fit the public-goods features of the sector. Examining the metaphor is part of examining the policy.
