# Horror Stories and Policy Narratives

A *horror story*, in Stone's vocabulary, is a single egregious or outlandish incident, selected and amplified to justify a policy change. The welfare-queen archetype in 1980s American debates over welfare reform. The hot-coffee lawsuit cited in arguments for tort reform. The single rotten teacher used to argue for sweeping changes to teacher tenure. Each is a real incident; each gets repeated until it stands in for the entire policy domain; and each carries arguments that the underlying statistical reality may not actually support.

The rhetorical work is obvious. A vivid extreme case bypasses the careful balancing that statistics would force. The audience feels the moral weight of the single instance and absorbs the conclusion that something must be done. The "something" is then the policy change the storyteller wanted in the first place — which the audience would not have accepted on the basis of the statistical aggregate alone.

The trick is recognisable across the political spectrum. Critics of regulation use horror stories of unreasonable enforcement to argue for deregulation. Critics of deregulation use horror stories of corporate abuse to argue for tighter rules. Critics of welfare use stories of abuse to push restriction; critics of restrictions use stories of harm to push expansion. None of these stories is invented; the political work is in their selection and the elision of the wider population they don't describe.

For analysts, the discipline is to treat horror stories as data points whose representativeness needs to be checked. If a single case is being used to justify a policy change, what does the underlying statistical distribution actually look like? Is the case at the extreme or near the median? Would the proposed policy actually address the conditions that produced the case, or would it merely respond rhetorically?

## Why this matters in policy

Policies justified on horror stories tend to over-fit the dramatic case at the cost of the broader population. Tort reforms aimed at hot-coffee lawsuits constrain ordinary plaintiff access. Welfare reforms aimed at the welfare-queen archetype harm the larger class of legitimate beneficiaries. Recognising the horror-story move and asking for the wider statistical picture is what protects policy from being captured by selected exemplars.
