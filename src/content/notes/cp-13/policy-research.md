# Policy Research Interviews

Interviews are part of evidence assembly that the literature often underplays. Published research and official data are visible and citable, and analysts naturally lean on them. But much of what makes a policy question tractable — the operational realities, the binding constraints, the political-economy dynamics, the failure modes of previous attempts — lives in the heads of practitioners who have done the work, in implementers who have run the previous programmes, and in affected populations who have been on the receiving end.

The discipline of policy research interviews is to access this tacit knowledge systematically. Identify practitioners with direct experience of the issue. Talk to people on different sides — implementers and affected parties; central government and field; advocates and sceptics. Prepare questions that draw on the gaps in the published evidence rather than retreading ground that the literature covers well. Take careful notes, attribute appropriately, and triangulate across interviews to identify where claims converge and where they diverge.

The interview material does not replace published evidence; it complements it. Where the published evidence is thin, interviews provide the operational context. Where the published evidence is rich, interviews reveal whether the field experience matches the published conclusions. Where claims in the published evidence look implausible from the field's perspective, the interview material flags it.

The standard quality issues are sample selection (talking to people who are easy to reach or share the analyst's prior), recall bias (people remembering their own successes more than their failures), and motivated misrepresentation (interviewees with stakes in the outcome). Acknowledging these in the analysis is what allows the interview material to be used credibly.

## Why this matters in policy

Analyses that rely only on published evidence often miss the operational realities that determine implementation. Analyses that draw on interviews routinely include detail that pure desk research could not produce. The combination — published evidence plus structured interview work — typically produces analyses that survive contact with the field better than either source alone.
