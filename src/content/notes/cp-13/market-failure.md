# Market Failure as a Reason for Intervention

The standard economic case for government intervention starts from the proposition that competitive markets, under certain conditions, deliver efficient outcomes. Where those conditions hold, the case for government action is weaker; where they fail, the case is stronger. The "market failure" literature identifies several recurring families of failure that justify some form of intervention.

*Externalities* — costs or benefits that fall on parties not involved in the transaction. A factory's pollution imposes costs on downstream residents who did not buy its products. A neighbour's well-maintained garden raises everyone's property values. Both are externalities; markets ignore them by default, and policy can correct by pricing them (taxes for negative externalities, subsidies for positive ones).

*Public goods* — goods that are non-excludable (you can't keep non-payers from benefiting) and non-rival (one person's use doesn't reduce another's). National defence, basic research, clean air, lighthouses. Markets undersupply these because no individual buyer captures enough of the benefit to justify paying. Public provision or public funding is the standard response.

*Information failures* — buyers and sellers don't share the same information, and the imbalance prevents efficient trade. Used cars (sellers know more about quality), insurance (buyers know more about their risk), credit (borrowers know more about their likelihood of repayment). Disclosure rules, certification, and regulation can correct these.

*Monopoly and market power* — when a single seller or small group controls supply, prices rise above competitive levels and quantities below. Anti-trust law, price regulation, and structural separation are the standard responses.

The market-failure framework is descriptively useful but worth applying with care. Government intervention has its own failure modes — political capture, bureaucratic inefficiency, unintended consequences — and a market failure is a *case* for considering intervention, not an *automatic justification* for it.

## Why this matters in policy

The market-failure lens disciplines policy analysis by asking *which* failure justifies the proposed intervention and whether the intervention actually addresses it. Interventions that don't map onto a clear market failure are often vulnerable to better critiques. Interventions that map cleanly are easier to design well and defend against critics.
