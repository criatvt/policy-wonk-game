# Diminishing Returns and Effectiveness

A pattern that recurs across policy domains: instruments produce large behavioural change at first and progressively less change with each additional unit of intervention. The first carbon tax raises prices enough to push out the easy-to-displace activities — driving short trips, leaving lights on, idling engines — and produces large per-unit reductions. The fifteenth carbon-tax hike, applied to actors who have already substituted away from the most carbon-intensive activities, raises revenue but produces much smaller per-unit reductions because the remaining activities are harder to displace.

This is the standard pattern for price-based instruments and applies similarly to most regulatory ones. Early enforcement of seat-belt laws produces large compliance gains; later enforcement struggles to reach the residual non-compliers. Early sanitation campaigns produce dramatic health gains in poorly served areas; later campaigns target the harder remaining gaps. Each instrument has an "effectiveness curve" that bends downward.

The policy implication is that no single instrument should be expected to do the whole job indefinitely. As an instrument approaches its effectiveness ceiling, the marginal effort of pushing it further yields less than the marginal effort of introducing complementary instruments that work on the residual problem from a different angle. Fuel taxes plus public-transport investment plus city-form reform together produce a different effectiveness curve than fuel taxes alone.

The pattern is also why measurement matters. Without evaluation that detects the bending of the curve, programmes can continue running well past the point of diminishing marginal returns, consuming political capital and fiscal resources for shrinking gains. Honest evaluation triggers the timely move to complementary instruments.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy design that anticipates the effectiveness curve avoids two failure modes: over-relying on a single instrument past its useful range, and abandoning instruments while they still have purchase. The right move is usually to build instrument mixes that cover the curve and to measure outcomes carefully enough to know when to shift weight from one to another.
