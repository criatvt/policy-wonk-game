# Supportive Actions

A category easy to underweight in the instrument taxonomy is the set of *supportive actions* that don't directly compel behaviour but lower the transaction costs and information barriers that prevent actors from responding to the main instruments. Government-funded R&D produces technologies that price-based instruments can then incentivise adoption of. Mandatory disclosure rules make markets work better for both buyers and sellers. Training programmes equip workers and firms to adopt better practices that regulation rewards. Demonstration projects show what's possible and reduce the perceived risk of adoption.

The supportive role is easy to overlook because each instrument on its own does little visible work. A research grant to a university lab does not, in itself, reduce emissions or improve learning. But the cumulative effect of supportive actions is often the difference between an instrument mix that works and one that doesn't. A carbon tax with no R&D investment behind it cannot incentivise adoption of technologies that don't exist; the same carbon tax paired with sustained R&D investment generates a moving target of viable alternatives.

The same logic applies in service delivery, regulation, and behavioural policy. Behavioural-change campaigns aimed at hand-washing or seat-belt use are weak alone; combined with infrastructure (soap, basin, accessible belts) and information (visible reminders, demonstrated norms) they become considerably stronger. Tax-compliance regimes depend on supportive information infrastructure — record-keeping requirements, audit trails, taxpayer education — at least as much as on enforcement.

## Why this matters in policy

The supportive layer is unglamorous to design and easy to cut from budgets because it doesn't produce visible outputs in the way main instruments do. But cutting it often undermines the instruments that do show up on dashboards. Well-designed policy mixes treat supportive actions as integral rather than as discretionary add-ons.
