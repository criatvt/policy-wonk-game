# Stone's Four Goals of Policy

Deborah Stone's framework identifies four primary goals of public policy: *equity* (fair distribution), *efficiency* (most output per input), *security* (protection from harm), and *liberty* (freedom from interference and freedom to act). The list is not exhaustive, but it captures most of what policy is in fact trying to do, and the four-way comparison turns out to be a useful organising device.

Each goal is internally complicated. Equity can mean equal shares, equal opportunities, equal needs met, equal procedural fairness, or some combination. Efficiency can be technical (maximum output per input) or allocative (resources flowing to highest-value uses). Security can be against external threats, internal disorder, economic loss, or environmental risk. Liberty can be negative (freedom from interference) or positive (capability to act). Even within a single goal, reasonable people disagree.

More importantly, the goals *conflict* — and they conflict structurally rather than incidentally. Maximum efficiency may concentrate resources where returns are highest, violating equity. Maximum security may require constraints on liberty. Maximum equity may dull the incentives that produce efficiency. Maximum liberty may leave people unprotected against external harms. These are not accidental conflicts that better policy design could remove; they reflect genuinely competing visions of what makes a society good.

This is why Stone titles her book *Policy Paradox*: policy choices typically force trade-offs between values that are each defensible on their own terms. There is no neutral place to stand. Different people, different parties, and different cultures resolve the trade-offs differently, and the resolution is what we usually call "politics."

## Why this matters in policy

The framework is useful because it gives analysts a vocabulary for naming the conflicts. Saying "this policy is efficient but inequitable" or "this policy improves security at the cost of liberty" is more honest and more politically usable than pretending the trade-offs don't exist. Recommendations gain credibility when they acknowledge what is being traded against what, and ask the decision-maker to make the call.
