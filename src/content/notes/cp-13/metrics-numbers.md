# Numbers as Political Acts

Statistical measurement looks like neutral description. Stone's argument — and a wide literature in critical metrics studies — is that it is in fact deeply political. The choice of what to count, how to define the categories, where to draw the boundary between "in" and "out" of the count, and how prominently to publish the resulting numbers all shape which problems become visible and addressable.

The unemployment rate is the standard example. Different countries define unemployment differently: who counts as "unemployed" depends on whether they are actively looking for work, how long they have been looking, whether they hold any part-time job, whether they have given up looking, and whether they are in school or in informal employment. Each definition produces a different headline number from the same underlying labour market. The number that ends up in the news shapes the political pressure on the government and the policy responses that follow.

The same is true across domains. The poverty line is a definitional choice with major consequences for which households receive benefits. Air-quality indices use thresholds that determine which days count as "polluted" enough to trigger emergency measures. School performance metrics determine which schools count as "failing" and qualify for intervention. The categories matter, the cut-offs matter, and the political work that goes into setting them is largely invisible to the public who interprets the resulting numbers.

For analysts, the upshot is that "what does the data say" is itself a political question. The data that exist were produced through choices about measurement; the data that don't exist were not produced because no one with political weight pushed for them to be. Reading the numbers with this in mind — asking who chose to count this, with what definition, and what they didn't count — is part of using statistics honestly.

## Why this matters in policy

What gets measured gets managed; what doesn't get measured tends to drift. The choice to start measuring a particular thing — learning outcomes rather than only attendance, district-level air quality rather than only national averages, gender-disaggregated programme outcomes rather than only headline totals — is itself a substantial policy act. The measurement infrastructure determines what subsequent policy can see.
