# Bardach's Eightfold Path

Eugene Bardach's *Eightfold Path* lays out eight steps that good policy analysis touches in some order:

1. **Define the problem.** State what the problem is, in language that distinguishes it from related but different problems and that names whom it affects.
2. **Assemble the evidence.** Gather relevant data, prior studies, comparable cases, and expert opinion. This usually takes most of the time and is often where analysis goes wrong.
3. **Construct the alternatives.** Lay out a set of candidate policy options. The set should include the status quo and at least one option different enough to make the comparison interesting.
4. **Select the criteria.** Decide what dimensions of outcomes will be used to evaluate the alternatives — cost, effectiveness, equity, political feasibility, and so on.
5. **Project the outcomes.** For each alternative, estimate what is likely to happen on each criterion. This is where evidence and judgement meet, and where honest analysts mark their uncertainty.
6. **Confront the trade-offs.** No alternative will dominate on every criterion. Identify where one alternative is better on one criterion but worse on another, and make the trade-offs explicit.
7. **Decide.** Make a recommendation. The work up to this point should support, but does not predetermine, the recommendation.
8. **Tell your story.** Communicate the analysis to the decision-maker in a form they can use. Analysis that no one reads, or that no one can act on, has produced nothing.

Bardach is explicit that the eight steps are *iterative*, not strictly sequential. Defining the problem clearly often requires partial work on alternatives and criteria; projecting outcomes often sends you back for more evidence; confronting trade-offs may reveal that the alternatives need to be reconstructed. The path is a discipline, not a recipe.

## Why this matters in policy

The value of the framework is partly that it forces analysts to do the unglamorous steps. Defining the problem rigorously, articulating criteria explicitly, and marking uncertainty in projections are easy to skip and consequential when skipped. Analysts who follow the path produce work that is harder to dismiss; analysts who don't tend to produce work that is easy to ignore.
