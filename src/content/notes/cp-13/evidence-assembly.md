# Assembling Evidence

The second step of Bardach's path — assembling evidence — is where the most time goes and where most analyses succeed or fail. The goal is to gather enough relevant, credible information to support projections about what each alternative will produce. The standard sources include published research, government data, organisational reports, comparable cases from other jurisdictions, interviews with practitioners and experts, and, where feasible, direct measurement.

The discipline is to gather selectively rather than exhaustively. Evidence-gathering is open-ended; in principle one could keep looking forever. The analyst has to make judgement calls about which evidence is most likely to be informative for the projections that matter, gather that evidence efficiently, and stop when the marginal piece of evidence is unlikely to change the recommendation. This requires some sense, early in the work, of which uncertainties are most important.

Quality matters more than quantity. A single rigorous evaluation of an analogous programme is worth more than a dozen anecdotes. A clean comparison from a similar setting is worth more than a stretched comparison from a very different setting. Engaging with the methodology and limitations of the evidence sources — rather than just quoting their conclusions — is what separates careful evidence assembly from quote-mining.

Two recurring failure modes are worth flagging. First, evidence is often *selected* to support a conclusion the analyst has already reached; the discipline of looking deliberately for evidence against the favoured option is what keeps analysis honest. Second, evidence is often *generalised* beyond what the underlying studies support; the analyst needs to be explicit about how directly applicable each piece of evidence is to the situation under analysis.

## Why this matters in policy

Evidence assembly is the input to projection, and projection is the input to recommendation. Sloppy evidence assembly produces shaky projections, which produce vulnerable recommendations. The careful, time-consuming work at this step is what gives the eventual recommendation room to be confidently defended.
