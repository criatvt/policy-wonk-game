# Constructing Policy Alternatives

The third step of Bardach's path — constructing alternatives — is where many analyses go silently wrong. The discipline is to lay out a set of candidate options that the analysis will then compare. The set should include the status quo (always — because doing nothing is an alternative), at least one option different enough from the status quo to make the comparison informative, and a manageable number in total (typically three to five; more than that produces an unreadable table).

The trap is that the initial selection of alternatives defines the solution space the analysis can search within. A thoughtful, rigorous comparison of three options will produce a thoughtful, rigorous recommendation — but if those three options were a poor sample of the actually-available possibilities, the recommendation is the best of a poorly chosen set. Bardach calls this the problem of *optimising the wrong measures*: doing excellent work on a set that excluded the better answer.

Avoiding the trap requires deliberate effort early in the analysis. Look at how other jurisdictions have addressed similar problems. Talk to practitioners who have direct experience with the issue. Use the disciplinary literature to identify the standard families of approach. Construct synthetic alternatives that combine elements from different sources. Then prune the candidate list down to the manageable set for detailed comparison, while keeping a record of what was excluded and why.

The exercise is uncomfortable because it surfaces options the analyst's client may not want considered. Senior decision-makers often have a preferred direction and would prefer the analysis to compare three variants of that direction rather than testing it against genuinely different alternatives. Good analysts hold their ground on the breadth of alternatives, because the integrity of the comparison depends on it.

## Why this matters in policy

Analyses that compare narrow variants of a single approach tend to look rigorous and to confirm the approach. Analyses that compare genuinely different alternatives are harder to write and harder to please everyone with, but they produce recommendations that are robust to the question "what else could you have done?"
