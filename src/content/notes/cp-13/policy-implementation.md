# Implementation Capacity and Policy Design

A perennial finding in the implementation literature is that even well-designed policies fail when the implementing organisation lacks the capacity to deliver. This is not the same as saying that bad implementation is the implementer's fault; it is more often the designer's fault for failing to take capacity as a binding constraint of the design.

Capacity constraints come in several forms. *Fiscal capacity* — the implementing organisation may not have the budget for the staffing, equipment, or infrastructure the design assumes. *Personnel capacity* — the right kind of people, in the right numbers, with the right training, may not be available. *Information capacity* — the data flows, monitoring systems, and feedback mechanisms the design assumes may not exist. *Organisational capacity* — the coordination across teams, departments, or levels of government that the design requires may not be feasible given the existing structures. *Political capacity* — the implementing organisation may not have the political backing to override the resistance the design will encounter.

Good policy design treats capacity as a constraint to be measured rather than ignored. The design questions become: what capacity does this design require? Does the implementing organisation actually have it? If not, can the design be modified to fit the available capacity, or does the design require investing in capacity-building before deployment? Designs that don't ask these questions tend to be elegant on paper and disappointing in the field.

This is part of why Bardach later argued that policy solutions should be *assembled* from existing resources and capacities rather than *designed* from scratch. The assembly framing forces explicit engagement with what is actually available; the design framing tends to elide it.

## Why this matters in policy

Designers who treat implementation as someone else's problem produce policy that doesn't survive contact with the field. Designers who treat implementation capacity as a constraint of equal status to fiscal cost produce policy that has a chance of arriving at the citizen in something like the intended form.
