# Government Intervention

A useful exercise *In Service of the Republic* runs is to take the most trivial-looking policy intervention you can think of and work through what implementing it would actually require. The example the book uses is standardising mobile phone chargers — replacing the many incompatible plugs of the early-2000s phone market with a single common standard. On the face of it, this is an obvious win. Consumers stop buying a new charger for every new phone; e-waste falls; cross-border travel gets easier. Nobody is harmed.

Once you start actually doing it, the simple intervention generates a thicket of complications.

Who decides which standard? The choice has commercial implications. The company whose standard is selected gets a windfall; the others lose. A formal selection process invites lobbying and capture. Selecting purely on technical merit is harder than it sounds because the alternatives often have different strengths.

How is it enforced? You can pass a law saying "all phones sold here must use this charger." Now you need to inspect imports. You need to define what counts as a "phone." You need to handle edge cases — accessories, prototypes, products that look like phones but aren't. Each definition gets gamed. Each gap in enforcement creates a grey market.

What about welfare costs? Forcing a standard locks out innovation in the standardised dimension. If a better connector technology comes along, the standard has to be revised — through the same political process that created it the first time, with the same chance of getting captured by incumbents. The dynamic cost of standardisation is invisible at the moment of decision but real over time.

What about the people you displace? Companies that built their business around the old standards lose. Employees of those companies lose. Customers who already own products under the old standards face stranded costs. None of these losses appear in the headline benefit-cost calculation, but they are real and politically loud.

What about substitution and avoidance? Some users won't replace their old chargers; some grey-market suppliers will keep selling them; some consumers will adapt with adapters that recreate the original problem. The intervention's effect is always less than the policy's design assumes.

The lesson the authors draw from the charger case is general. Even a trivial-seeming intervention generates real problems of decision, enforcement, welfare, and second-order effect. Larger interventions — restructuring electricity markets, reforming labour law, rolling out a digital identity system — generate the same problems at far greater scale. None of this means intervention is wrong. It means the case for any specific intervention has to be made against a serious accounting of these costs, not assumed from the policy's stated intent.

The book's broader argument is that this difficulty is a feature, not a bug. The state is the only organisation legitimately empowered to use force, which gives it unique capacity to solve some problems no other organisation can solve. That same capacity makes it dangerous when applied to the wrong problems, or applied poorly. Restraint is not the opposite of state effectiveness; it is part of it.

A few practical heuristics follow.

First, prefer narrow interventions to broad ones. The narrower the intervention, the more contained its unintended effects.

Second, build in reversibility. Policies that are easy to undo when they don't work are less costly to try than policies that lock in.

Third, design for monitoring. If you can't measure whether the policy is working, you can't adjust it. Building the monitoring mechanism into the intervention from the start is part of the cost.

Fourth, take the second-order effects seriously. Most interventions have effects beyond their direct target. Whether those effects are net positive depends on knowing they exist, which requires looking for them rather than assuming them away.

## Why this matters in policy

The "even the chargers are hard" framing is a useful corrective to the assumption that government can simply pick the right outcome and execute. It also pushes against the opposite assumption, that government should do nothing. The realistic position is that intervention is sometimes necessary, often costly, and almost always more complicated than it looks. Humility about the difficulty is part of the discipline of getting interventions right when they are worth doing.
