# Balance of Power and Power Transition

Within realist international relations theory, two prominent accounts try to explain when great-power war becomes more likely. They are not direct contradictions of each other, but they predict different conditions as dangerous, and the disagreement is instructive about how structural theories handle change.

## Balance of power

The older and more familiar theory holds that states act to prevent any single state from achieving hegemony — overwhelming dominance — over the rest of the system. The reasoning is straightforward. In an anarchic system, a dominant state is a permanent threat to every other state; it can extract concessions, dictate terms, and reshape the system to serve its interests, and no one can stop it. So states have a structural incentive to balance against any rising power that threatens to achieve dominance, either by building up their own capabilities (internal balancing) or by forming alliances against it (external balancing).

The empirical pattern balance-of-power theorists point to is the long European record from roughly 1648 to 1945: every time one continental power looked close to dominance — Habsburg Spain, Louis XIV's France, Napoleon's France, Wilhelmine and Nazi Germany — the others coalesced against it, often through war. The theory predicts war as the mechanism that resists hegemony.

In its more sophisticated forms, balance-of-power theory makes finer claims about which configurations are more stable. Bipolar systems (two great powers, as in the Cold War) are sometimes argued to be more stable than multipolar ones because alliance commitments are clearer and miscalculation is harder. Other versions argue the opposite. The core claim — that hegemonic threats produce balancing — survives across variants.

## Power transition

The newer competing theory, most associated with A.F.K. Organski, argues the opposite about when war is most likely. War is least likely, on this account, when there is a clear hegemon and the gap between it and the next-largest power is wide. War is most likely during the transition — the period when a rising challenger approaches parity with the dominant state.

The reasoning is also structural but different. A dominant state has built the international order around its preferences. A rising challenger, as it grows, becomes increasingly dissatisfied with an order it didn't shape and increasingly capable of changing it. The hegemon, in turn, sees the challenger's rise as a threat to the order it benefits from. The dangerous moment is when the gap is small enough that the challenger believes it can change the order by force, but the hegemon still has enough strength to resist. War, in this view, is most likely at the inflection point.

The empirical pattern power-transition theorists point to is roughly the same European record, read differently: World Wars I and II are read as Germany's rising power challenging the existing British-led order. The Athens-Sparta war in Thucydides — Athens rising, Sparta dominant — is read as the prototype. The same data can be read either way; the disagreement is about which feature of the configuration is the trigger.

## What the two theories share and where they differ

Both theories agree that material power matters, that great powers are the key actors, and that the structure of the system shapes outcomes. Both treat war as the mechanism by which structural disputes get resolved when other instruments fail.

They differ on when war is most likely:

- Balance of power: war is most likely when one state is approaching hegemony — peace requires roughly balanced distributions.
- Power transition: war is most likely when a rising challenger approaches parity with a clear hegemon — peace requires either clear hegemony or established multipolar balance.

The practical implication is that the two theories give different advice. Balance-of-power thinking would have you watch for and resist any single power that looks like it could dominate. Power-transition thinking would have you watch the relative growth rates of major powers and worry most when those rates are converging from a position of inequality.

## Modern complications

Several features of the contemporary system complicate both theories. Nuclear weapons add a powerful intervening variable — neither theory's nineteenth-century version anticipated that great-power war could mean global civilisational damage, which alters the cost-benefit calculation on both sides. Economic interdependence between major powers, while not making war impossible, raises the costs in ways the older theories did not foreground. Cyber, information, and grey-zone competition allow rising states to challenge the existing order well below the threshold that triggers conventional power-transition wars, which may delay or distort the historical pattern.

Both theories are best read as identifying real structural dynamics rather than as offering complete predictions. The balance-of-power instinct to resist would-be hegemons and the power-transition concern about dangerous moments of catching up are both worth taking seriously, even if neither theory closes the case alone.

## Why this matters in policy

For policy thinkers in middle and rising powers — India sits in interesting positions here — both theories have practical implications. The balance-of-power lens suggests attending to which great powers look most dominant and adjusting alignments accordingly. The power-transition lens suggests attending to which great powers are growing fastest, because the convergences are where the dangerous moments will come. Neither lens by itself is sufficient; the working analyst keeps both in view and notices which one fits the situation better at any given time.
