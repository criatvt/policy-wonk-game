# Free Markets and Self-Organisation

A recurring claim in modern political economy is that decentralised, self-organising systems often outperform centrally planned ones, especially in environments that are complex and changing. The argument is not that markets are always better than governments, or that planning never works. It is more specific: in problems where the relevant information is scattered across many actors, where preferences and conditions change quickly, and where solutions can't be specified in advance, the system that lets each actor act on their local knowledge will tend to outperform the one that requires central decisions.

*In Service of the Republic* uses a concrete example to make the case: the contest between TCP/IP and X.25 as networking standards in the 1980s and early 1990s.

X.25 was the official, government-backed standard. It was developed through formal international processes by national telecommunications authorities, the union of state-owned telephone companies known as the CCITT (now ITU-T), and large industrial vendors. It had institutional weight, generous funding, official endorsement, and the support of the most powerful players in global telecoms. By every conventional metric, it was the technology that should have won.

TCP/IP was scrappier. It emerged from a research community — academics, graduate students, defence researchers, early commercial enthusiasts — working in a much looser style. Nobody had to approve a TCP/IP change before it shipped. Different groups tried different things; what worked got adopted by others; what didn't, didn't. There was no global plan, no central authority, no mandatory standard. By the metrics that telecom executives cared about, it should have been an also-ran.

It wasn't. Within a decade, TCP/IP had absorbed almost the entire networked world and X.25 was a footnote. The Internet that ran on it became the largest engineering achievement of the late twentieth century, and the protocol that didn't have the institutional backing won decisively against the one that did.

The case is not unique. Wikipedia outscaled Encarta and Britannica. Linux became the dominant operating system on the world's servers and most mobile phones, despite having no corporate parent. Open-source software ecosystems have produced critical infrastructure with no central planner. In each case, the explanation is roughly the same: dispersed contributors, each acting on local knowledge and incentives, producing a coordinated outcome that nobody designed.

Why does this work? Two reasons that systems theory makes available.

The first is the knowledge argument. Friedrich Hayek's case against central planning, made for market economies, applies more broadly: the information needed to coordinate well is dispersed across many actors and often tacit. A self-organising system lets that information get used in place; a planned system has to gather it first, which is costly and slow.

The second is the experimentation argument. A decentralised system runs many small experiments in parallel. Most fail; the successful ones get copied; the failures are absorbed by the people who tried them. The system learns much faster than a central planner could, because it tries more things and pays for fewer of the failures.

The argument has real limits, which the authors note. Self-organising systems can produce outcomes that are individually rational but collectively bad — externalities, free-rider problems, races to the bottom. They are weak at producing public goods. They can lock in early winners through network effects, sometimes the wrong ones. And they don't automatically distribute the gains equitably. The case for self-organisation is not that it solves every problem. It is that when the problem matches the conditions — dispersed knowledge, changing environment, need for experimentation — it tends to outperform planning, and policy that fails to recognise this is leaving large gains on the table.

## Why this matters in policy

The lesson is not "let markets handle everything." It is closer to "before designing a planned solution, ask whether a self-organising one could do better with less effort." Government policy that creates the conditions for self-organisation — clear property rights, low entry barriers, open standards, transparent information — often delivers more than the same government attempting to manage the same problem directly. The trick is recognising which problems are which. Some genuinely need central coordination; many that are treated as if they do, don't.
