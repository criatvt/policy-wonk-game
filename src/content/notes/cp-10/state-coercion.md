# What the State Is For

The most influential modern definition of the state comes from the German sociologist Max Weber, writing in the early twentieth century. A state, on Weber's account, is the entity that holds a monopoly on the legitimate use of physical force within a given territory. The definition does not say the state should do nothing else, and obviously states do. It says that this monopoly on legitimate violence is the distinguishing feature — the thing that makes a state a state and not just another organisation in society.

Vijay Kelkar and Ajay Shah's *In Service of the Republic* takes this Weberian definition seriously and follows it where it leads. At the heart of the state, they argue, is violence. Not metaphorical violence — real physical force. The state acquires a monopoly on the legitimate use of it, and uses the threat of it to obtain obedience. Almost every interaction between an individual and the state is, in the last analysis, backed by the possibility of forced compliance: pay your taxes or face seizure of assets; obey the court order or face imprisonment; submit to the regulation or face penalty. The velvet glove is real, but there is an iron fist inside it.

This is uncomfortable framing because most of the time, in a reasonably functioning state, the violence stays in the background. Citizens experience the state through schools, hospitals, post offices, and ration shops — not through the police. The visible state is service-providing. The structural state, the one that makes everything else possible, is coercive.

A few consequences follow from taking this seriously.

The first is that any state powerful enough to do the visible good things is also powerful enough to do harm. The same coercive capacity that lets it collect taxes lets it crush dissent. The same monopoly on violence that suppresses private crime can be used to enable state crime. This is the fundamental governance problem: how do you give an organisation enough power to do its job without that power being misused?

The second is that depending on the personal virtue of officials is naïve. The state is, in the authors' phrase, a "soulless machine." It doesn't have a conscience. The individuals inside it might, and many do, but the machinery as a whole responds to incentives and constraints, not to virtue. If accountability depends on the kindness of the police officer, the integrity of the auditor, and the prudence of the minister, you have built a state that works only when you are lucky. Accountability has to be engineered into the institutional design — through checks and balances, free press, independent judiciary, transparent data, regular elections — not assumed from the character of the people inside.

The third is that the costs of state action are not just monetary. Every regulation is enforced, ultimately, with the threat of force. Every tax raised has a coercive substrate. This doesn't mean regulation and tax are wrong — most are clearly necessary — but it does mean each carries a real cost beyond the rupees it raises or the resources it directs. Asking whether a given exercise of state power is worth that cost is part of the analysis, not separate from it.

## Why this matters in policy

The Weberian framing is a useful corrective to two opposite errors. One is sentimentalising the state, treating it as a benign service-provider whose only limitation is funding. The other is demonising it, treating every exercise of its power as inherently illegitimate. Neither captures the actual structure. The state is the organisation that has the legitimate authority to use force, which makes it uniquely capable of solving some problems no other organisation can solve, and uniquely dangerous when that authority is misused. Good governance is the discipline of preserving the capacity while constraining the dangers, and there is no permanent solution — only institutional design that is more or less successful.
