# Policy Design: Science and Art

A theme that runs through *In Service of the Republic* is the distinction between two kinds of policy work. Some questions in public policy have answers that can be supported by analysis, evidence, and accumulated empirical experience. Others can't — not because nobody has looked hard enough, but because the question itself involves judgments about value, uncertainty, and politics that no amount of analysis will resolve. The authors call the first kind science and the second art, and they argue that distinguishing between them is essential to doing either well.

The science of policy is the part where standard analytical methods help. Whether a tax change is likely to raise revenue, by how much, and at what cost in compliance burden — these are questions with credible quantitative answers. Whether a vaccination programme reduces childhood mortality — likewise. Whether a specific subsidy reaches the intended beneficiaries — likewise, given good data. In these areas, careful analysis can narrow the range of plausible outcomes, identify trade-offs, and inform choice. The honest practitioner takes the analysis seriously and updates beliefs accordingly.

The art of policy is what's left after the analysis runs out. Most consequential policy questions contain components the science can't decide. How much weight should we give to current versus future welfare? Whose interests count, and how heavily? What level of risk is acceptable when the downside is irreversible? How much should we lean toward state action versus market provision in a domain where reasonable people disagree? These are not failures of analysis to find the answer; they are questions that don't have an analytically determinable answer at all.

The mistake the book warns against is collapsing one kind into the other.

One direction of mistake is to treat everything as art — to say that because some elements of policy require judgment, all elements do, and therefore evidence-based analysis is just one input among many to be weighed against political instinct. This produces policy disconnected from evidence, where strong priors override empirical reality.

The other direction is to treat everything as science — to insist that good analysis will eventually resolve all the disagreements, and that anyone holding out for judgment is just resisting the data. This produces policy that overclaims certainty, ignores legitimate value disagreements, and tends to be brittle when the analysis turns out to have been wrong.

The mature position is to be honest about where one mode stops and the other starts. For a given policy question, the work is to identify the components that have evidence-based answers, do the analysis honestly, and then turn deliberately to the judgment-based components without pretending they have analytical answers. Both modes deserve their full weight; neither should be smuggled in under the cover of the other.

A few practical consequences.

Analytical work usually narrows the range of viable options but rarely picks among them. The narrowing is a real contribution; the picking is where judgment, politics, and values legitimately enter. A policymaker who acts as though analysis hands them a single answer is either getting lucky or not asking the right questions.

Judgment under uncertainty isn't arbitrary. It can be made explicit — by stating the underlying assumptions, the values being weighed, and the risks being accepted — even when it can't be reduced to numbers. Explicit judgment is auditable and updatable; implicit judgment hides where the decision was made and who made it.

The boundary between science and art shifts over time. As evidence accumulates, questions that used to be in the art column move to the science column. New questions push in the other direction. Treating the boundary as fixed misses where the field actually is at any given moment.

## Why this matters in policy

Public policy works at the boundary between technique and value, and getting either side wrong tends to produce bad policy. Practitioners who are good at the science can be naïve about the art; practitioners who are good at the art can dismiss the science. The best work tends to combine both, with conscious awareness of which mode is in use at each point. The discipline is uncomfortable because it forces one to admit, sometimes publicly, that a key decision didn't come from the data but from a judgment one is making. That admission is the price of doing the work honestly.
