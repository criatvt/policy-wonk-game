# Levels of Analysis and the Causes of War

Asking "what causes war" is like asking "what causes accidents." The question is real but the answer depends on what you mean. Are you asking about the underlying conditions that make accidents possible? About the kinds of road or vehicle design that make them more likely? About the rules and norms that govern driving? Or about the specific decisions of the people involved in any given crash? Each of these is a legitimate question, but they ask about different things and call for different kinds of explanation.

The same is true of war. Half the confusion in the international relations literature comes from theorists arguing past each other because they are working at different levels of analysis. The "levels of analysis" framework, formalised by Kenneth Waltz in *Man, the State, and War* (1959), provides a vocabulary for keeping them straight.

## The four levels

- **Individual** — the leader making the decision, with their psychology, beliefs, biases, and information. Why did Saddam Hussein invade Kuwait in August 1990? Some part of the answer involves Saddam himself: what he thought he knew, what risks he was willing to run, what advice he was listening to.
- **State** — the domestic political system, the regime type, the bureaucratic actors, the public opinion. Why are democracies historically rare in starting wars with other democracies? Some part of the answer involves features of democracies as states: accountability mechanisms, the cost of mobilising public support, the institutional checks on executive power.
- **Society** — the broader social and cultural conditions: nationalism, ideology, ethnic divisions, economic conditions. Why was Europe so war-prone in the late 19th and early 20th centuries? Some part of the answer involves the rise of mass nationalism and the way it shaped politics in every major state.
- **System** — the structure of the international order: anarchy, distribution of power, alliance patterns, prevailing technology. Why is war a permanent possibility in international politics at all? Anarchy is the standard structural answer.

Most consequential wars have causes at all four levels operating simultaneously. World War I had system-level (multipolar balance breaking down), society-level (nationalism, militarism), state-level (alliance commitments, mobilisation timetables), and individual-level (specific decisions by emperors and generals) causes. The theorist who insists only one level matters is usually missing what the others contribute.

## Permissive causes versus explanations of variation

A second useful distinction. Some causes of war are permissive — they explain why war is possible at all — without explaining why it happens at one time and not another. Anarchy is the classic permissive cause: it makes war a permanent feature of the system but doesn't say when. Human aggressiveness, in classical realist accounts, is similarly permissive. These causes explain the recurrence of war over centuries but cannot say when the next one happens.

Other causes are explanations of variation — they explain why war breaks out at this time and place and not at others. Specific alliance configurations, particular leaders' beliefs, immediate crises, balance-of-power moments — these are variation-explaining causes. They tell you why August 1914 specifically.

Both kinds of cause matter. Permissive causes set the field; variation-explaining causes pick the moment. Explaining a specific war requires both — the structural conditions that made it possible and the immediate dynamics that lit the fuse. Peace policy needs to address both: the long-run structural conditions that make conflict more or less likely, and the immediate crisis-management tools that prevent specific situations from escalating.

## Why structural theories explain less than they seem to

A recurring criticism of structural realist theories — neorealism in particular — is that their main explanatory variable, anarchy, is a constant. Constants cannot explain variation. If anarchy is always present, anarchy alone cannot explain why war happens at some times and not others. Structural theorists respond by adding additional variables — polarity, capability distribution, offence-defence balance — that vary across time and place. This rescues the theory's predictive power but complicates the claim that it is "purely structural." The theory now has multiple variables operating at multiple levels.

The honest version of structural theory is that it explains the landscape — which configurations make conflict more or less likely — while leaving specific events to be explained at lower levels. That's a real contribution, just not the whole story.

## Why this matters in policy

For practitioners, the levels-of-analysis framework is useful as a diagnostic. When thinking about a particular conflict or crisis, asking "which level am I working at?" forces clarity about what kind of explanation, and what kind of intervention, the situation calls for. A crisis driven by system-level pressures — say, an emerging power transition — will not be resolved by individual-level interventions like high-level diplomacy alone; the structural pressures will keep pushing. A crisis driven by individual-level miscalculation may be tractable through communication and reassurance, even when the structural backdrop is stable. Matching the level of intervention to the level of cause is part of what separates effective foreign policy from theatrical foreign policy.
