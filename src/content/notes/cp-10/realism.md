# Realism and the Anarchic System

Realism is the most influential framework in international relations theory. Its core claim is that the international system is anarchic, in a specific technical sense: there is no overarching authority that can enforce agreements between states or protect them from one another. Domestic politics happens under the shadow of a government that holds the monopoly on legitimate violence within its territory. International politics happens without any such authority. Each state is, ultimately, on its own.

From this structural fact, realists derive a series of predictions about how states will behave. Because there is no protector, every state has to take care of its own security. Because intentions are uncertain — you can never be sure another state won't attack you at some future point — capabilities matter more than expressed intentions. States accumulate the means of self-defence; they form alliances when useful; they balance against threats; they accept that conflict is a permanent possibility. None of this is because state leaders are bad people. It is because the structural condition of anarchy makes self-help the rational strategy.

Realism comes in two main flavours that have been arguing with each other since the 1970s.

## Classical realism

The older tradition, associated with names like Hans Morgenthau, Reinhold Niebuhr, and E.H. Carr, treats human nature as a key explanatory variable. People — and the states they run — have a drive for power. Some statesmen are more aggressive than others; some periods produce more militarised societies than others. The unit of analysis is the human being and the state, and the explanation for conflict is partly that humans, in their political behaviour, seek dominance. The realist's job is to recognise this rather than wish it away.

## Neorealism (structural realism)

The newer tradition, most associated with Kenneth Waltz in his 1979 book *Theory of International Politics*, removes human nature from the explanation entirely. What matters is the structure of the international system: the fact of anarchy and the distribution of capabilities among states. Two states in the same structural position should behave similarly regardless of what kind of people happen to lead them. The unit of analysis is the system, and individual leaders are interchangeable parts whose behaviour is largely determined by the structure they operate in.

Waltz's move was deliberately reductive. He wanted a theory that was clean, parsimonious, and testable, and he was willing to drop variables that didn't help with that. The price is that neorealism explains the recurrence of war over centuries quite well but explains specific wars less well. Saddam Hussein's invasion of Kuwait in August 1990 fits the structural prediction that states pursue power, but the structural theory cannot say why August 1990 specifically. For that you need a theory of how Saddam, in that moment, made his decision — which is back at the classical realist's level.

## The logical puzzle

Both flavours of realism face a tension between the constancy of their main explanatory variable (anarchy, or human nature) and the variation they are trying to explain. War happens sometimes; peace happens other times. If anarchy is constant, anarchy can't fully explain the variation. Realists have responded by adding intermediate variables — polarity (how many great powers exist), the offence-defence balance (whether military technology favours attackers or defenders), the geography of vulnerability — that vary across time and place while leaving the structural framework intact. The theory remains structural but no longer purely so.

## What realism does and doesn't tell you

Realism is good at predicting the landscape — which conditions make conflict more or less likely, which alliances are stable, which states are more vulnerable. It is less good at predicting specific events, because specific events depend on individual decisions made under particular circumstances. The standard move is to treat realism as a baseline explanation and use other approaches — liberalism, constructivism, foreign policy analysis — to fill in what the structural theory leaves out.

## Why this matters in policy

For working policy thinkers, realism is the framework you have to engage with even if you ultimately disagree with it. The reason is that the people on the other side of the table — leaders, generals, foreign ministers in other countries — are often operating in realist terms whether or not they would call themselves realists. They worry about capabilities, distrust intentions, hedge against worst cases, and assume that the structural pressures of anarchy will eventually shape the relationship. A policy framework that doesn't take this seriously will tend to be surprised in ways the realist isn't. A policy framework that takes only this seriously will miss everything realism leaves out, which is also substantial.
