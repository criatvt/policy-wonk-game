# Information Failures and Business Collapse

If you survey the post-mortems of large business failures, a striking pattern shows up. The collapse is rarely caused by a single bad decision made with full information. It is much more often caused by good decisions made with incomplete information — because the information that would have changed the call was somewhere in the organisation, but not in the room when the call was made.

This is what economists mean when they talk about information failure inside a firm. The relevant signal exists. Someone on the trading floor knows the position is underwater. Some engineer knows the platform is two years behind the competition. Some branch manager knows the customer complaints are spiking. But that knowledge does not travel up to the people who could act on it, and so the organisation continues behaving as if the knowledge did not exist.

Nick Leeson's concealment of the Barings Bank losses is the textbook case. The losses were real. The information about them was held by exactly one person, Leeson himself, who actively hid it. Senior management at Barings was unable to take corrective action — to stop the bleeding, to cut the position, to limit further exposure — because they didn't know there was anything to act on. By the time the information surfaced, the £208 million loss had grown into £827 million and the bank was insolvent. The lesson isn't really about a single rogue trader. It is about an organisation whose controls didn't surface the information senior management needed.

Nokia's loss of the smartphone market is a related case with no rogue actor. Engineers and product managers inside Nokia knew Symbian was uncompetitive by 2007. The information existed inside the firm. But middle management was unwilling to escalate bad news, and senior leadership was insulated from the unfiltered view. By the time the picture reached the top floor, the window to respond had narrowed sharply. The same pattern shows up in many large-organisation post-mortems: the truth was knowable inside the firm long before it was acted on.

A second flavour of the same problem is the magical-belief failure — when prices or expectations stop being treated as information at all. In the years before the 2008 financial crisis, large parts of the financial system operated on the belief that property prices in major markets would simply keep rising. Warnings existed; data existed; sceptical analysts existed. The system as a whole was not reading the signal. When the magical belief broke, the adjustment was violent and global.

These three patterns — concealment, non-escalation, and magical belief — are all forms of the same underlying problem. The decision-maker is operating on a picture of the world that has stopped matching reality. The longer the gap persists, the worse the eventual correction.

## Why this matters in policy

Governments face the same problem at larger scale and with worse feedback loops. A ministry running a programme rarely has strong incentives to surface evidence that the programme isn't working. The people closest to implementation often know first that something is wrong, but the structures that would let them say so — independent evaluation, parliamentary scrutiny, transparent data — are precisely the structures that political pressure pushes back against. Designing institutions that surface uncomfortable information, rather than suppress it, is one of the harder problems in governance, and it is the institutional analogue of the corporate failures above. When the auditor general, the independent evaluator, the whistleblower, or the data dashboard is weak, the same dynamic that took down Barings and Nokia is what will eventually take down the programme.
