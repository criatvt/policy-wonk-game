# Opportunity Cost

The cost of doing something is the best alternative you gave up to do it. If you spend an hour reading this note instead of going for a walk, the cost of reading isn't zero — it's the walk you didn't take. That's opportunity cost, and it's the most important cost concept in economics because it is the cost that actually changes behaviour.

In ordinary speech, "cost" usually means money out of pocket. Economists treat that as one component of cost, often not the most important one. The opportunity cost of using land for a parking lot is the apartment building it could have been. The opportunity cost of an hour spent in a meeting is everything else the participants could have done with that hour. Neither of those alternatives shows up on an invoice, but both are real costs of the choice.

The reason this matters is that decisions get made at the margin — that is, between the next-best alternatives, not against a baseline of zero. If a farmer in Kenya is deciding whether to plant more roses, the relevant question is not "do roses pay" but "do roses pay more than the maize, the coffee, the lettuce, or whatever else this field could grow." When rose prices rose during the 1970s oil shock, what changed wasn't that growing roses became attractive in some absolute sense — it was that the opportunity cost of *not* growing roses (the profit foregone) went up. Producers shifted because the alternatives stopped looking equally good.

The same logic applies to consumers. When the price of roses doubles, the household choosing Valentine's gifts faces a sharper opportunity cost: every bouquet now costs them two bouquets' worth of chocolate, or a dinner out, or some other thing they would have enjoyed. The decision isn't whether roses are "worth it" in isolation; it's whether they are worth what is being given up.

Some opportunity costs are easy to observe — the next-highest market price for the same resource will usually do. Others require imagination, because the alternative was something you didn't end up doing and so there's no receipt for it. This is where opportunity-cost reasoning often goes wrong: people focus on the visible, accounted-for costs and forget the invisible, foregone ones.

## Why this matters in policy

Public budgeting is particularly vulnerable to ignoring opportunity cost. A government programme that consumes administrative capacity, ministerial attention, or political capital is rarely costed at the value of whatever else those scarce resources could have done. A scheme that looks "free" because it uses an existing department's staff isn't free — those staff hours have an opportunity cost. The same goes for capital: a rupee of public spending is also a rupee that didn't go to schools, or wasn't returned to taxpayers, or wasn't deployed by the private sector. The discipline of asking "what is this displacing?" is one of the most important moves in policy analysis, and it is the question that opportunity cost forces.
