# Entrepreneurial Response and Specialisation

When a price rises, the most familiar response is that existing producers make more of the good. The more interesting response — the one that takes longer to play out but has the bigger effect — is that entirely new producers enter the market, often from places and lines of work nobody would have expected. The profit motive is what drives this. A persistently higher price is a persistent signal that there is money to be made by doing things differently, and entrepreneurs respond to it.

The rose story is again the clearest example. When American greenhouse roses became expensive after the 1970s oil shock, the existing American rose growers tried to maintain output, but heating costs continued to squeeze their margins. The real adjustment came from somewhere else entirely: farmers in Kenya, Ecuador, and Colombia who had previously grown other crops realised they had a natural advantage. Their climate provided the warmth that American growers had to buy from heating oil. Investors built cold-chain logistics and air-freight networks to move cut flowers from Nairobi to Amsterdam to Mumbai overnight. Within a couple of decades, the geography of global rose production had been rewritten — not by anyone planning it, but by entrepreneurs each spotting the profit available in their own corner of the system.

The economic name for this is specialisation, and the underlying principle is comparative advantage. If two places differ in their costs of producing two goods, both can be better off if each specialises in what it can produce relatively more cheaply and they trade. Kenya's comparative advantage in roses came from its climate. America's comparative advantage shifted to other things — capital-intensive manufacturing, high-tech services — where its endowments paid off better. The shift wasn't planned in Washington or Nairobi. It was the cumulative effect of millions of marginal decisions by entrepreneurs reading prices.

A second feature of entrepreneurial response is creativity. Prices don't just tell producers to make more of the same thing — they create incentives to find new ways of doing things, new substitutes, new product categories. When roses got expensive in the 1970s, some consumers shifted to chocolate or teddy bears for Valentine's Day, and the firms that sold those things expanded. New rose varieties were bred to be more transportable. Air-freight scheduling was optimised. The set of possible responses to a price signal is open-ended in a way that planning rarely is, because nobody has to ask permission to try a new response — they just try it and see if it pays.

The third feature is risk. Most entrepreneurial responses fail. The Kenyan rose industry today is the success that made it; the businesses that tried similar bets and got the climate, the logistics, or the timing wrong have been forgotten. This is part of how the market mechanism extracts good information from a noisy world. Many entrepreneurs guess; the ones who guess right survive; the ones who guess wrong absorb the losses. The losses are real and they are not socialised, which is what makes the surviving signal trustworthy.

## Why this matters in policy

Two related implications follow. The first is that scarcity tends to call forth solutions if entrepreneurs are allowed to respond to prices. A persistent high price for a good often turns out to be temporary, because the high price itself attracts new supply. Policymakers tempted to suppress the high price — by price control or by subsidising the existing producers — can suppress the very signal that would have brought the new supply into being. The second is that comparative advantage is not destiny. A country's pattern of specialisation today reflects current relative costs, and those costs change as technology, infrastructure, and skills evolve. Policy that locks a country into yesterday's pattern — through tariffs or subsidies that protect incumbents — tends to retard the response to tomorrow's prices.
