# Price as a Signal of Quality

Prices do a second job, in addition to telling everyone how scarce something is. They also tell consumers something about what they're buying. A high price on a watch is taken as a claim about craftsmanship; a low price on a bag of rice is taken as a claim about ordinariness. The price is not the only quality signal — brand, packaging, reviews, country of origin all play a role — but it is one of the strongest, and consumers rely on it heavily when other information is scarce.

Economists call this the signalling role of prices. The argument is that in markets where buyers can't fully inspect the goods before purchase, sellers use price as a credible message about quality. A producer who is confident in their product can charge a premium and survive; a producer of bad goods who tries the same thing gets discovered, loses customers, and exits. Over time, prices in a healthy market come to correspond roughly to perceived quality, and consumers learn to read them as such.

This is why deliberately undermining the quality signal of your own product is so disastrous. The English jewellery chain Ratners is the cautionary tale. In 1991, the company's chief executive Gerald Ratner gave a speech to the Institute of Directors in which he joked that his sherry decanters were "total crap" and that an earring sold in his shops "wouldn't last as long as a prawn sandwich." The audience laughed. The press reported the speech. Within days, the company's value fell by about £500 million. The product hadn't changed. The price hadn't changed. What changed was that the public no longer believed the price-and-product combination was a credible quality signal — and once that trust was broken, the customers stopped coming. The episode entered the language as "doing a Ratner."

The point is not just that you should be polite about your own merchandise. It's that the price you charge is part of an implicit conversation with your customer about what they are getting. When you say "this earring costs £4.99" you are also implicitly saying "and this is a real piece of jewellery, not a joke." Break the second half of that promise and the first half stops working too — customers won't buy at any price, because the price has stopped meaning what it used to mean.

The same logic explains why luxury brands are so aggressive about preventing discounting and counterfeiting. A handbag sold at half price by a third party doesn't just lose revenue for the brand; it weakens the signal that the brand's price is meant to send. Counterfeits, even obviously fake ones, do the same thing — they pollute the signal.

## Why this matters in policy

Quality signals matter in regulated markets too. A medicine sold cheaply in a context where consumers can't easily verify what's in it carries a different signal than the same medicine sold at a regulated price by a licensed pharmacy. Regulatory frameworks that build credibility — quality marks, certified suppliers, public ratings, mandatory disclosures — are doing essentially the same job as a brand reputation: restoring the information content of the price. When the regulatory signal breaks down, as it does in markets for counterfeit drugs or unverified financial products, consumers can't read prices reliably any more, and the market for the good degrades or disappears. Restoring the signal is often more useful than capping the price.
