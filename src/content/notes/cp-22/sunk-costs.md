# Sunk Costs and the Sunk-Cost Fallacy

A sunk cost is a cost that has already been incurred and cannot be recovered no matter what you do next. The money is gone. The hour has been spent. The investment is in the ground. Because no future decision can bring it back, a sunk cost should not influence any decision about what to do next. The only costs that matter going forward are future costs, and the only benefits that matter are future benefits.

This is one of the clearest results in economics, and one of the most consistently violated. People — and the organisations they run — find it astonishingly hard to walk away from things they've already put money into. The pattern shows up everywhere: the failing project that gets one more round of funding because the company has already spent so much; the bad investment that gets held instead of sold because selling would "lock in" the loss; the war that continues because too many have already died to stop now. Daniel Kahneman and others have shown experimentally that this bias is robust across cultures and decision contexts.

The sunk-cost fallacy is the name for treating sunk costs as if they should affect future decisions. The decision rule is simple but uncomfortable: from this point forward, would you make this investment if you were starting fresh and had no history with it? If yes, continue. If no, walk away — regardless of what has already been spent. The right comparison is always between the future you get by continuing and the future you get by stopping. The past expenditure is the same in both branches, so it cancels out of the comparison.

A few cases illustrate how expensive the fallacy can get.

Barings Bank was destroyed in 1995 when a single trader, Nick Leeson, accumulated losses on the Japanese stock market and hid them in a holding account rather than report them. He kept doubling his bets in the hope of trading his way out, partly because each new bet was justified by the sunk losses of the previous ones. By the time the concealment was discovered, the losses had grown from about £208 million to over £800 million — large enough to bankrupt a 233-year-old institution.

Nokia in the late 2000s knew its Symbian operating system was falling behind iOS and Android. Engineers inside the company saw the problem clearly. But Nokia had invested enormously in Symbian over years, and middle management was reluctant to relay bad news upward. The phone giant kept pouring resources into a platform that was already losing, partly because of how much it had already spent on it, and lost its dominant market position within a few years.

Thomas Cook, the company that essentially invented mass tourism in the nineteenth century, kept investing in physical travel agencies and brochures as the internet was making both obsolete. Heavy debt from past acquisitions made it harder to pivot, and the past spending kept rationalising more past-style spending. The firm collapsed in 2019.

The throughline in all three is not just bad luck. It is the difficulty of treating past investment as irrelevant to future choice. Once an organisation has committed publicly to a path, reversing course often costs reputation, careers, and political support — so the rational stopping point is overshot.

## Why this matters in policy

The sunk-cost fallacy is especially dangerous in government because public commitments are politically visible. An infrastructure project that has consumed years of effort and crores of rupees acquires a constituency: contractors, ministries, and politicians who staked their credibility on it. Abandoning the project signals failure and invites opposition, so it tends to continue past the point where any honest analyst would say "the future cost of finishing this exceeds the future benefit." The same dynamic can keep a subsidy programme, a military deployment, or a regulatory framework alive long after it has stopped doing what it was meant to do. The discipline of asking "would we start this today, given what we now know?" is the antidote — but it is a discipline that political incentives often punish.
