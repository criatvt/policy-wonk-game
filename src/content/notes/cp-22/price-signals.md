# Prices as Signals

A price isn't just a number on a tag. It's a piece of information about how scarce something is relative to how much people want it. When the price of a good rises, that change is telling every producer in the world that the good is becoming more valuable, and telling every consumer that it has become more costly to choose. Both sides respond — usually without consulting each other, and usually without knowing why the price moved in the first place.

The textbook example is the global rose market in the 1970s. When oil prices spiked, heating American greenhouses got expensive. Rose prices rose. A Kenyan farmer noticing the higher price he could now get for roses had no idea about OPEC or the politics of crude oil — he just saw a more profitable crop and planted more of it. Within a few years, much of the world's cut-flower production had migrated to naturally warm countries like Kenya, Ecuador, and Colombia. No one organised this. No conference was called. The price did the organising.

The reason this works is that a single number — the price — bundles together an enormous amount of dispersed information: the cost of every input that went into producing the good, the value every potential buyer places on it, what alternatives exist, what substitutes are available. None of these pieces of information live in any one head. Yet the price aggregates them into a signal that every actor in the market can read and respond to with their own local decisions.

A rising price tells producers: scarcity is here, profit is available, expand. A falling price tells them: too much supply, redirect resources elsewhere. The same signal, read from the consumer side, says: economise, look for substitutes, or pay more if you really want it. The genius of the system is that the same number does both jobs simultaneously, and the adjustment happens automatically as buyers and sellers respond to their own circumstances.

This is why economists describe the price system as performing a coordination function that no committee could replicate. The committee would need to know what every consumer prefers, what every producer's costs are, what every substitute is available, and how all of these change minute to minute. A market doesn't need any committee to know any of this — the price aggregates it.

## Why this matters in policy

When a government caps a price, fixes it, or subsidises it heavily, it isn't just transferring money. It is changing the signal that producers and consumers are reading. A cap below the market clearing price tells consumers "this is cheap, use lots of it" while telling producers "there's no point making more of this." Shortages follow. A subsidy that lowers a price below cost can persuade consumers to over-consume something that is genuinely scarce. The information embedded in the price is what gets disrupted — and a distorted signal produces distorted decisions across millions of actors who have no way of knowing what the true scarcity is. Whether to intervene in a market is a legitimate policy question, but the cost of intervention is partly the loss of information the price would otherwise have carried.
