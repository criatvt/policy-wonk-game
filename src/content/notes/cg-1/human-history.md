# Cultural Revolutions in Human History

Big-history accounts of the human story usually identify a handful of cultural revolutions — moments when the way humans organised themselves changed so fundamentally that everything downstream looked different. The course material follows Yuval Noah Harari's framing in *Sapiens*, identifying four such turning points.

## The Cognitive Revolution, around 70,000 years ago

What changed: the development of language sophisticated enough to express not just things that exist ("there is a tiger") but things that don't — myths, gods, abstract ideas, shared fictions ("our tribe descends from the lion spirit"). This new cognitive capability let humans cooperate flexibly in groups of strangers, because strangers who share a myth can act as if they share a kinship. Other species cooperate, but only in small kin-based groups or rigid hive-based ones. Sapiens learned to scale cooperation across hundreds, then thousands, then millions, by building shared imagined orders — religions, nations, companies, money — that everyone in the group treated as real. The capability that distinguishes humans is not abstract intelligence; it is collective imagination.

## The Agricultural Revolution, around 12,000 years ago

What changed: the domestication of plants and animals. Bands of foragers became settled farmers, and food production rose dramatically while diversity of diet and quality of individual life often fell. Agriculture supported much larger populations, made denser settlements possible, and produced the surplus that allowed some people to specialise in things other than food production — priests, artisans, soldiers, accountants. Cities, writing, taxation, organised religion, and large-scale property all emerge in the slipstream of agriculture. The course material notes that this was not unambiguously a good deal for the average human — early farmers worked harder and lived shorter lives than the foragers they replaced — but agricultural societies out-competed and absorbed the alternatives.

## The Scientific Revolution, around 500 years ago

What changed: the recognition that we don't know everything, combined with a method for finding out what we don't know. Pre-modern knowledge systems treated the truth as essentially complete and already revealed in sacred texts or classical authority. The scientific revolution, beginning in Europe in the 16th and 17th centuries, replaced that assumption with the working premise that ignorance is the default condition and that systematic empirical investigation can reduce it. Combined with the political and economic incentives of European maritime expansion, this produced a feedback loop in which new knowledge led to new capability, new capability funded more investigation, and the accumulation accelerated. The natural sciences as we know them are a product of this turn.

## The Industrial Revolution, around 200 years ago

What changed: the application of mechanical power, first steam and then electricity, to production. Output per worker rose by an order of magnitude over a few generations. Cities grew, families shrank, life expectancy lengthened, and the state and market replaced family and village as the dominant institutions organising human life. Most of what we think of as modernity — mass schooling, mass media, mass politics, factory work, the nuclear household — is a downstream consequence of the industrial reorganisation of work and life.

These four turning points share a feature worth noting. Each was driven by a technological or cognitive capability that allowed humans to operate at a new scale: larger groups, denser settlements, deeper knowledge, more powerful production. And each, in turn, required new cultural and institutional forms to manage the new scale. We are currently in the middle of what some call a fifth revolution — variously labelled the information, computational, or cognitive — whose long-run cultural consequences are still being worked out.

## Why this matters in policy

The deep-history view is useful as a corrective to short-termism. Policy debates often treat current arrangements as natural, when they are in fact recent. The nuclear family, the eight-hour workday, mass democracy, universal literacy, the welfare state — none of these existed in their current forms two hundred years ago. Knowing that the social and political forms we work within are themselves products of relatively recent transformations makes it easier to take seriously the idea that they will keep changing.
