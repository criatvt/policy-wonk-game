# What Culture Is

When social scientists talk about culture, they usually mean something broader than the everyday English usage of the word. Culture in this technical sense isn't just art and music and food — it's the entire package of learned beliefs and behaviours, the unwritten rules people in a group use to order their lives, and the shared meanings they use to interpret the world. The point of the broader definition is that it includes the things people don't notice they share, alongside the things they explicitly value.

A useful way to break culture down is into four kinds of manifestation: symbols, values, rituals, and heroes. Symbols are the visible carriers of meaning — words, gestures, flags, colours, dress — and they are the easiest to notice because they show up on the surface. Rituals are patterns of behaviour that are socially required even when they have no obvious practical function: a wedding ceremony, a board meeting's opening prayer, the way an office greets new hires. Heroes are the people, real or mythical, whom a culture holds up as exemplars of how to live; what kind of person becomes a hero tells you a lot about what the culture prizes. Values sit underneath all of these — the deeper preferences for what is good, important, or admirable — and they are usually the hardest to see and the slowest to change.

A second useful idea is that culture is "symbolic communication." A culture's skills, attitudes, knowledge, and motives are embedded in symbols that get learned and passed on through institutions — family, school, workplace, religion, media. The transmission isn't only through explicit statements ("we believe X"). It happens through the way people behave, the stories they tell, the way buildings are arranged, the way meetings are run. A great deal of cultural learning is implicit, which is why culture is hard to describe from the inside: most of it is invisible to the people who live in it because they have nothing to compare it to.

This has a few practical implications. First, culture is not biology. People aren't born with the values and beliefs they end up holding; they learn them as members of the society they grow up in. Second, culture is not static. It evolves, sometimes slowly and sometimes fast, in response to environmental, technological, and political change. Third, every individual carries pieces of multiple cultures simultaneously — national, regional, professional, generational, and so on — and these layers interact in ways that make any one person's behaviour not fully reducible to any single cultural identity.

## Why this matters in policy

Public policy frequently runs into cultural assumptions without realising it. A nutrition programme that assumes a household has a male breadwinner who decides spending will perform differently in cultures where women manage household budgets. A water-conservation campaign that frames thrift as virtue will land differently in cultures where conspicuous use is a marker of status. The same intervention, with the same design, can succeed in one society and fail in another for reasons that have nothing to do with the intervention itself and everything to do with the cultural context it lands in. Treating culture as background — something to be acknowledged in the foreword and then ignored in the design — is a recurring source of avoidable policy failure.
