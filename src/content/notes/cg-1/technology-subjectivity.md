# The Subjectivity Problem

Technical systems work by standardising inputs, producing predictable processes, and generating measurable outputs. That is what makes them powerful: a well-designed technical system can be applied a million times with consistent results, in places and circumstances its designer never saw. The price of this power is that the system has to treat the things it processes as standard. Variation, ambiguity, and judgment-dependent input are problems for technique, not opportunities.

Humans, awkwardly, are full of variation, ambiguity, and judgment-dependent input. We weigh evidence differently, frame problems differently, hold contradictory preferences, change our minds, and routinely make decisions for reasons we can't fully articulate. This is what makes us interesting, and it is also what makes us hard to feed into technical systems unchanged.

The course material puts it sharply: technology wants to solve the dilemma of human subjectivity. That is, technology is in constant tension with the messiness of human judgment, and it consistently pushes — through automation, standardisation, and quantification — to reduce that messiness so its systems can run. This shows up in several recognisable patterns.

First, the conversion of social sciences into "sciences." Disciplines like psychology, sociology, and economics have over the past century moved progressively toward quantitative methods, formal models, and replicable experiments. There are good reasons for some of this. But part of the pressure comes from the demands of technique: if human behaviour can be turned into measurable variables, it can be fed into systems that act on it. What the conversion loses is the interpretive, narrative, contextual side of human life — the things that resist being numbered.

Second, the standardisation of work. Industrial and post-industrial work has steadily replaced craft skill with procedure. A skilled worker who used judgment to handle each unit becomes an operator who runs a process designed to handle units identically. The output is more reliable; the work is more bearable for unskilled labour; the cost of variation falls. What is lost is the judgement of the skilled worker, who often had embedded knowledge that the standard process can't reproduce. Some of this trade-off is fine. Some of it leaves real value on the table.

Third, the rise of metrics-driven management. Organisations that can be measured can be managed at scale. The flip side is that things that get measured get optimised, and things that don't get measured get ignored. A school managed by test scores gets better at producing test scores. A hospital managed by length-of-stay gets better at keeping length-of-stay low. Whether the underlying purpose of the institution is well served depends on whether the metrics capture what mattered, and they usually don't fully.

The tension between technique and subjectivity isn't going to be resolved. The question is where, in any given system, the line falls. Pure subjectivity is unmanageable at scale; pure standardisation strips away things people legitimately care about. Most working systems sit somewhere in between, and the politics of where to sit is one of the long-running political fights of modern life.

## Why this matters in policy

Public administration is particularly exposed to the subjectivity problem. Welfare assessments, asylum decisions, parole boards, school placements — all are domains where the underlying question requires judgment, but the demands of scale, fairness, and accountability push toward algorithmic standardisation. The argument for standardisation is that it reduces arbitrary variation across cases; the argument against is that it strips away the contextual judgment that hard cases require. Where to sit on this trade-off is one of the design choices every public service makes, often without fully realising that it is making it. Becoming explicit about the trade-off — rather than leaving it implicit in the choice of software — is the first move toward making it well.
