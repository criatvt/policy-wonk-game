# Cultural Determinism

Cultural determinism is the position that human nature is mostly shaped by the ideas, meanings, beliefs, and values people learn as members of a society — rather than by biology, environment, or any universal human essence. In its strongest forms, it implies that almost anything about how humans think and behave could in principle be different in a different cultural setting, and that "human nature" is mostly a placeholder for "what we happened to learn growing up here."

The position comes in two flavours that are worth distinguishing because their implications are very different.

The optimistic version holds that because human nature is malleable, people can collectively choose the kind of life they want to lead. If our values are learned rather than fixed, then we can revise them. If our institutions reflect our values, then we can build new institutions to reflect better values. This reading underpins a lot of progressive political thought: the assumption that intentional change is possible because nothing in human nature stops it.

The pessimistic version holds that because culture shapes us so deeply, people are essentially passive — products of conditioning who do what their culture tells them to do without genuine choice. On this reading, individuals are not the authors of their own values; they are downstream of forces they didn't pick. This reading underpins more deterministic accounts of social life, where escape from cultural conditioning requires either extraordinary effort or external rupture.

Both versions point at the same empirical claim — that culture matters enormously — but they draw opposite practical conclusions. The first says: change is possible, get on with it. The second says: change is hard, expect resistance.

A second piece of the cultural determinism thesis is its insistence that there is no universal "right way" of being human. Each society sees its own way as natural and correct, but the variation across societies in what they consider natural is so wide that no single set of practices can be defended as universally right. This is sometimes called cultural relativism, and it has serious implications: it means that judging another society's practices against the standards of your own is itself a culturally specific move.

The position has a well-known self-referential puzzle. If all values are culturally specific, then so is the value of cultural relativism — it is itself a position held from somewhere. The relativist therefore cannot claim relativism as a universal truth without contradicting themselves. This doesn't necessarily destroy the position, but it limits how strongly it can be asserted as a basis for cross-cultural judgment.

## Why this matters in policy

In international policy, cultural determinism is a useful corrective and also a useful caution. The corrective: policies designed against the assumption that "people everywhere basically want the same things" tend to misfire when they collide with cultures whose actual priorities differ. The caution: taking cultural relativism too far paralyses policy debate, because if every practice is justified by the culture that produced it, no policy can ever be criticised from outside. Most working policymakers end up holding a weaker version of the position: that cultures differ significantly, that any policy must take that seriously, but that some things — large-scale violence, the exclusion of whole groups from basic rights — can still be evaluated across cultural lines. Where exactly that line sits is a permanent argument.
