# Culture and Technology

One useful framing for thinking about how humans differ from other species is that culture is our operating system. Biological evolution is too slow — operating over tens of thousands of generations — to let us adapt to environments that change as fast as the ones we now build for ourselves. Culture handles the rest. It is the layer of inherited knowledge, behaviour, and shared meaning that lets a child born today function in a world her great-grandmother could not have imagined, without having to evolve a new brain.

Technology, in turn, is what culture produces. Treating technology narrowly as "machines" misses most of it. Technology in the broader sense is the application of knowledge — how we make and use tools, techniques, and systems of organisation to solve problems. A spreadsheet is a technology; so is double-entry bookkeeping; so is a court system; so is a school. Each is an artefact of some culture's accumulated knowledge being deployed against some problem the culture defined as worth solving.

The relationship between the two runs both ways. Culture shapes which problems get framed as worth solving, what counts as a good solution, and which technologies get adopted. A society that prizes individual achievement will build different technologies of work than one that prizes collective contribution. A society that distrusts surveillance will regulate identity systems differently than one that doesn't. The technologies a society produces tell you something about what it values.

Running the other direction, technologies reshape the cultures that produced them. Print made literacy widespread and reorganised authority. Steam concentrated populations in cities and unmade rural family economies. The smartphone has rewired attention, intimacy, and politics in ways nobody planned. Each technological wave changes the cultural conditions under which the next is created, so the two co-evolve rather than running in parallel.

This co-evolution has an unsettling feature. Cultures adopt technologies for their short-term benefits — cheaper, faster, more powerful — before they can see the long-term cultural effects. By the time the cultural shift becomes visible, the technology is usually too embedded to roll back. The printing press, the factory, social media: each was adopted because its immediate value was obvious, and each ended up transforming the societies that adopted it in ways that took decades to understand. There is no straightforward way to opt out of this dynamic, because once a competing society adopts a powerful new technology, the cost of refusing it tends to compound.

The course material puts the point sharply: every technology is a product of its economic and political context, and every technology has a program, an agenda, and a philosophy. None of them are neutral tools. Spreadsheets favour quantification. Algorithms favour patterns over outliers. Surveillance technologies make some kinds of social order easier and others harder. Choosing a technology is therefore also choosing — often without realising it — the cultural direction it will pull.

## Why this matters in policy

Two implications follow. First, evaluating a technology only on efficiency or safety grounds misses what often matters most: the values it embeds and the behaviours it makes easier or harder. Second, technology transfer between cultural contexts rarely works as a simple drop-in. A health record system designed around the institutional assumptions of one country's clinics doesn't run the same way in another country's clinics; an identity system designed against one culture's privacy expectations will collide with another's. The technology and the culture have to fit, and figuring out the fit is part of the work — not something to be left to deployment.
