# Layers of Culture

It is tempting to talk about culture as if each person belonged to exactly one — a national culture, an ethnic culture, a religious culture — and as if that one membership explained their behaviour. Anyone who has ever introduced themselves to a stranger knows it isn't that simple. A person is at the same time an Indian, a Tamilian, a Muslim, a millennial, a software engineer, a parent, a woman. Each of those is a cultural layer, and each contributes something to how she sees the world, what she takes for granted, what she finds odd in other people.

A standard list of layers, working roughly from broadest to narrowest, looks like:

- National — the country one is a citizen of, with its associated history and language.
- Regional — sub-national identity rooted in a state, a region, or a city.
- Ethnic, linguistic, and religious — sometimes overlapping with regional, sometimes cross-cutting it.
- Generational — what was going on in the world during one's formative years.
- Gender — patterns of behaviour and expectation organised around being identified as male, female, or neither.
- Class — the cultural register associated with one's economic position.
- Corporate or professional — the implicit rules of one's workplace, profession, or industry.

Each layer brings its own symbols, rituals, values, and heroes, and a given person carries all of them simultaneously. The layers don't always agree. A professional culture might demand long hours that a family culture frowns on. A national culture might emphasise collective duty while a generational culture emphasises individual self-realisation. Most cultural conflict within a single person is in fact a tension between layers, and the resolution is rarely clean.

This has two important consequences for thinking about culture in social science.

First, treating "national culture" as the dominant unit of analysis is a simplification that often hides more than it reveals. Two Indian software engineers in Bangalore may share more cultural ground with two software engineers in Berlin than with farmers in their own state. The professional layer can be louder than the national layer for some purposes. Saying "Indian culture says X" usually means "this layer of some Indian cultures says X" — and getting precise about which layer matters.

Second, individual behaviour is not predictable from cultural membership alone. The mix of layers, plus the individual's own history and choices, produces an irreducible amount of variation. Marketing analysts and policy designers who try to model behaviour from demographic categories alone tend to be surprised by how much variance is left over.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy communication has to work across these layers without flattening them. A health campaign that reads well in a national-cultural register might lose force in a regional-cultural one. A nudge that resonates with a generational layer (say, scarcity-framing for older audiences) might fail with a different one. The practical move is usually to think in terms of the dominant layer for a given decision context: who is this message reaching, in which role, at which moment? A young woman receiving a public-health text message might be reading it as a daughter, a wife, a professional, or a member of a generation — and the most effective framing differs. Designing as if the audience has one cultural identity is a bet against most of what the social science actually says.
