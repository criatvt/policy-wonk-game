# How Technologies Get Adopted

Technologies don't succeed because they are objectively better. They succeed because the society they meet finds them acceptable. The history of innovation is littered with technically superior products that lost to inferior rivals because the rivals fit the cultural moment better. Sony's Betamax was widely considered a higher-quality video format than VHS; VHS won the home market because of price, licensing, and content availability. Dvorak keyboard layouts have been shown to outperform QWERTY on speed and ergonomics; QWERTY won because it got there first and locked in. The lesson is that technical merit is necessary but rarely sufficient.

The course material describes the social acceptance process in three phases.

## Awareness

People have to know the technology exists. This sounds trivial but isn't. Awareness depends on channels of communication that themselves are cultural artefacts — markets, media, trade networks, word of mouth, schooling. Technologies that don't reach the right channels don't get adopted, no matter how good. The history of the developing world has many cases of useful technologies that existed elsewhere for decades before reaching the people who would have benefited most.

## Evaluation

Once aware, people assess the technology against their existing beliefs, practices, and constraints. This is where cultural fit does most of its work. A technology that contradicts strongly-held values, undermines existing social arrangements, or requires changes in behaviour the culture rewards differently will fail at this stage — even if it would, on paper, deliver clear benefits. A new agricultural method that demands female labour in cultures with strict gender segregation will struggle. A digital identity system that asks people to register their biometrics in cultures with strong privacy norms will struggle. Evaluation isn't a separate rational exercise; it's deeply entangled with how people already see the world.

## Adoption

The technology becomes part of normal life. Adoption is rarely uniform; usually it spreads in waves, starting with early adopters and reaching the wider population only after social proof accumulates. Once adopted at scale, the technology becomes part of the cultural infrastructure and is hard to remove — which is part of why the evaluation phase is so consequential. After adoption, the technology starts to reshape the culture that adopted it, completing the loop.

Several features of this process are worth pulling out.

First, the same technology can succeed in one society and fail in another, because the evaluation depends on cultural fit. The contraceptive pill spread fast in some societies and slowly in others. Mobile money took off in Kenya and lagged in many richer countries. Neither outcome is about the underlying technology; both are about how the technology met the culture.

Second, even within a single society, evaluation varies by sub-group. Different generational, professional, and class layers evaluate the same technology differently. This is why mass adoption often follows a slow start: it takes time for the first adopters to demonstrate use cases that translate across layers.

Third, technologies can fail even with strong policy backing, when the cultural evaluation goes against them. Subsidies, mandates, and information campaigns can push past mild resistance, but they cannot override deep cultural mismatch. Health programmes promoting practices that conflict with religious or family norms regularly underperform their forecasts because the cultural evaluation, not the cost, was the binding constraint.

## Why this matters in policy

Designing for cultural fit is part of the design, not an afterthought. Programmes that pilot a new technology in one community before scaling, that involve potential adopters in evaluation, and that pay attention to which layers of culture will read the technology as compatible — these tend to do better than programmes that assume universal applicability. A useful question to ask early is "which features of the existing culture is this technology asking people to change?" If the answer is "many" or "deeply held ones," expect resistance and budget for it.
