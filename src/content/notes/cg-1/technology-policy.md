# Technology, Politics, and Policy

A common framing of technology treats it as a neutral tool that can be put to good or bad use depending on the person using it. The course material rejects this framing as too convenient. Every technology, it argues, is a product of the economic and political context that produced it, and every technology carries — embedded in its design — a program, an agenda, and a philosophy. Choosing the technology is therefore choosing the program, often without realising you have.

A few examples bring the abstraction to ground.

The shipping container is a thoroughly humble technology. It is a metal box with standard dimensions. But the standardisation it enforced — interchangeable across ships, trucks, and trains worldwide — restructured global trade, dismantled the role of dockworker as a skilled trade, and made it cost-effective to manufacture in one continent for consumption in another. The box was not neutral. It came with an agenda: standardise, scale, decouple production from consumption geography. Anyone choosing to use it was also choosing those consequences.

The spreadsheet looks like a productivity tool. It is also a way of seeing the world. Things on a spreadsheet are numbers in cells; things not in cells aren't in the picture. Decisions made through spreadsheets favour what can be quantified over what can't — financial returns over morale, measurable outcomes over diffuse benefits, this-quarter results over long-term resilience. None of this is the spreadsheet's fault, but adopting the spreadsheet as the decision medium tilts decisions in those directions.

The recommendation algorithm in a social media feed is even more obviously an embedded philosophy. It selects content to maximise engagement on the platform, by whatever proxy the platform measures. The proxy is not "what's true" or "what's useful to know" or "what will leave the user better off in a year." The algorithm carries an agenda — keep people on the platform — and a philosophy — engagement is the value worth optimising. Users encounter the algorithm as a neutral pipe; it isn't one.

Surveillance technologies make a category of governance possible that wasn't previously practical. Mass facial recognition, behavioural tracking, social credit scoring — these are not just tools that an existing government could use more or less. They reshape what government can be, by making forms of monitoring cheap that used to require armies of secret police. A society that adopts these technologies for narrow purposes — finding criminals, optimising traffic — also enables a much wider set of possible governance arrangements, some of them less attractive than the original purpose.

The general lesson the course draws is that governance of technology has to address the embedded assumptions, not just the surface use. Evaluating a technology only on whether it works safely and efficiently misses the larger question of what kind of society it helps construct. Two governance principles follow.

The first is scrutiny. Important technologies deserve political and ethical examination of the assumptions they embed, not only technical examination of their performance. This is uncomfortable because it means the public and its representatives need to engage with technical detail, and the technical experts need to engage with politics. Neither tends to enjoy the conversation. The alternative — leaving the embedded politics to the people who designed the system, or to the users who happened to adopt it — has historically not gone well.

The second is humility about transfer. Technologies that work well in their context of origin often misfire when transplanted to a context with different cultural and institutional assumptions. A clinic management system designed for a country with universal coverage doesn't fit cleanly into one without. An identity system designed for one privacy regime doesn't fit another. The work of adaptation isn't optional, and pretending it is produces predictable failure.

## Why this matters in policy

A working approach is to make the embedded assumptions explicit. When evaluating a technology for adoption, asking questions like "what does this technology assume about its users? what behaviours does it reward? what does it make harder to do? what does it make easier?" surfaces design choices that would otherwise stay invisible. The answers often change the case for or against adoption — and sometimes change the design before adoption, which is the more valuable outcome.
