# International Institutions and Sovereignty

International institutions sit at the heart of Krasner's paradox. On one hand, they *constitute* state sovereignty: the United Nations, the International Court of Justice, and the network of mutual diplomatic recognition are the means by which a state's claim to be a state is acknowledged by other states. Without international institutional recognition, sovereignty is incomplete. On the other hand, the same institutions impose constraints — substantive ones — on how member states can behave. The recognition is conditional on submission to collective rules.

The conditionality is what produces the paradox. A state that wants to be acknowledged as fully sovereign must accept that other states have an interest in, and a partial say over, its conduct. The UN Charter prohibits aggression against fellow members. The WTO regime constrains protectionist trade measures. Human-rights treaties commit signatories to enforce particular domestic standards. International humanitarian law constrains how the state may treat civilians in conflict, including its own citizens. Each of these mutually recognised commitments is, in strict Westphalian terms, a partial cession of sovereignty.

The institutions work, despite this, because the trade is broadly favourable for most states most of the time. Recognition, the protection of diplomatic immunity, the predictability of trade rules, access to multilateral finance, and the right to be heard in disputes are all worth the constraints. The framework is fragile when a powerful state decides the bargain is no longer worth it — but it has proved durable across many decades because the alternative, for most states, is worse.

## Why this matters in policy

Engagement with international institutions is not a one-way cession of authority but a structured exchange. Practitioners who understand the exchange tend to negotiate it better; those who frame it as a binary choice between sovereignty and submission tend to get less out of it.
