# How States Justify Sovereignty Violations

A striking feature of the international system is that sovereignty as a principle survives mountains of evidence that it gets routinely violated. The trick, Krasner points out, is that states *justify* the violations in ways that purport to leave the principle intact. The justifications come from a small, well-rehearsed library.

*Exception:* the violation is an exception to a rule that otherwise holds. Sanctions, humanitarian interventions, and pre-emptive strikes are presented as exceptional responses to exceptional circumstances. The very framing as "exception" keeps the underlying rule of non-interference notionally undisturbed.

*Consent:* the target state, or its previous government, agreed to the constraint, so the constraint is not really a violation. Treaty membership, bilateral aid agreements, debt restructuring, and basing rights all run on this logic. Whether the consent was freely given is a separate question that the doctrine prefers not to look at too closely.

*Responsibility:* the intervening states are discharging a higher obligation — to protect populations from atrocity, to enforce international human-rights norms, to prevent weapons proliferation. The principle of sovereignty cedes to a higher principle for the limited purpose, but is otherwise untouched.

*Necessity:* the violation is required by overriding national interest — usually security. National emergency, self-defence, and threats to existential interests are invoked. The frame is that any state in the same position would have done the same.

The cumulative effect is that the principle of sovereignty absorbs its own breaches without breaking. Every violation comes with a story explaining why this particular one does not undermine the rule.

## Why this matters in policy

For policy actors, the takeaway is to read justification language carefully. Whether an action is framed as "exception" vs "responsibility" vs "necessity" tells you which rhetorical channel the state is using, and which precedents it is and isn't willing to accept being held to later.
