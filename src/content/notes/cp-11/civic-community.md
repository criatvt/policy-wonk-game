# Civic Community

Civic nationalism — the close cousin of, and sometimes alternative label for, liberal nationalism — defines the national community through shared political institutions and constitutional principles rather than through shared ancestry. To belong, on this view, is to be a participating member of the civic order: to obey its laws, to enjoy its rights, to take part in its democratic processes, and to accept the constitutional framework as legitimate. Where one's grandparents lived, and what they spoke, are secondary.

The intellectual genealogy of civic nationalism runs through the French and American revolutions. Both founding traditions, at least in their official self-descriptions, claimed to constitute a *people* through political acts — declarations of rights, constitutional conventions — rather than through a shared ethnic past. The American "we, the people" and the French Republic's universalism both lean civic in this sense, however imperfectly the practice has tracked the rhetoric over two centuries.

The civic conception is more inclusive than ethnic nationalism — in principle, anyone who commits to the constitutional project can become a full member — but it asks more of its citizens. They have to share a political vocabulary, accept the legitimacy of disagreement within rules, and tolerate cultural plurality on the assumption that civic loyalty is enough to hold them together. When that civic loyalty thins out, civic nationalism is correspondingly vulnerable.

## Why this matters in policy

Civic nationalism makes specific demands on the design of public institutions. Schools have to teach a shared civic curriculum without doubling as cultural assimilation machinery. Courts have to be visibly fair across communities. Citizenship procedures have to admit newcomers on principled rather than ethnic grounds. Public commemorations have to be inclusive enough to feel like everyone's. Getting these details right is what gives a civic-national project something to belong to.
