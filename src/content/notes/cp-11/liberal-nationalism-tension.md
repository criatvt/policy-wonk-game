# Liberal Nationalism: The Universal–Particular Tension

Liberal nationalism's most interesting structural problem is that it asks citizens to commit, at the same time, to two pulls in different directions. The liberal half is *universalist*: human rights belong to humans as such, not in virtue of where they were born; equal dignity is owed to everyone regardless of nation; basic justice does not stop at the border. The national half is *particularist*: this is *our* country, with *our* history, *our* institutions, and a special set of obligations to fellow citizens that we do not owe to strangers in other countries.

Both halves are defensible. But in concrete cases they pull apart. The universalist commitment says that a refugee fleeing persecution has the same claim to safety as a citizen. The national commitment says that the citizen has a stronger claim on shared resources. The universalist says that a war crime is a crime regardless of who commits it; the national says that we have a special interest in protecting our own.

The honest liberal nationalist neither resolves this tension nor pretends it does not exist. The position is that the modern nation-state is the place where this tension gets *managed* — through immigration policy, foreign aid, refugee law, the differential rights of citizens and residents, the rules of universal jurisdiction. None of these resolves the conflict philosophically. They produce workable, contestable settlements.

## Why this matters in policy

A great deal of practical politics — immigration, foreign aid, refugee admission, defence spending — is the universal-particular tension argued out in the form of policy. Understanding both pulls as legitimate makes the arguments more tractable. Pretending one of them isn't real makes them harder.
