# Sovereignty and International Relations

A standard objection to Krasner's framework is that the violations he catalogues only matter for weak or failing states. Strong states, the objection goes, really do enjoy full sovereignty in the Westphalian sense — the violations he describes are simply pressure exerted on those who can't push back. Krasner's reply is that the gap between juridical sovereignty (legal recognition) and empirical sovereignty (actual control) is *structural*, not merely a reflection of power imbalances. It shows up across the system, including for the most powerful states.

The evidence is that even the most powerful states accept significant constraints on their sovereignty. The United States is bound by trade rules it cannot unilaterally rewrite, by Security Council resolutions even when domestically inconvenient, by extradition treaties, by environmental commitments. Major European powers operate inside an EU framework that pools sovereignty extensively. China accepts WTO discipline, runs an enormous foreign-exchange operation that depends on cross-border rules it didn't write, and joins climate negotiations whose outcomes constrain its industrial policy. None of these states is exercising the absolute non-interference of pure Westphalian doctrine.

The pattern is that sovereignty is breached predictably along particular axes — human rights, finance, security, environment — and that the breaches are organised by stable rules, not by random power plays. Krasner's framework is descriptive rather than prescriptive: it tells you where to look for the actual structure of international authority, behind the surface vocabulary of sovereign equality.

## Why this matters in policy

For practitioners, the consequence is that international cooperation is less constrained by sovereignty doctrine than the rhetoric implies. The frontiers of feasible cooperation are set by domestic political economy and by the rules of specific regimes (trade, finance, security), not by the formal principle of sovereignty itself. Treating that principle as the binding constraint usually misreads the actual room for manoeuvre.
