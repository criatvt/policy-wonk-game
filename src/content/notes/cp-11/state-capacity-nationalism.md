# State Capacity and Nationalism

Chatterjee's analysis points to a less-noticed function of nationalist discourse: it gets used by states to legitimise the expansion of their administrative reach into corners of social life that earlier states did not try to govern. The argument is that nationalism's claim to represent "the people" makes interventions into family, religious, educational, and economic life look like the people governing themselves, rather than like the state extending control.

The instruments are familiar across both colonial and postcolonial settings. Mass schooling extends state-defined curricula into village classrooms. Civil registries record births, marriages, and deaths that were previously private and communal. Welfare programmes deliver benefits in exchange for bureaucratic registration. National development plans set targets that mobilise local labour and resources for state-defined priorities. National security frameworks justify surveillance and emergency powers that would otherwise be politically harder to legitimise. Each of these is an extension of state capacity into social terrain, and each is more politically saleable when wrapped in nationalist framing.

This is not necessarily bad. Some of these expansions — public health programmes, universal schooling, anti-discrimination enforcement — represent genuine progress. The point is descriptive: nationalist rhetoric makes the expansions politically feasible. The state's nationalism is, partly, the legitimating story for its administrative growth.

The flip side is that when nationalist legitimation weakens, state capacity tends to come under pressure. Citizens become more willing to push back against interventions that no longer feel like the people governing themselves. Capacity that was built on a national consensus is harder to maintain when the consensus thins.

## Why this matters in policy

For state-building work, the implication is that capacity expansion and nationalist legitimation are linked in practice. Capacity built without legitimation tends to face friction; legitimation without capacity tends to disappoint. Sustainable state-building usually has to work on both at once.
