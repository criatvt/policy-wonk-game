# International Law as Legitimation

A reasonable response to a constant pattern of sovereignty violations would be that international law is therefore broken — that it doesn't work because compliance is so selective. Krasner pushes back against this. His view is that international law does not primarily function as a *constraint* on state behaviour; it functions as a *legitimation device* that gives state behaviour international recognition and orderly form. The selectivity is not a failure of the system; it is a feature of how the system actually works.

The mechanics: states write international law together. They negotiate the texts, attach reservations, ratify selectively, and operate the institutions that interpret the rules. When a state acts in accordance with rules it helped write, it gets to claim the legitimacy of those rules. When a state acts in tension with them, it usually finds an interpretive route that frames its action as consistent — invoking exceptions, claiming higher authority, citing precedents that point the other way. The system rarely *forces* compliance, and so the binding work international law does is more about giving states a recognised vocabulary in which to defend their actions than about preventing them.

This is a deflating view, and Krasner accepts the deflation. But it is also useful: it predicts that international law will be most effective where states already have aligned interests in coordination (postal services, air-traffic control, financial settlement) and least effective where interests diverge (human rights enforcement against unwilling states, nuclear non-proliferation, climate). The empirical record fits the prediction.

## Why this matters in policy

For practitioners, the implication is to keep clear-eyed about which international rules have genuine purchase and which are largely vocabulary. Coordinating regimes in which compliance is in everyone's interest are durable; constraining regimes in which compliance is costly are leaky. Both serve purposes — but very different ones.
