# Partha Chatterjee on the State

Partha Chatterjee's contribution to this module is to challenge the implicit European framing of much nationalism theory. The European story — nation first, then state, then nation-state — fits the late-arriving nationalisms of the nineteenth century in places like Germany and Italy, but does not fit the colonised world's experience. In colonised societies, Chatterjee argues, the state arrived first, as a foreign administrative apparatus imposed from outside. The nation had to be defined *against* that state, *through* it, and eventually *in inheritance of* it.

This produces a distinctive postcolonial pattern. Anticolonial nationalist movements were not simply expressions of a pre-existing national community demanding self-rule; they were *constructions* of national community partly by appropriating the colonial state's categories, partly by mobilising vernacular cultural resources, and partly by inventing new institutional and ideological forms. The "Indian nation" that the Congress claimed to represent was not a primordial fact that the British had merely suppressed; it was a project that the colonial state's territorial unification, administrative integration, and bureaucratic categorisation had made thinkable in the first place.

When independence came, the new states inherited the administrative apparatus, the territorial boundaries, and many of the categories that the colonial state had imposed. The "nation" they were now formally representing was therefore in unstable conversation with state forms it had not designed and that did not originally exist to serve it. This is the source of the productive tension that Chatterjee's work keeps returning to — a tension that does not exist in the same form in European nationalism.

## Why this matters in policy

For postcolonial states, the inherited mismatch between colonial state form and indigenous social reality is not a transitional problem that recedes with time. It is an ongoing feature of policy work — visible in language policy, in legal codification, in territorial administration, and in the recurring question of whether received institutional forms genuinely fit the societies they are meant to serve.
