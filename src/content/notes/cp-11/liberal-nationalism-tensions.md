# Liberal Nationalism: Solidarity versus Liberty

A second tension inside liberal nationalism is between *national solidarity* — the felt obligation toward fellow nationals that makes redistributive taxation, democratic compromise, and mutual aid feel natural — and *individual liberty* — the protected sphere within which individuals can hold dissenting views, live unconventional lives, and refuse to share in the common project.

Both matter. A society with no solidarity dissolves into a competitive scramble: tax bases erode, public services starve, democratic give-and-take collapses. A society with no liberty becomes oppressive: the demands of solidarity get used to police speech, identity, and lifestyle. Liberal nationalism's working bet is that the right mix produces a society that is generous enough to redistribute and tolerant enough to dissent.

The mix is not stable. In times of external threat or rapid social change, solidarity tends to demand more — sometimes more than liberalism can comfortably give. Calls to suspend habeas corpus, to restrict speech in the name of national security, to police cultural deviation as betrayal, all draw on solidaristic intuitions. In quieter times, the liberty side recovers, often at the cost of weaker public commitments.

A serviceable liberal nationalism keeps the conversation going rather than picking a side. It maintains visible institutions that everyone is allowed to participate in (solidarity); it protects the right to opt out, dissent, or live differently (liberty); and it accepts that the boundary between the two will be argued over indefinitely.

## Why this matters in policy

Welfare-state design, free-speech doctrine, anti-discrimination law, and emergency powers are the visible places where this trade-off gets implemented. Recognising both values as legitimate is what makes argument over the details a normal politics rather than an existential crisis.
