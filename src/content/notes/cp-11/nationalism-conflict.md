# Nationalism and Conflict

Nitin Pai's essay characterises some contemporary nationalist movements as the "flash mobs of class warfare" — episodic, energetic mobilisations around real underlying grievances, but without the deep organisational scaffolding that sustained political projects need to last. The image is borrowed from the early internet's flash-mob phenomenon: large numbers of people convening briefly in a single place, doing one dramatic thing together, then dispersing.

The argument has two halves. The first is that contemporary nationalist mobilisation often draws on real material discontent — economic insecurity, status anxiety, perceived loss of position by groups that used to count on rising prospects. Class and economic grievance are doing more of the work in modern nationalism than the cultural rhetoric on the surface admits. The second is that the *form* of the mobilisation has changed: where twentieth-century nationalist movements built party structures, mass memberships, and durable institutions, twenty-first-century versions are increasingly platform-driven, episodic, and structurally light. They convene, peak, and disperse on faster cycles.

The combination produces a distinctive pattern of conflict. Mobilisations are intense while they last; they leave less institutional residue than older mass movements; and their underlying class grievances tend to remain unresolved, ready to be mobilised again by the next entrepreneur with a sufficiently grabby narrative. Pai is not optimistic that flash-mob nationalism produces lasting change for the groups whose grievances it mobilises — the dispersal usually leaves the underlying conditions intact.

## Why this matters in policy

Reading nationalist conflict as partly class-driven changes the policy response. Cultural-policy responses to mobilisations driven by economic dislocation tend to miss the source. Honest engagement with the underlying class question — labour markets, regional inequality, public services — tends to be more effective, if slower, than either suppression or symbolic accommodation.
