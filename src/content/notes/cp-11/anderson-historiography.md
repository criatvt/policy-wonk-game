# Anderson on Creole Nationalism

One of Anderson's more provocative claims is that the original nationalisms did not appear in nineteenth-century Europe but earlier and elsewhere — in the Spanish and Portuguese colonies of the Americas. The standard textbook genealogy puts the French Revolution at the start of the story and treats the rest of the world as catching up. Anderson reverses the order. The first generation of modern nationalist movements, in his telling, came out of creole elites — locally-born settlers of European descent — who organised independence movements in places like Venezuela, Mexico, Argentina, and Brazil between the 1770s and 1820s.

The mechanism, in Anderson's argument, was bureaucratic. Spanish colonial administrators were rotated through specific districts (audiencias). Creole functionaries, by law, could not be transferred up the imperial career ladder; their careers ran along the borders of their colonial district and stopped there. This pattern of administrative confinement, combined with shared print-newspapers within the district and a shared sense of grievance against metropolitan rule, produced bounded imagined communities defined by the colonial map. When independence movements arrived, the units they fought for were not pre-existing nations but these administrative provinces. The shape of nineteenth-century Latin America is essentially the shape of late Spanish colonial administration.

The implication is that nationalism is *modular* — a piece of cultural technology that gets invented in one place and then ported to others, often with different ingredients producing similar political results.

## Why this matters in policy

The creole story is a useful reminder that the boundaries of nations are often the boundaries of administrative units that happened to be there. Many postcolonial states inherit borders that were drawn by colonial cartographers for unrelated reasons and then become objects of intense national feeling. Reading those boundaries as administrative artefacts rather than primordial truths can defuse some of the politics around revising them — or, where revision is impossible, around governing across them.
