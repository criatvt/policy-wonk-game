# State and Nation Mismatch

A distinct kind of postcolonial puzzle is that colonial borders were not drawn with national communities in mind. They were drawn for administrative convenience, in negotiation between European powers, or as the residue of military advance. The states that inherited those borders therefore contain multiple national communities packed inside a single boundary, single national communities split across multiple boundaries, or both at once.

The familiar examples are striking. The Durand Line splits Pashtuns between Afghanistan and Pakistan. Colonial-era borders in Africa cut across hundreds of pre-colonial nations and group together populations with no shared political history. Partition in 1947 split Punjab and Bengal and produced two states each containing populations that had not previously imagined themselves apart. The borders of much of Latin America trace the administrative units of late Spanish colonial rule rather than any prior cultural community.

The consequence is durable political work. States have to manage the internal diversity that the borders produce, which can be done well (constitutional federalism, language rights, minority safeguards) or badly (suppression, expulsion, denial of plural identity). They also have to manage the cross-border populations of co-ethnics in neighbouring states, which can produce irredentist demands, diaspora politics, or quiet diplomatic complications.

The mismatch is not transitional. Eighty years after independence in much of the postcolonial world, the borders remain, and so does the political work of governing across them.

## Why this matters in policy

Constitutional design and diplomatic posture both have to account for the mismatch directly. Federalism, accommodation of plural identities, and bilateral arrangements with neighbours that have shared populations are not optional add-ons; they are the basic equipment of governing the postcolonial state form. Pretending that the borders match the nations they enclose leads to predictable, recurring failures.
