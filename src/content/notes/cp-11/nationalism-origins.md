# Origins of Nationalism

Anderson dates the origin of nationalism to the late eighteenth century, and locates the engine in the slow displacement of Latin by vernacular print. For most of European history, Latin had been the language of the church, the courts, the universities, and the printed book — a sacred ceiling above a babble of spoken dialects. Once printers shifted to vernaculars in pursuit of larger markets, three things changed at once. Literate elites in different regions started reading the same books in the same standardised version of their everyday tongue. The print-vernacular fixed itself in place while the spoken language kept drifting. And the new readers began to imagine themselves as belonging to a community of fellow readers — bounded, horizontal, secular.

The argument deflates a lot of nationalist self-description. National identity is not the awakening of a primordial spirit; it is what happens when commercial print finds a vernacular market and a critical mass of literate readers. The story is contingent, not destined: had print arrived in a different sequence, or under different political constraints, the boundaries of "nations" would have been drawn elsewhere. Anderson's framing also helps explain why nationalism appears almost simultaneously across radically different societies in the nineteenth century — they are all running variants of the same modular cultural technology.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy choices about which language a state prints in, broadcasts in, and educates in are not neutral administrative decisions. They are the same machinery that produced nineteenth-century nationalism, still operating. States that ignore this end up surprised when their language policy turns into an identity politics.
