# Liberal Nationalism

Liberal nationalism is the position that the bonds of national community can be reconciled with the liberal commitment to individual rights, democratic representation, and limited government. Its leading defenders — Yael Tamir, David Miller, Neil MacCormick — argue that a nation is one of the few units within which the demanding solidarities of democracy actually work: redistributive taxation, military service, mutual aid in crisis. Strip away the national community and you are left with a thinner liberalism that has more trouble explaining why citizens owe each other anything.

The liberal nationalist accepts both halves of the bargain. On the national side: identifiable belonging, shared political culture, a sense of common destiny that makes redistribution and democratic give-and-take feel like family business rather than charity. On the liberal side: individual rights against the state, equal protection regardless of group identity, freedom of speech and association, and explicit accommodation of minorities within a constitutional framework.

The synthesis is not easy. It requires that national feeling be strong enough to motivate solidarity but not so strong that it suppresses internal dissent or minority cultures. It requires institutions that visibly belong to all citizens — not just to the dominant ethnic or linguistic group. And it requires the political class to keep affirming both halves of the bargain even when one of them would be temporarily more convenient.

## Why this matters in policy

Liberal nationalism is the working assumption behind a lot of welfare-state design and constitutional liberalism in mid-sized democracies. When the national framing slips, public support for redistribution tends to slip with it; when it strengthens too far, minority rights come under pressure. Maintaining the balance is partly a matter of constitutional design and partly a matter of routine political practice.
