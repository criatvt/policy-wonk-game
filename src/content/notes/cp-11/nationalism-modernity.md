# Nationalism and Modernity

A useful framing for nationalism is that it is not a freestanding ideology but one corner of a broader bundle that came of age in the long nineteenth century. Industrialisation moved populations off the land and into cities, where they needed to read signs, follow timetables, and recognise themselves in print. Mass schooling produced literate citizens able to consume newspapers and absorb a standardised national curriculum. Secular calendrical time replaced sacred time as the default mental clock. Bureaucracy expanded the reach of the state into life events — births, marriages, deaths, taxes — that had previously belonged to family, village, and church.

Nationalism slots into this bundle. It supplies a horizontal solidarity that earlier sources of meaning — religion, dynastic loyalty, local custom — had stopped providing on their own. Industrial workers in Manchester or Mumbai needed a story about who they were that didn't depend on a village they'd left. Conscript armies needed soldiers who would fight for "the nation" rather than for a feudal lord. Mass democracies needed a "people" to be sovereign. Modern bureaucracies needed citizens uniformly legible to the state.

The point is not that any of these single factors produced nationalism on its own. It is that they pulled in the same direction and made the imagined national community a useful answer to several different problems at once.

## Why this matters in policy

When societies undergo similar bundles of change today — rapid urbanisation, mass schooling, mobile-internet literacy — they tend to throw up similar identity-politics aftershocks. Reading current nationalisms as part of a modernity package, rather than as inexplicable atavism, is usually more accurate and more policy-useful.
