# The State as Identity-Maker

A useful supplement to Anderson is the observation that, in most modern societies, the state itself is the single most active producer of national identity. Nationalism imagines the state as the political *expression* of a pre-existing nation. The empirical pattern is closer to the reverse: states use their administrative reach to construct, standardise, and propagate the very national identity they claim to represent.

The instruments are familiar. Schooling — compulsory, standardised, in an official language — gives generations of children a shared mental map of the country. Census operations sort the population into categories (caste, religion, language, tribe) which then harden into political identities of their own. Public broadcasting reaches villages where private media wouldn't, and shapes the standard idiom of national speech. Official languages get hardwired into courts, civil-service exams, signage, and currency, structurally privileging some communities over others. Holidays, public commemorations, monuments, and national days frame the shared calendar.

Partha Chatterjee and others writing in postcolonial contexts emphasise that this process is especially visible — and especially contested — in states that inherited their bureaucratic apparatus from colonial rulers. The state arrived first; the "nation" it was supposed to represent had to be assembled afterwards, often using categories the colonisers had introduced. Modern Indian, Pakistani, Indonesian, and Nigerian nation-building all bear marks of this sequence.

## Why this matters in policy

Two consequences follow for policy. First, identity politics in plural societies is rarely a spontaneous outpouring; it is usually a response to particular administrative choices about whom to count, in which language, under which categories. Second, when those choices change, the politics of identity changes with them — often slowly, sometimes abruptly. Treating identity as a fixed input to policy misses how much of it is actually an output.
