# Nations versus Ethnic Groups

It is worth keeping the analytic distinction between a nation and an ethnic group clear, because in popular usage they are often run together. An *ethnic group* is a cultural category — people connected by some combination of shared language, religion, descent, ritual practice, or sense of common origin. A *nation* is a political project — a population claiming, or being mobilised toward, self-rule within a territorial state. The two concepts overlap (most nationalisms draw on ethnic markers) but they are not the same.

The reason this matters is that real populations don't sort cleanly. Most states contain multiple ethnic groups; many ethnic groups straddle multiple states. The Kurds are an ethnic group spread across Turkey, Iraq, Iran, and Syria, with a nationalist movement but no state. The Swiss are a nation made up of speakers of four different official languages, with no single Swiss ethnic identity in any thick sense. India is a nation that absorbs hundreds of ethnic, linguistic, and caste identities under a shared political roof.

Civic nationalisms (about which there is more in the liberal-nationalism notes) explicitly try to pull nation and ethnic group apart — defining national membership through political participation and constitutional principle rather than descent. Ethnic nationalisms run the other way, treating ethnicity as the necessary basis for national membership. The choice between these framings is one of the more consequential decisions a constitutional order can make.

## Why this matters in policy

Citizenship law is the visible place where the nation/ethnicity choice gets implemented. Jus sanguinis (citizenship by descent) leans toward an ethnic conception; jus soli (citizenship by birthplace) and naturalisation paths lean toward a civic one. The choice has long-run consequences for who gets to be a full participant in national life.
