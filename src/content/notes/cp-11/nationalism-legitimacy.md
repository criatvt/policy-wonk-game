# Nationalism and State Legitimacy

For most of history, state authority was justified on grounds that did not reference the population: divine right, dynastic inheritance, religious sanction, conquest. Modern states cannot rely on any of these for the long run, and so they have come to rely on a different claim — that they *represent* a national people. The legitimacy formula is roughly: the state has authority because it acts on behalf of the nation, and the nation has identifiable existence as a people united by shared identity and shared political will.

This is a load-bearing claim. It is what lets a state demand taxes from citizens who could refuse, send conscripts to die in distant wars, redistribute income across regions, and enforce uniform laws across diverse populations. Without the legitimating myth of national representation, each of these acts would look like power exercised on uncooperative subjects rather than power exercised by a self-governing people.

The catch, examined throughout this module, is that the "nation" being represented is itself substantially the state's own creation. Schools, censuses, broadcasters, and bureaucratic standardisation produce the national consciousness that then legitimates the state. This is not necessarily cynical — many of the people doing the work believe in the project — but it does mean the legitimacy is more circular than nationalist rhetoric admits. The nation is invoked to justify the state; the state spends decades constructing the nation that is invoked.

## Why this matters in policy

States in identity-political stress are often, underneath, having a legitimacy crisis. Renewing the national-representation story — through inclusive constitutional design, visible fairness, and credible public institutions — is unspectacular work that pays back over decades. Failing at it, by contrast, manufactures contested authority where there did not need to be any.
