# Internal Dissent within Nationalist Projects

Most nationalist projects have to do their work across populations that are internally diverse. The standard response — one many states have run, with varying degrees of intention and brutality — is to suppress or marginalise groups whose culture, language, or religion does not fit the dominant nationalist narrative. The visible instruments include compulsory schooling in a single official language, restrictions on minority-language media, official religions, and exclusionary citizenship rules. The less visible instruments include selective infrastructure investment, biased recruitment into state services, and the slow attrition of regional cultural institutions.

The predictable result is that the marginalised groups become sources of internal dissent. Linguistic minorities organise to defend their languages. Religious minorities seek constitutional protections. Regional populations who feel left out of the national project either demand fuller inclusion or, in the harder cases, demand separation. The very project of building a homogeneous national community manufactures the diversity-based political conflict it set out to overcome.

There is no neat way around this. The genuinely accommodating alternative — federalism, multilingualism, recognised minority rights, plural citizenship arrangements — is harder to design, slower to consolidate, and less emotionally satisfying for majority nationalists. But the empirical record across the twentieth century is that suppression-based nation-building produces durable internal conflict; accommodation-based nation-building produces less. The countries that have remained stable through deep diversity (India for most of its history, Switzerland, Canada) are mostly accommodation models. The states that have fragmented (Yugoslavia, the USSR, Sudan) are mostly suppression models that ran out of road.

## Why this matters in policy

Constitutional design choices made decades earlier carry forward into the day-to-day politics of dissent. Federalism, language rights, religious accommodation, and minority safeguards are slow-acting policy instruments whose absence shows up as crisis.
