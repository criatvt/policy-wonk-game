# State Capacity in Sovereignty

Krasner's working account of *why* states routinely violate sovereignty norms while maintaining the rhetoric of sovereign equality is straightforward: they prioritise strategic interests. When the cost of a violation is low and the benefit is real, the violation happens. When the cost is high and the benefit is marginal, the principle holds. Sovereignty is, in this sense, more like a default norm that gets adjusted under pressure than an inviolable rule.

This is not a cynical reading. It is empirically descriptive. Strong states impose conditions on weaker ones because they can, because the conditions matter to them, and because the international system does not seriously punish them. Weaker states accept the conditions because the alternative — isolation, sanctions, loss of access — is worse. Mid-sized states pick their battles, standing firm on issues that matter to them and accepting constraints elsewhere. The system runs on this strategic calculus, and the legal language of sovereignty is the vocabulary in which the calculus gets conducted.

The corollary is that sovereignty is *negotiated* in practice. The terms of the negotiation depend on the relative capacity of the parties — economic, military, diplomatic, ideational — and on the specific issue at hand. A state with strong technological capacity can resist constraints on its technology policy that a state without that capacity has to accept. A state with strong domestic political consensus can hold firm on sensitive issues where a fragmented state would yield. State capacity, in many forms, is what determines where on the sovereignty spectrum any particular state actually sits.

## Why this matters in policy

For policy practitioners, the implication is that the sovereignty rhetoric in any specific negotiation tracks the underlying capacity balance more than the formal principle. Building capacity — fiscal, technological, military, diplomatic, regulatory — is what eventually lets a state defend the sovereignty positions it cares about.
