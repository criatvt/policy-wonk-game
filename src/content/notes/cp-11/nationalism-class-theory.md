# Marxism's Trouble with Nationalism

Marxist political theory has had a long-running difficulty with nationalism. The core Marxist expectation is that workers across countries share more interests with each other than with their own ruling classes, and that political consciousness will eventually arrange itself along class lines rather than national ones. The historical record has stubbornly refused this prediction. From 1914 onward, when European socialist parties voted for their own national governments' war credits, nationalism has consistently outperformed class as a mobilising frame.

Anderson treats this puzzle directly. Nationalism, he argues, is not the same kind of thing as liberalism or socialism. It is closer to religion or kinship: a structure of meaning and belonging rather than a political programme with explicit content. Class-based politics is asking workers to identify with a category defined by their economic position; national politics is asking them to identify with a community defined by shared horizontal fellowship. The second is, empirically, the stronger pull for most people most of the time.

Pai's contribution is to note that class hasn't *disappeared* — it has slipped under the surface of nationalist mobilisation. Workers, displaced middle classes, and squeezed regional elites still mobilise around class interests, but increasingly within nationalist frames that make those interests politically respectable. The result is a kind of class politics that doesn't speak in class language and a kind of nationalism that is doing more economic work than it admits.

## Why this matters in policy

For policy analysts, the practical takeaway is to treat the class/nationalism layering as the default rather than as a strange exception. Most political mobilisations have both layers. Reading them apart improves diagnosis; responding to only one layer almost guarantees the other will keep generating fresh political weather.
