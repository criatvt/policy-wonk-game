# Westphalian Sovereignty

The doctrine commonly called Westphalian sovereignty has two main planks. First, the world is organised into territorial states. Each state exercises supreme authority within its territory; there is no higher political authority above it. Second, states do not interfere in each other's internal affairs. The principle of non-interference is the international corollary of territorial supremacy: each state's internal arrangements are its own business.

The doctrine is conventionally traced to the 1648 Peace of Westphalia that ended the Thirty Years' War in central Europe. Historians of international law tend to dispute how much of "Westphalian" sovereignty the Westphalia treaties actually contained — the modern interpretation does a lot of retrospective tidying — but the label has stuck, and the substantive principles became canonical in international politics during the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries.

The intellectual appeal of Westphalian sovereignty is that it offers a clean answer to who has the right to do what to whom internationally: the territorial state has authority over its territory, and other states have no business interfering. The empirical appeal is that the principle helps reduce the kind of constant religious and dynastic intervention that produced enormous instability in early-modern Europe.

Krasner's project, which the rest of these notes work through, is to show that the doctrine is consistently violated in practice — and that the gap between the principle and the practice is a structural feature of the international order, not a bug.

## Why this matters in policy

Westphalian sovereignty is the default vocabulary of international diplomacy. Knowing both the doctrine and its routine violations is what lets practitioners read international negotiations accurately. Pretending sovereignty is absolute leads to surprise when it gets breached; pretending it is meaningless leads to surprise when states stand on it.
