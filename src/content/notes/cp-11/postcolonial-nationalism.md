# Postcolonial Nationalism

Postcolonial nationalism — the strand that emerged in Asia, Africa, the Caribbean, and parts of the Pacific during the long twentieth-century retreat of European empire — has features that distinguish it from the European nationalisms Anderson and his successors initially worked on. The most important is that it developed under conditions of *contestation with a foreign state*. The nationalist movement was simultaneously trying to construct a sense of national community among the colonised and to wrest sovereignty from the coloniser.

This shaped postcolonial nationalism in several ways. The category of "the people" was defined partly against the coloniser — a counter-identity formed in opposition rather than in unmediated continuity with pre-colonial communities. The institutional architecture the movement aimed at was usually the colonial state, slightly redesigned, taken over rather than rebuilt from scratch. Legal codes, civil services, military structures, schooling systems, and administrative geographies were inherited with modest revision. The territorial boundaries of the new nation were typically the boundaries the coloniser had drawn — boundaries that frequently did not match pre-colonial communities and that often grouped multiple peoples together or split single peoples apart.

The result is a postcolonial state that is fully sovereign in the international sense, fully nationalist in its self-description, and yet permanently in conversation with the colonial inheritance it cannot wholly disavow. Anti-colonial rhetoric remains current decades after independence because the inherited frame keeps producing reasons to invoke it.

## Why this matters in policy

Recognising that postcolonial nationalism inherits its main institutional vocabulary from the coloniser changes the policy frame in important ways. Reform of legal codes, administrative structures, and inherited bureaucratic categories is not a betrayal of national identity; it is one of the unfinished tasks of decolonisation. Treating inherited forms as if they were the nation's own can obscure how much room there actually is to redesign them.
