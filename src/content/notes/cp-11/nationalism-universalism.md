# Nationalism and Universal Values

Modern liberal-national rhetoric has a recurring move: it claims that the nation in question is a particular vessel for *universal* values. The French Republic claims to instantiate the universal Rights of Man. The American republic claims to embody liberty and self-government for everyone. Post-1947 Indian nationalism claims pluralism, secular democracy, and constitutional dignity as universal goods rendered in a particular national form. Each of these positions tries to bridge the universal-particular gap by saying that *this* national community is the historical carrier of values that, in principle, belong to everyone.

The bridge is intellectually attractive because it lets the nationalism feel proud without feeling parochial. The catch is that other nations are simultaneously claiming the same thing in their own idiom. France's universalism, America's universalism, India's universalism, China's universalism — all are at least partly self-flattering descriptions of particular national projects. From the outside, the universal claim shrinks back into a particular cultural inheritance dressed up as a gift to humanity.

A more honest liberal nationalism makes a weaker, sounder claim: the nation is a *workable scale* for universal commitments. The values themselves do not belong to the nation; the nation is the scale at which a community has organised institutions capable of honouring them. The institutions are particular and contingent; the commitments they implement aspire to be universal.

## Why this matters in policy

This framing helps with foreign policy. It distinguishes between defending universal values in international fora (a legitimate move) and exporting one's particular national institutions as if they were universal (often counterproductive). It also makes domestic policy more honest: claiming universal warrant for particular national arrangements is a rhetorical move, and rhetorical moves can be examined.
