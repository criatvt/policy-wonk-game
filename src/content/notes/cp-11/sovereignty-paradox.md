# The Sovereignty Paradox

The central paradox Krasner draws out is that absolute sovereignty is structurally incompatible with participation in any genuinely interconnected international system, and yet states continue to maintain the rhetoric of absolute sovereignty *while* participating fully in such a system. The two cannot both be true; both are nonetheless asserted; and the discrepancy is one of the durable features of how international politics actually works.

The mechanics are straightforward. Trading internationally requires accepting that contracts and disputes will be decided under some rule-set that has authority over the state's commercial actors. Borrowing internationally requires accepting that creditors will impose conditions, formally or informally. Joining the United Nations requires accepting the Charter, including provisions that allow the Security Council to authorise collective action against member states. Joining the WTO requires accepting binding dispute-settlement procedures. None of these commitments is consistent with the strict Westphalian principle that no authority sits above the territorial state, yet states sign up to them anyway because the alternative — autarky — is too costly.

The paradox is managed rather than resolved. States use the rhetoric of sovereignty when they want to push back against international constraints, and lean on international rules when they want to discipline the behaviour of others. The vocabulary is selectively deployed. From outside, the inconsistency is obvious. From inside any particular state's foreign-policy machinery, the rhetorical flexibility is just how the work gets done.

## Why this matters in policy

Diplomatic practice depends on knowing when sovereignty rhetoric is operative and when it is decorative. International agreements that look like clean cessions of sovereignty often contain carefully drafted escape hatches, and apparent assertions of absolute sovereignty usually come with quiet acceptance of international constraints elsewhere. Reading these moves accurately is much of what working diplomats actually do.
