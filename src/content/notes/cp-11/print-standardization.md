# Print Standardisation

Before mass print, European vernaculars existed as a shifting continuum of dialects: Picard blurred into Norman, Norman into Parisian French, Parisian French into Occitan in the south. Speech communities were local; mutual intelligibility decayed with distance. Print changed this in a quiet, mechanical way. A publisher in Paris or London could not afford to typeset a book in every dialect; the economic logic of print drove standardisation toward whichever variant gave the largest market.

Anderson treats this as a contingent process rather than a planned one. No king sat down and decided that Parisian French would become French; the market did. Once a variant became the print-variant, several things followed. Schools taught it. Civil services used it. Newspapers entrenched it. Literate elites in distant provinces learned to read and write a language increasingly different from what they spoke at home. Within two generations, the printed vernacular started to feel like *the* national language, with regional speech repositioned as dialects of it — sometimes celebrated as colour, sometimes deprecated as backwardness.

The deflating insight is that the boundaries of national languages — and therefore, often, of nations themselves — were drawn by printers chasing readers, not by historical destiny. That French is one language and Italian another, that Hindi and Urdu are separate but mutually intelligible, that Mandarin is now the standardised reference for a huge family of Chinese vernaculars — these are outcomes of which printing centres got there first and which scripts they used.

## Why this matters in policy

Language policy in plural states inherits this history. Deciding which vernacular becomes the medium of instruction, the language of courts, the script for government forms is an act of nation-building whether or not it is announced as one. Multilingual states (India, Switzerland, Belgium, South Africa) handle this with explicit compromises; monolingual policies in plural societies tend to produce predictable minority grievances.
