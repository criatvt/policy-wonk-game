# Nationalism

Nationalism is the political doctrine that the world is naturally divided into nations and that each nation is entitled to govern itself. Stated baldly it sounds either obvious or absurd depending on the listener. The harder question — the one the readings in this module worry about — is what a "nation" is in the first place, and where these things came from.

The unsatisfying answer that nationalists themselves usually give is that nations are old, primordial, and given by history. Their language, their attachments, their shared past go back to time out of mind. The unsatisfying answer the careful historian gives is that almost none of this is true. Benedict Anderson's *Imagined Communities* opens by noting that nation-ness is among the most universally legitimate political values of our time and yet has no plausible grand theory; the great political philosophers — Hobbes, Locke, Marx, Weber — wrote almost nothing about it. The thing is colossally powerful and intellectually thin.

Anderson's own move is to treat nationalism not as an ideology like liberalism or socialism but as a *cultural artefact* — closer in kind to religion or kinship than to political doctrine. Once you treat it that way, the explanatory question becomes when and how this kind of imagined attachment to large groups of strangers became thinkable. His answer points to the late eighteenth century, to the printing press, to the standardisation of vernacular languages, and to a new way of imagining time itself. The rest of the module unpacks that argument and tests it against postcolonial and liberal alternatives.

## Why this matters in policy

Whether nations are primordial or constructed is not just a historical curiosity. It changes what a government can plausibly do. If national identity is given, the state's job is to protect it. If national identity is made — by schools, by language policy, by media, by ceremonies — the state is one of the principal makers, and any policy that touches culture, education, or citizenship is doing nation-building whether it intends to or not.
