# Politics & Society

This module is about the political units that hold modern life together — nations and states — and the often awkward fit between the two. The core puzzle is that the nation-state feels timeless and natural to the people who live inside one, but is in fact a recent, contingent invention. The readings work through this puzzle from several angles.

Benedict Anderson treats nations as imagined communities, brought into being by print capitalism, vernacular standardisation, and the modern habit of imagining strangers reading the same newspaper at the same time. Yael Tamir and other liberal nationalists ask whether the bonds of national community can be reconciled with universal rights and democratic citizenship. Partha Chatterjee, writing from a postcolonial standpoint, asks what happens when a state form inherited from colonial rulers has to claim authority over a nation it didn't create. Stephen Krasner picks at the concept of sovereignty itself and finds it riddled with predictable, structural violations — a legal fiction that everyone agrees to maintain even as everyone routinely breaks it. Nitin Pai's essay on the "flash mobs of class warfare" brings the argument home: nationalist movements often dress up class interests in the language of unified national feeling.

Topics in this module:

- **Nations as imagined communities** — Anderson, print capitalism, vernacular standardisation, and the cultural roots of nationalism.
- **Liberal nationalism** — the attempt to reconcile national feeling with individual rights, and the tensions that surface when you try.
- **Postcolonial nationalism and the state** — Chatterjee on inherited colonial state forms and the contested authority they carry.
- **Sovereignty in theory and practice** — Krasner on the gap between what states claim and what they actually do.
- **Class, ethnicity, and the politics of nation-building** — how nations get built across the diversity that already exists inside their borders.
