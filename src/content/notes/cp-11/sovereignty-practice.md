# De Jure and De Facto Sovereignty

A useful distinction for thinking about real-world sovereignty is between *de jure* sovereignty — the legal recognition by other states that this state is sovereign over its territory — and *de facto* sovereignty — the actual ability to control what happens inside that territory. The two come apart routinely.

Many internationally recognised states have weak or partial control over their own borders, peripheries, or capital cities. Government writ ends at the edge of the city; remote provinces are administered by tribal authorities, armed groups, or no one at all; large parts of the economy operate outside any tax or regulatory system. De jure, these states are sovereign in international fora; de facto, their sovereignty is full of internal blank spaces. Somalia's central government during long stretches of its post-1990s history is a familiar example; less spectacular but real versions exist in many states with insurgencies, weak peripheries, or contested capital regions.

The reverse pattern also occurs. Some entities exercise effective control over a territory and population but lack international recognition — Taiwan, Somaliland, parts of historical Kosovo, the de facto authorities of various separatist regions. They have *de facto* sovereignty without the *de jure* component.

Krasner uses this distinction to make a sharper point: sovereignty is not a single thing that a state either has or doesn't have. It is a bundle of related properties — legal recognition, control over territory, control over flows across borders, freedom from external interference in domestic policy — and any actual state has some subset of these in varying degrees.

## Why this matters in policy

Treating sovereignty as a bundle rather than a single property is what lets analysts diagnose what is actually wrong in a state that "isn't working." The legal status may be intact while the on-the-ground capacity is collapsing, or vice versa. Different problems call for different responses, and the bundle decomposition helps you tell which problem you are looking at.
