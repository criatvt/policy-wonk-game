# The Paradox of Postcolonial Sovereignty

Chatterjee's central paradox: postcolonial states inherit European state forms — bureaucratic administration, codified law, territorial boundaries, central command of force — while claiming to represent indigenous nations whose pre-colonial authority structures often did not work that way. The state apparatus and the nation it speaks for fit awkwardly together because they came from different sources at different times.

The fit problems show up in characteristic places. Pre-colonial South Asian political authority, for instance, did not operate through fixed territorial sovereignty in the European sense; it operated through layered, overlapping, and graduated claims of allegiance, tribute, and protection. When the British imposed a unified administrative state on this layered landscape, they imposed a European political form. The Indian Republic inherited that European form and now exercises authority through it, while invoking a national identity whose deeper cultural sources predate, and operate differently from, that form.

This is not a defect to be fixed; it is a structural condition of postcolonial politics. The state form is here to stay — exiting from it is not a real option — but the friction between it and the society it administers keeps generating political work. Subaltern movements demanding recognition of customary practices, federalist demands for regional autonomy, debates over personal law in plural societies, and disputes over the place of religion in public administration are all aspects of this productive friction.

## Why this matters in policy

Chatterjee's paradox suggests that policy design in postcolonial states needs to be deliberately conscious of the fit between received state forms and the social realities they administer. Off-the-shelf institutional designs, often imported from European or American models, tend to fit poorly in ways that take a long time to diagnose. Building in flexibility — federal arrangements, plural personal law, customary rights, devolved decision-making — tends to ride the friction better than insisting on uniformity.
