# Critiques of Liberal Nationalism

Liberal nationalism's critics come from several directions. The empirical critique is that the model — strong civic identity coexisting with deep ethnic, religious, or cultural diversity — sounds elegant on paper but is hard to find in practice. Plural societies that try to operate this way (Belgium, Canada, India, Spain) repeatedly run into communal conflict, secessionist movements, and constitutional crises. The critics argue that liberal nationalism underestimates how much shared culture is actually doing the work of holding a society together, and how brittle civic ties are when stretched across communities that don't share much else.

A second, more philosophical critique is that liberal nationalism is a *compromise formation* that satisfies neither side. Strict liberals object that any concession to national particularity is a concession too far: rights are universal or they are not. Strict nationalists object that diluting national identity with liberal universalism produces a thin civic shell that nobody actually feels deeply enough to die for. Both sides accuse liberal nationalism of trying to have it both ways.

A third critique notes that liberal nationalism, in practice, often privileges the dominant national community while claiming to be inclusive. The "civic" framework turns out to be the cultural inheritance of the largest group, repackaged as neutral. Minorities are asked to assimilate to that framework in the name of civic equality. From inside the minority, the liberal-national bargain looks less like inclusion and more like a softened majoritarianism.

## Why this matters in policy

The critiques sharpen up the policy work. If liberal nationalism is going to function in plural societies, its institutions have to be visibly fair to all communities — not merely formally neutral. Visible fairness usually requires positive accommodations: minority-language rights, group-based protections, federal structures, and political conventions that all communities recognise as theirs.
