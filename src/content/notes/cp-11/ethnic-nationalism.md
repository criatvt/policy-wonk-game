# Ethnic Nationalism

Ethnic nationalism is the strand of nationalism that grounds national membership in shared ancestry, descent, or cultural inheritance. To be part of the nation is, on this view, to be born into it: through shared blood, language, religion, or some bundle of these. People who don't share these markers can live within the territory and may be tolerated, but they are not fully *of* the nation in the strong sense.

The intuitive appeal of ethnic nationalism is that it offers a clear answer to who belongs. The cost is that it is structurally exclusive. In any plural society — which is to say almost every real society — some part of the population will lack the relevant ethnic markers, either because their ancestors arrived more recently, or because they belong to a religious minority, or because they speak a different mother tongue, or because the ethnic boundary has been drawn so as to leave them outside. Ethnic nationalism gives this group the status of guests, second-class citizens, or worse.

The contrast with civic nationalism (see the separate note) is sharp. Civic nationalism asks for adherence to a political project; ethnic nationalism asks for the right ancestry. The two often coexist in the same country in unstable mixtures, and the public arguments people have about citizenship, immigration, and minority rights are usually arguments about how the balance should sit.

## Why this matters in policy

The ethnic-civic distinction maps onto concrete policy choices: citizenship law, immigration policy, naturalisation procedures, school curriculum, and recognition of minority languages and religions. Constitutions that lean ethnic tend to produce predictable minority politics and, in stressed conditions, predictable communal violence. Constitutions that lean civic ask more of their citizens — adherence to political principles rather than easy descent-based belonging — but tend to weather diversity better.
