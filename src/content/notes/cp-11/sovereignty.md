# The Hole in the Whole: Krasner on Sovereignty

Stephen Krasner's contribution to the literature is to take the standard doctrine of Westphalian sovereignty seriously enough to look for where it gets violated, and to discover that it gets violated almost everywhere, almost all the time, in patterned ways. His phrase "the hole in the whole" captures the position: sovereignty is treated as a complete and inviolable principle, while in practice it is full of holes — areas where states interfere in each other's internal affairs, accept external authority over their territory, or have their formal sovereignty constrained by international institutions.

Krasner's catalogue of routine violations is long. Powerful states impose conditions on weaker ones through aid, loans, or trade agreements that reach deep into domestic policy. International financial institutions require structural-adjustment policies. Human-rights treaties commit states to enforce standards their domestic politics did not produce. Bilateral investment treaties give foreign investors the right to sue host governments in international tribunals. Military interventions justified as humanitarian protection set aside non-interference. Even ordinary diplomatic immunity is a partial cession of territorial sovereignty.

Krasner's point is not that sovereignty is fake. It is that sovereignty is a *legal fiction* that everyone agrees to maintain even as everyone routinely breaks it. The fiction is useful: it provides a default vocabulary, a starting point for negotiation, and a baseline expectation against which violations stand out. But the practical reality of how international politics works can only be understood by paying close attention to where, how, and why the violations happen.

## Why this matters in policy

Foreign-policy practitioners who treat sovereignty as absolute end up underprepared for the routine violations they actually face. Practitioners who treat it as meaningless end up surprised when a state stands firmly on it. Krasner's stance — sovereignty as durable fiction, routinely breached in predictable places — is the working middle ground that most practical international policy operates inside.
