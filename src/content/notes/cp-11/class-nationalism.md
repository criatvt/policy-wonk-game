# Class and Nationalism

Beneath the cultural surface of most nationalisms, class is doing more work than either nationalists or their critics typically admit. Pai's framing is that nationalist movements can become *vehicles for class conflict* — vehicles in which workers, displaced middle classes, or threatened elites mobilise around their material interests using the language of shared national feeling rather than the language of class. The cultural framing is often more politically usable than the class one: it draws a wider tent, it lets the leadership avoid explicit redistributive promises, and it disguises material conflict as cultural unity.

The pattern recurs in several historical settings. Late-Weimar German nationalism was, among other things, a mobilisation of economically anxious middle classes around a story of national betrayal. Mid-twentieth-century anticolonial nationalisms blended genuine national feeling with peasant land hunger and labour grievances. Contemporary right-populist movements in many countries combine cultural anxieties about identity with very specific economic grievances about manufacturing decline, housing costs, and stagnant wages.

This is not a debunking. The grievances are real, the cultural attachments are real, and people are not naive to be moved by both. The analytical point is that reading a nationalist movement only through its cultural rhetoric will routinely miss what is actually driving it. The reverse is also true: reading it only through class will miss the genuine cultural and identitarian content. Both layers operate, and they reinforce each other.

## Why this matters in policy

If you respond to a movement that is really about wages and housing by offering only cultural-policy gestures, you don't address the source and the movement returns. If you respond to one that is really about identity by offering only redistribution, you don't address the source either. Reading the actual mix matters.
