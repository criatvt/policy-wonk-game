# National Competitiveness as a Confused Concept

The frame of "national competitiveness" — the idea that nations compete with each other the way firms compete in a market, and that some nations are winning while others are losing — has been criticised by economists for at least three decades. Paul Krugman's 1994 essay "Competitiveness: A Dangerous Obsession" argued that the metaphor was conceptually confused and practically harmful. The criticism stands up reasonably well.

Firms compete in a zero-sum sense: when one firm wins a contract, the other firms lose it. Profits are at stake; market share is at stake; firms can go out of business. Nations are not like this. When one country grows, other countries do not generally lose. International trade is positive-sum: both partners gain from voluntary exchange. The success of South Korea didn't come at the expense of the US or vice versa; it came partly from US-Korean trade that benefited both.

The "national competitiveness" frame conflates the firm-level zero-sum dynamic with the national-level positive-sum dynamic, and produces several confusions. It treats trade balances as scores in a competition (we're "losing" if we have a deficit) rather than as reflections of macroeconomic conditions. It treats foreign success as threat (their growth means our loss) rather than as opportunity (their growth means more demand for our exports). It treats industrial policy as a competition to subsidise the right sectors before other countries can subsidise theirs, rather than as a domestic question about which sectors are worth public investment.

The empirical evidence supports the economists' view here. Countries that have grown most rapidly have generally not done so at the expense of their trading partners. Periods of broadly shared global growth (post-WWII expansion, the 1990s and 2000s) have been periods when most countries grew, not periods of zero-sum jostling. Periods of global stagnation or contraction (the 1930s, the 2008-9 recession) hurt most countries simultaneously.

## Why this matters in policy

The competitiveness frame leads to policy mistakes. Tariffs justified by competitiveness rhetoric usually fail at the stated goal because the gains from trade are positive-sum rather than zero-sum. Industrial policy justified by competitiveness rhetoric often subsidises politically connected sectors rather than the genuinely high-spillover sectors. Honest policy analysis tracks the underlying positive-sum dynamics rather than treating trade partners as adversaries.
