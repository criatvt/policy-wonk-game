# Trade and Poverty

One of the most consequential empirical findings of the past four decades is that trade-led growth has reduced global poverty dramatically. China's manufacturing boom alone lifted several hundred million people out of extreme poverty. Vietnam, Bangladesh, Indonesia, and other Asian economies that pursued export-led growth showed similar (though smaller in absolute numbers) poverty reductions. India's growth, accelerated after the 1991 reforms, contributed to a substantial reduction in extreme poverty even with the country's specific challenges. The aggregate global poverty rate (people living on less than $1.90 per day) fell from roughly 36% in 1990 to under 10% by 2019, with most of the reduction occurring in countries that opened to international trade.

The mechanism by which trade reduces poverty is straightforward. Labour-intensive export industries create jobs for workers who otherwise have limited options. Bangladesh's garment industry employs roughly four million people, mostly women, who earn wages far above what they could earn in subsistence agriculture. China's manufacturing boom drew hundreds of millions of rural workers into higher-productivity urban jobs. The cumulative effect on household income, when sustained over years, transforms life expectations for the workers and their families.

The qualification is that trade does not automatically reduce poverty in every country that opens to it. Several African countries opened trade in the 1990s and 2000s without seeing comparable poverty reductions, often because the supporting infrastructure (transport, ports, electricity, governance) wasn't there to make their exports competitive at scale. Trade is necessary but not sufficient for export-led poverty reduction.

The within-country distribution of poverty reduction is also uneven. Workers in export sectors and their family members typically benefit substantially. Workers in non-tradable sectors (much of rural agriculture, informal services) benefit indirectly and more slowly through the broader economic growth. The very poorest, who often have limited connection to the formal economy entirely, may benefit least and slowest unless explicit social-protection measures reach them.

## Why this matters in policy

For countries with substantial poverty and labour-abundant economies, trade openness is a major lever for poverty reduction. The lever has to be paired with supporting investments (infrastructure, education, basic services, social protection) to make the gains reach the very poorest. The pure trade-liberalisation approach reduces poverty for labour-intensive-export workers; broader poverty reduction requires complementary policies.
