# Trade and Income Distribution

A specific theoretical prediction about the distributional consequences of trade comes from the Stolper-Samuelson theorem, derived in the 1940s. The result says that international trade benefits the *abundant* factor of production in each country and harms the *scarce* factor. In a country that is relatively labour-abundant (like much of South Asia), trade with capital-abundant countries should raise the relative return to labour and lower the relative return to capital. In a country that is relatively capital-abundant (like the US or Western Europe), the same trade should raise the relative return to capital and lower the relative return to labour.

The intuition: when a labour-abundant country opens to trade, it specialises in labour-intensive exports, which increases demand for labour at home. The increased demand raises wages. Meanwhile, the country imports capital-intensive goods rather than producing them domestically, which reduces demand for domestic capital and lowers the return to capital. The result is a shift in the income distribution toward labour.

The empirical record largely confirms the Stolper-Samuelson prediction, with qualifications. Trade with developing countries has put downward pressure on wages for less-skilled workers in developed countries (consistent with developed countries being relatively scarce in low-skill labour). Trade has raised demand for skilled labour in developing countries that have moved into manufacturing and services (consistent with these countries being relatively abundant in the relevant labour types). The magnitude of the effects is contested — automation and technology change are also doing substantial work — but the direction is broadly consistent with the theory.

The policy implication is that trade has distributional consequences that go beyond which sectors gain and lose. The same trade liberalisation can produce different distributional patterns in different countries depending on factor endowments. In labour-abundant countries, trade tends to be relatively pro-poor (because the poor mostly own labour). In capital-abundant countries, trade tends to be relatively pro-rich (because the rich own more of the capital that gains). Trade's reputation as inequality-reducing vs inequality-increasing depends partly on which side of the factor-endowment line the country sits.

## Why this matters in policy

Distributional analysis of trade liberalisation has to take factor endowments into account. Programmes designed in capital-abundant countries (where trade widens inequality) often need additional progressive measures to share the gains. Programmes designed in labour-abundant countries (where trade narrows inequality, at least within the formal sector) may need fewer compensating measures but more support for those left outside the labour-intensive sectors that benefit.
