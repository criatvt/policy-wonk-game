# Trade & Specialisation

This module is about the economics and politics of international trade. The core economic claim — that voluntary exchange between countries makes both better off, even when one country is absolutely more productive than the other — is among the more durable results in economics. Ricardo's argument from the 1810s on comparative advantage still holds: each country should specialise in what it has the lowest opportunity cost of producing, and trade for the rest.

The empirical record is mostly consistent with the theory. Countries that opened up to international trade in the second half of the twentieth century (East Asian "tigers," post-1991 India, post-1978 China) grew faster than those that remained closed. Trade has lifted hundreds of millions of people out of poverty. Consumer access to variety and quality across the world is unimaginable from a 1950s baseline.

The political record is messier. Trade liberalisation produces *aggregate* gains but distributes those gains unevenly. Workers in import-competing industries bear concentrated losses; consumers benefit from cheaper goods in diffuse, hard-to-mobilise ways. The political math repeatedly produces backlash, calls for protection, and cycles between liberalisation and retreat. The economic case for free trade is robust; the political case requires constant rebuilding.

Topics in this module:

- **Comparative advantage and specialisation** — Ricardo's insight, its scope, its dynamic limits, and what global supply chains have done to it.
- **Gains from trade** — for consumers, producers, and workers, with attention to how the gains get distributed.
- **Tariffs, quotas, and other barriers** — how they work, what they cost, and why they persist.
- **Trade policy choices** — multilateral agreements, regional blocs, the WTO, the difficult politics of liberalisation sequencing.
- **Critiques of free trade** — environmental, labour-standards, infant industry, race-to-the-bottom arguments — and what the evidence says about each.
- **Trade and development** — how export-led growth has worked for some countries and not others.
