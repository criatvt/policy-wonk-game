# Division of Labour

Adam Smith's pin-factory observation is the founding image: ten workers, each specialising in one operation, produce vastly more pins per day than one worker doing every operation alone would produce. The mechanism has three parts. Specialists develop greater skill in their narrow task through practice. Specialists avoid the time and friction of switching between operations. Specialist tools, tailored to one operation, become economical at scale where they would be impractical for generalists. The cumulative effect is productivity many times higher than a generalist workforce.

The pin-factory image scales. The modern global economy is a vast distributed division of labour. Workers in different countries specialise in components of value chains spanning continents: chip design in California, semiconductor manufacturing in Taiwan, electronics assembly in China, distribution from Singapore, sales worldwide. Each step is a narrow specialisation; the cumulative output is products and services no single country could produce alone.

The cost of division of labour is interdependence. Specialists cannot supply their own non-specialty needs; they have to trade for them. The system functions as long as trade functions. When trade breaks — through pandemics, wars, trade-policy reversals, transport disruption — the specialised producers are vulnerable in ways that less-specialised ones aren't. The COVID-era supply-chain crises made this visible: deeply specialised producers found themselves unable to obtain inputs from upstream suppliers, and unable to switch quickly to alternatives.

The trade-off between specialisation gains and resilience risks is one of the more important policy questions in the modern global economy. Pure efficiency optimisation pushes toward deeper specialisation and longer supply chains. Resilience concerns push back toward shorter, more redundant supply chains with some domestic capability. The right balance depends on the specific products, the risks, and the cost of maintaining redundancy.

## Why this matters in policy

The COVID experience pushed many countries toward partial "decoupling" — onshoring or near-shoring certain critical supply chains, building domestic stockpiles, diversifying away from single-source suppliers. The efficiency cost of these moves is real; the resilience benefit is also real. The policy work is to identify which sectors genuinely need the resilience investment and to avoid the temptation to apply the same logic to every sector indiscriminately.
