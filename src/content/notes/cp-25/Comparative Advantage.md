# Comparative Advantage

Comparative advantage is the cornerstone result in trade economics. The claim, due to David Ricardo in the early nineteenth century, is that two countries can both benefit from trade if each specialises in the good for which it has the lowest opportunity cost — *even when one country is absolutely more productive than the other at producing every good*.

The intuition is counter-intuitive but mathematically clean. Imagine England can produce a unit of cloth in 100 hours or a unit of wine in 120 hours. Portugal can produce cloth in 90 hours or wine in 80 hours. Portugal has absolute advantage in both: it uses fewer hours per unit for cloth (90 < 100) and for wine (80 < 120). The naive intuition is that England should produce nothing and Portugal everything. The Ricardian insight is that the *opportunity cost* differs: in England, one unit of wine costs 120/100 = 1.2 units of cloth; in Portugal, one unit of wine costs 80/90 ≈ 0.89 units of cloth. Portugal's opportunity cost of wine is lower; Portugal has *comparative advantage* in wine. England, by contrast, has lower opportunity cost in cloth (100/120 ≈ 0.83 units of wine per unit of cloth, vs Portugal's 90/80 = 1.125). England has comparative advantage in cloth.

If each country specialises in its comparative-advantage good and they trade, both end up with more cloth and more wine than they would have produced in autarky. The total output of the two-country system rises. Specialisation according to comparative advantage is the source of the gains.

The result generalises to many countries and many goods. It is the foundation of the case for international trade as a positive-sum activity, and it explains why countries with apparently weaker economies still benefit from participating in global trade rather than retreating to self-sufficiency.

## Comparative Advantage: Static and Dynamic

The Ricardian comparative-advantage argument has one important limitation: it is *static*. The argument tells you what each country should produce given its current set of capabilities, resources, and technology. It does not tell you anything about whether the current capabilities are the ones the country *should* have, or whether specialising in them now might lock the country out of higher-value sectors later.

The dynamic critique of comparative advantage runs like this. Country X has current comparative advantage in low-skill, labour-intensive manufacturing — say, basic garments. Specialising according to current comparative advantage means producing more garments and importing higher-value goods. But the *capabilities* required to produce higher-value goods — engineering know-how, supply-chain coordination, technology development, sophisticated manufacturing — typically develop through the doing of more sophisticated production. By specialising in garments, the country doesn't develop those capabilities, and stays stuck in low-value activities indefinitely. The static comparative advantage prevents the dynamic capability development that would have made higher-value advantage possible.

This is the developmental-state critique of free-trade orthodoxy. East Asian economies — Korea, Taiwan, Singapore — did *not* simply specialise according to their static comparative advantage in cheap labour. They used industrial policy (selective tariffs, subsidised credit, public investment in technology, education investments) to push capabilities into progressively higher-value sectors. By the time they were globally competitive in semiconductors, automobiles, or shipbuilding, they had built capabilities that pure free trade would not have produced.

The counter-argument is that targeted industrial policy is hard to do well. Picking winning sectors requires information governments don't usually have. Protected industries often become rent-seekers rather than competitive players. The successful East Asian cases may be exceptions enabled by specific conditions (capable bureaucracies, geopolitical alignment with the US, export discipline) that other countries don't share.

### Why this matters in policy

Both sides have force. The pure static case for free trade ignores capability development. The pure dynamic case for industrial policy assumes a state capacity that often isn't there. Practical trade policy in middle-income countries tends to use elements of both: substantial openness to capture the gains from trade, combined with targeted public investment in capabilities (education, infrastructure, applied R&D) that gradually shift comparative advantage upward.

## Why this matters in policy

Trade-policy debates often centre on absolute advantage ("we can't compete with their cheap labour"). The comparative-advantage frame redirects the analysis: the question is not whether you are absolutely better, but whether the opportunity cost of producing something domestically exceeds the cost of importing it. The answer is frequently yes, and the gains from trade follow from acting on that calculation.
