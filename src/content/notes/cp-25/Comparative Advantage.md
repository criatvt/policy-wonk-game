# Comparative Advantage

Comparative advantage is the cornerstone result in trade economics. The claim, due to David Ricardo in the early nineteenth century, is that two countries can both benefit from trade if each specialises in the good for which it has the lowest opportunity cost — *even when one country is absolutely more productive than the other at producing every good*.

The intuition is counter-intuitive but mathematically clean. Imagine England can produce a unit of cloth in 100 hours or a unit of wine in 120 hours. Portugal can produce cloth in 90 hours or wine in 80 hours. Portugal has absolute advantage in both: it uses fewer hours per unit for cloth (90 < 100) and for wine (80 < 120). The naive intuition is that England should produce nothing and Portugal everything. The Ricardian insight is that the *opportunity cost* differs: in England, one unit of wine costs 120/100 = 1.2 units of cloth; in Portugal, one unit of wine costs 80/90 ≈ 0.89 units of cloth. Portugal's opportunity cost of wine is lower; Portugal has *comparative advantage* in wine. England, by contrast, has lower opportunity cost in cloth (100/120 ≈ 0.83 units of wine per unit of cloth, vs Portugal's 90/80 = 1.125). England has comparative advantage in cloth.

If each country specialises in its comparative-advantage good and they trade, both end up with more cloth and more wine than they would have produced in autarky. The total output of the two-country system rises. Specialisation according to comparative advantage is the source of the gains.

The result generalises to many countries and many goods. It is the foundation of the case for international trade as a positive-sum activity, and it explains why countries with apparently weaker economies still benefit from participating in global trade rather than retreating to self-sufficiency.

## Why this matters in policy

Trade-policy debates often centre on absolute advantage ("we can't compete with their cheap labour"). The comparative-advantage frame redirects the analysis: the question is not whether you are absolutely better, but whether the opportunity cost of producing something domestically exceeds the cost of importing it. The answer is frequently yes, and the gains from trade follow from acting on that calculation.
