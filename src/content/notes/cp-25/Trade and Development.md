# Trade and Development

Trade has been one of the central mechanisms through which developing countries have grown rapidly in the post-WWII era. The standard mechanism is *export-led growth*: countries that build manufacturing or services export capacity find that the foreign-market demand can support production at scales the domestic market alone could not. The result is rising employment, rising wages, rising productivity, and a virtuous cycle of capability development.

The standard cases are well-known. South Korea moved from a poor agrarian economy in 1960 to a high-tech industrial economy by 2000, mostly through export-led growth in successively more sophisticated manufacturing. Taiwan and Singapore followed similar paths. China, after the late-1970s reforms, grew at rates the world hadn't seen since the Industrial Revolution, driven heavily by export-led manufacturing. Vietnam, Bangladesh, and several other Asian economies are in earlier phases of similar trajectories.

The mechanism is more than just "exports rose." Export-led growth typically involves several mutually reinforcing features. Foreign-market scale supports investment in larger production facilities than the domestic market could justify. Competition with sophisticated foreign buyers forces continuous improvement in quality, cost, and capability. Foreign direct investment brings technology, management know-how, and connection to global supply chains. Currency earnings from exports finance imports of capital goods and inputs that domestic capability didn't yet produce.

The qualification is that export-led growth is not automatic. Many countries have pursued similar policies without achieving the same outcomes. The successful cases have shared certain features: capable bureaucracies that could coordinate the policy effort, political stability that supported long-term investment, and supportive geopolitical context (notably US market access for early East Asian exporters during the Cold War). Countries without these features have often run trade-policy programmes that looked similar to East Asian success cases but produced more modest results.

## Why this matters in policy

For developing countries, trade is generally part of any plausible growth strategy. The right mix — how much to open, how much to protect, in what sequence — depends on the country's specific endowments and capabilities. Pure free trade tends to lock in current comparative advantage; pure protectionism tends to lock in inefficiency. The successful cases have generally combined gradual opening with deliberate capability investment, calibrated to the country's actual position rather than to ideological prescription.
