# Trade Barriers

Governments restrict trade through several distinct instruments. *Tariffs* are taxes on imports. They raise the domestic price of imported goods, reduce import volumes, generate revenue for the government, and partially protect domestic producers. *Quotas* are quantitative limits on imports: a fixed maximum quantity that can enter the country per period. They restrict supply directly rather than raising prices, but the effect on the domestic market is similar — higher prices, less competition, partial protection. *Non-tariff barriers* are a broader category: regulatory requirements, certification standards, customs procedures, technical specifications, and other administrative hurdles that raise the cost of importing without explicitly limiting quantity or applying tax.

Each instrument has distinct welfare consequences. Tariffs transfer some surplus from consumers to producers (via higher prices), some to the government (via tariff revenue), and produce deadweight loss from forgone transactions. Quotas transfer surplus from consumers to producers and to whoever holds the quota allocations (importers who happen to have allocations gain windfall profits), with similar deadweight loss. Non-tariff barriers can produce similar effects but typically with less transparency and more administrative cost; the welfare loss often goes to compliance overhead rather than to anyone in particular.

From a purely efficiency standpoint, tariffs are usually preferred over quotas (the revenue at least goes somewhere productive) and over non-tariff barriers (the costs are clearer and the system is more transparent). From a political-economy standpoint, however, non-tariff barriers are often preferred by domestic producers because they are harder to negotiate away in trade agreements and easier to apply selectively. Many of the most binding contemporary trade restrictions are non-tariff barriers (rules-of-origin requirements, sanitary and phytosanitary standards, certification rules) rather than headline tariffs.

The cumulative effect of multiple barriers can be substantial. A sector with a 10% tariff, a 5% quota-rent on imports, and 5% effective non-tariff barrier costs faces about 20% effective protection compared to free trade. The components are often invisible individually but bind together.

## Why this matters in policy

Effective trade-liberalisation reform has to address all the barrier types, not just headline tariffs. Many post-WTO tariff cuts have been partially offset by rising non-tariff barriers, leaving the actual openness less changed than the headline numbers suggested. Honest measurement of openness has to include the full barrier stack.
