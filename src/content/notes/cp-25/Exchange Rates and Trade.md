# Exchange Rates and Trade

The exchange rate is the price at which one currency trades for another. It is the mechanism through which domestic prices translate to international prices and vice versa. When the rupee weakens against the dollar — fewer rupees per dollar before, more rupees per dollar after — Indian exports become cheaper to American buyers (the same product costs the same in rupees but fewer dollars), and American imports become more expensive to Indian buyers (the same dollar product costs more rupees).

The implications for trade flows are direct. A weaker domestic currency boosts exports (cheaper for foreign buyers) and depresses imports (more expensive for domestic buyers). A stronger domestic currency does the opposite. Over time, exchange-rate movements rebalance trade flows in response to underlying macroeconomic conditions — countries with trade deficits tend to see their currencies weaken (under flexible exchange rates), which moves the balance back toward equilibrium.

Exchange-rate policy is therefore a partial substitute for trade policy. A country that wants to boost exports can do so by depreciating its currency (intentionally or by letting market forces do the work) rather than by trade barriers on its trading partners. Many countries have used exchange-rate management for this purpose at various times: the East Asian "tigers" maintained relatively undervalued currencies during their export-led growth periods; China was widely accused of exchange-rate management during the 2000s; the US has periodically tried to weaken the dollar to boost exports.

Fixed and floating exchange-rate regimes have different trade implications. Fixed regimes (currencies pegged to a major currency) provide predictability for trade but require the country to align its monetary policy with the anchor country's, limiting domestic flexibility. Floating regimes allow domestic monetary independence but produce exchange-rate volatility that complicates trade contracts. Most large economies operate floating regimes; many smaller ones operate managed floats or pegs.

## Why this matters in policy

Exchange-rate policy interacts with trade policy in important ways. Trade liberalisation under an overvalued currency produces disappointing results because exports remain uncompetitive at the inflated exchange rate. Trade liberalisation under a market-determined or modestly undervalued currency produces the export response the policy was supposed to generate. Aligning exchange-rate policy with trade-policy intent is part of competent macroeconomic management.
