# Global Value Chains

Modern international trade is increasingly *trade in stages of production* rather than trade in finished goods. A smartphone designed in California has components manufactured across many countries: displays from Korea, processors from Taiwan, memory from Japan, sensors from various European specialists, assembly in China, and final distribution worldwide. Each step is a stage in a global value chain (GVC), and each country participates in the stage where it has comparative advantage.

The GVC pattern has changed several things about trade. First, comparative advantage now operates at the *stage* level rather than at the *whole-industry* level. A country isn't comparative-advantage in "smartphones" as a finished product; it's comparative-advantage in display manufacturing, or in chip packaging, or in final assembly. The granularity of specialisation is finer.

Second, GVCs have made it easier for developing countries to enter manufacturing. Rather than needing to develop the capability to make complete products, they can specialise in a single stage (often labour-intensive assembly), which requires much less capability to start. Bangladesh's garment industry, Vietnam's electronics assembly, and India's pharmaceuticals all illustrate this pattern.

Third, GVCs have made the world economy more interconnected. A disruption at any stage of a long value chain can cascade through every downstream stage. The COVID-era supply-chain disruptions made this visible: shortages of specific components rippled through final-product availability across many sectors.

Fourth, GVCs have changed the politics of trade. Old-style trade was visible: imports of finished products competed with domestic production of finished products. GVC trade is partly hidden: imports of components feed into domestic production of finished products. Tariffs on intermediate goods raise the cost of domestic production rather than protecting it; tariffs on finished goods can be partly evaded by importing components and finishing locally. Trade policy designed around finished-goods imports is increasingly mismatched with how trade actually works.

## Why this matters in policy

Modern trade policy has to think in GVC terms. Tariffs that look like protection for finished goods may actually punish domestic manufacturers who use imported inputs. Industrial policy aimed at developing domestic capability needs to identify which stages of which value chains are realistic targets given current capabilities. Trade negotiations increasingly focus on the rules governing GVC operation — rules of origin, intellectual property, services trade, and digital flows — rather than on the headline tariffs of finished goods.
