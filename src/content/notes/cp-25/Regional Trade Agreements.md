# Regional Trade Agreements

Regional trade agreements — the European Union, NAFTA/USMCA, ASEAN, Mercosur, the African Continental Free Trade Area, and many bilateral arrangements — have proliferated over the past three decades. Each lowers tariffs among the member countries while maintaining tariffs with non-members. The pattern is meant to deepen trade among members and is widely seen as a complement (or sometimes a substitute) for slower-moving multilateral liberalisation.

Economic analysis of regional agreements identifies two effects, both important. *Trade creation* is the gain from members trading more with each other because the lower internal tariffs make that trade economical. Members gain access to each other's markets and consumers gain from increased competition within the bloc. *Trade diversion* is the loss from trade shifting away from efficient non-member producers toward less efficient member producers. A regional agreement that effectively makes member-country goods cheaper than equivalent goods from non-members (because of the tariff differential) can cause trade to flow to less efficient producers within the bloc, reducing aggregate welfare.

Whether a regional agreement is net beneficial depends on the balance of trade creation and trade diversion. When members are natural trading partners with substantial existing complementarity, the trade creation tends to dominate. When members are similar economies with limited complementarity and the non-members include important efficient producers in relevant sectors, trade diversion can dominate. The empirical record across many agreements is mixed; most produce net trade creation, but some (especially among similar-economy member countries) have produced substantial trade diversion.

A second consideration is whether regional agreements complement or substitute for multilateral liberalisation. Some economists worry that the proliferation of regional agreements has reduced political pressure for multilateral progress — countries get the trade openness they want through bilateral and regional arrangements and lose interest in the multilateral negotiations. Others argue that regional agreements provide a path forward when multilateral progress is stalled and that the cumulative regional liberalisation is preferable to no liberalisation at all.

## Why this matters in policy

Countries negotiating regional agreements have to think carefully about partner selection (look for genuine complementarity), rules-of-origin design (to limit trade-diversion effects), and the integration with multilateral commitments. Well-designed regional agreements can produce substantial gains. Poorly designed ones can produce trade diversion that costs members more than they gain.
