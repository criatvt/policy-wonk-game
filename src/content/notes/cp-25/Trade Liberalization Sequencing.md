# Trade Liberalisation Sequencing

When and how fast to liberalise trade is one of the harder design questions for developing countries. Liberalising too quickly can overwhelm domestic industries before workers, capital, and infrastructure can shift to expanding sectors. Liberalising too slowly can entrench protected industries politically against further reform. Both extremes have failed in well-documented cases. The right pace is a judgement call that depends on the specific context.

The fast-liberalisation school points to evidence that gradual reform programmes give protected industries time to lobby for continued protection, drag out the reform process, and capture the political system. Rapid reform — "shock therapy" — disorients the protected interests and gets the openness in place before the political-economy resistance can re-organise. The successful Polish and Czech transitions from communism (rapid liberalisation in the early 1990s) and some Latin American reform episodes (Chile in the 1970s, Argentina in the 1990s) are cited as evidence.

The gradual-liberalisation school points to evidence that fast reform produces disruptive adjustment that destabilises political support for the reform itself. The Indian gradualism from 1991 onwards, the Chinese gradual opening from 1978, and the East Asian export-led growth trajectories all show that gradual, sequenced liberalisation can produce sustained growth without the dramatic political backlash that fast liberalisation has often produced. The cases of rapid liberalisation that failed badly (Russia in the 1990s, several Latin American reform programmes that were politically reversed) suggest that the disruptive-adjustment cost is real.

The contemporary consensus is closer to gradualism with some sequencing rules. Liberalise inputs before outputs (so domestic producers can use cheaper imported inputs to compete). Liberalise capital goods early (to support investment in the new export sectors). Build adjustment infrastructure before liberalising affected sectors. Sequence with macroeconomic stability (exchange-rate management, fiscal balance) so that the trade openness can deliver its gains rather than getting absorbed by macroeconomic disruption.

## Why this matters in policy

Sequencing is partly art and partly engineering. The art is in reading the political-economy and institutional context to identify what pace the system can absorb. The engineering is in identifying the specific liberalisation steps and supporting policies that should accompany them. Done well, the sequencing produces a sustainable trajectory; done badly, it produces backlash or stagnation.
