# Terms of Trade

A country's *terms of trade* is the ratio of the prices of its exports to the prices of its imports. Improving terms of trade means each unit of exports buys more units of imports — the country's purchasing power on the world market has risen. Deteriorating terms of trade means the reverse: each unit of exports buys less imports, so the country has to export more to maintain the same level of imports.

Terms of trade are driven by global prices, which depend on world supply and demand for the goods involved. A commodity exporter sees improving terms of trade when commodity prices rise relative to manufactured-goods prices (oil exporters in the 1970s, mineral exporters in the 2000s). The same commodity exporter sees deteriorating terms of trade when commodity prices fall relative to manufactured-goods prices (much of the 1990s for primary-product exporters).

Long-run trends in terms of trade have been a subject of academic debate. The *Prebisch-Singer hypothesis*, advanced in the mid-twentieth century by Latin American development economists, argued that primary-product prices tend to fall over time relative to manufactures, producing structurally deteriorating terms of trade for primary-product exporters. The empirical evidence on this is mixed; it has held over some periods and not others. But the policy implication of the hypothesis — that primary-product exporters should diversify into manufacturing — drove substantial industrial-policy effort in Latin America, Africa, and parts of Asia during the late twentieth century.

The contemporary picture is more nuanced. Terms of trade for commodity exporters move with commodity-price cycles, which themselves track global demand and supply factors. Terms of trade for manufacturing exporters depend on the specific manufactures and on competition from other producers. The long-run trends across categories are less uniform than mid-twentieth-century theory expected.

## Why this matters in policy

Countries dependent on commodity exports face terms-of-trade volatility that complicates fiscal planning, balance-of-payments management, and long-run development. Stabilisation policy — building reserves during high-price periods, smoothing fiscal spending across the cycle — partly offsets the volatility. Diversification policy — building export capacity in less volatile sectors — addresses the underlying exposure. Both are appropriate; the right balance depends on each country's specific endowments and capabilities.
