# Tariff Effects

The welfare analysis of a tariff has four parts. *Consumer loss*: the domestic price rises, so consumers pay more per unit and consume fewer units. The total consumer surplus loss is a rectangle (units still bought at higher price) plus a triangle (units no longer bought). *Producer gain*: domestic producers receive the higher domestic price for each unit they sell, and they sell more units. Their producer surplus rises by a smaller rectangle. *Government revenue*: the government collects the tariff on each unit of imports still flowing in. *Deadweight loss*: the net welfare effect is negative because the units that consumers no longer buy and the foreign units that no longer flow in represent transactions that would have been mutually beneficial.

The deadweight loss has two components. One is the *production efficiency loss*: domestic units produced by less efficient domestic producers (under the umbrella of the tariff) cost more to produce than the imports they replaced would have cost — the resources used in inefficient domestic production could have been used more productively elsewhere. The other is the *consumption efficiency loss*: consumers who would have bought at the world price but cannot afford the protected price lose access to the product, and their forgone consumption is no one's gain.

The size of the deadweight loss depends on elasticities. With elastic demand and supply, the quantity changes substantially in response to the price change, and the deadweight triangles are large. With inelastic demand and supply, quantity barely moves and the triangles are small. Tariffs on inelastic-demand goods (necessities) produce relatively small deadweight losses but transfer substantial consumer surplus to producers. Tariffs on elastic-demand goods produce larger deadweight losses.

The tariff revenue is not deadweight loss; it is a transfer from consumers to the government and can in principle be returned to consumers through other channels. The deadweight loss is the *additional* welfare destruction beyond the transfer.

## Why this matters in policy

Tariff design — when and where to apply them — should account for both the transfers (who gains and who loses) and the deadweight losses (the unrecoverable cost). Tariffs that target inelastic-demand goods are often less efficient ways to raise revenue than broader taxes, because they distort behaviour and have hidden welfare costs that don't show up in the headline numbers.
