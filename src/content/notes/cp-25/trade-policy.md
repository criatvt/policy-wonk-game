# India's Trade Policy History

India ran one of the longest and deepest experiments in import-substitution industrialisation (ISI) of any major economy in the post-WWII era. From independence in 1947 through the 1991 reforms, India systematically protected domestic industries from import competition through high tariffs, quantitative restrictions, industrial licensing, foreign-exchange controls, and a wide array of supporting regulations. The intent was to build domestic industrial capacity that international competition would not have allowed to emerge.

The results were mixed. India did build substantial industrial capacity in heavy industry, machine tools, chemicals, pharmaceuticals, and certain consumer goods. Some of this capacity laid the foundation for later success (the pharmaceutical industry became globally competitive after liberalisation; some engineering capabilities transferred to later export sectors). But the cost was substantial: protected industries lacked competitive discipline, innovation lagged, consumer goods quality stayed poor, exports remained modest, and growth was much slower than that of comparable open economies (notably the East Asian "tigers").

The 1991 reforms — triggered by a balance-of-payments crisis — dramatically liberalised trade and investment. Tariffs fell sharply. Quantitative restrictions were dismantled. Foreign investment was opened up. The decades following showed rapid growth, dramatic improvement in consumer welfare, and the emergence of new export sectors (software services, pharmaceuticals, engineering goods) that thrived under the more open framework.

The lessons drawn from the Indian case are widely contested. The market-friendly reading is that ISI was a costly mistake and the liberalisation produced the gains. The structuralist reading is that the ISI period built capabilities that the liberalisation period then exploited — both phases were necessary, and the liberalisation alone wouldn't have succeeded without the earlier capability-building. A middle reading: ISI built some valuable capacity but was sustained too long and protected too much. A few decades of carefully sequenced protection might have built capability without the deadweight loss of the actual programme.

## Why this matters in policy

The Indian case is informative for current debates about industrial policy and partial protection. The case shows that substantial industrial capability can be built under protection — but also that knowing when and how to liberalise is essential to avoid permanent inefficiency. The state capacity required to manage that timing well is significant, and is itself a binding constraint in many developing economies.
