# Trade Policy Synthesis

If there is a pragmatic consensus emerging from the long debate over trade policy, it goes something like this. Substantial openness — most of the economy operating under low tariffs and few quantitative restrictions — captures most of the aggregate gains from trade and avoids most of the entrenched-protection pathologies. The standard recommendation is for tariffs to average in the single digits, with most sectors fully open.

Selective protection makes sense for genuinely strategic sectors: defence-critical industries, public-health-critical industries, technologically strategic industries with substantial spillovers, and infant industries with credible commitment mechanisms to phase out the protection. The selectivity is the key — broad-based protection produces inefficiency without strategic benefit; narrow strategic protection can be defended on grounds other than pure efficiency.

Industrial policy can play a constructive role when it supports capability development rather than protecting incumbents. Investment in education, infrastructure, applied R&D, and supportive regulation can shift comparative advantage upward over decades. Industrial policy that targets specific sectors works better when accompanied by competitive discipline (export benchmarks, automatic phase-outs) than when it simply protects without performance requirements.

Adjustment infrastructure is essential for political sustainability. Trade adjustment assistance, retraining programmes, regional development support, and social insurance for transitions are what allow the political coalition for openness to hold even as some workers and communities bear concentrated losses. Skimping on this dimension produces the backlash that eventually rolls back the liberalisation.

The right combination depends on the country's specific position. Labour-abundant countries with weak institutions might prioritise opening alongside basic adjustment support. Capital-abundant countries with strong institutions might do more on adjustment and selective industrial policy. Middle-income countries shifting comparative advantage might combine openness with deliberate capability investments. There is no single template.

## Why this matters in policy

Trade policy is one of the areas where the academic and the practical have come closer over recent decades. The ideological extremes (pure free trade, pure autarky) have lost ground; the pragmatic combinations have gained credibility. Working trade-policy practitioners increasingly operate within this synthesis, adjusting the specific mix for the specific country rather than applying a uniform template.
