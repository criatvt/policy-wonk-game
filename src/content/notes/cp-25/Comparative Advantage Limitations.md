# Limitations of Comparative Advantage

Comparative-advantage theory predicts that countries should specialise fully in their lowest-opportunity-cost sectors and import everything else. In practice, almost no country actually does this, and there are reasonable reasons why not. The deviation from pure specialisation is not necessarily irrational; it reflects considerations the static efficiency analysis doesn't capture.

*Food security*. Countries with the option to import food cheaply often maintain substantial domestic agricultural production anyway. The reason is that food imports depend on stable international supply, stable shipping routes, and willing trading partners. None of these is guaranteed during conflicts, pandemics, or supply-chain disruptions. Maintaining some domestic food production is essentially an insurance policy against the upside risk of import disruption. It costs efficiency but buys resilience.

*Defence and strategic industries*. Critical defence-related industries — weapons, aviation, semiconductors at the leading edge, certain medicines — are often maintained domestically even when they could be imported cheaper. The reasoning is that imports from potential adversaries or from politically unstable suppliers carry strategic risk. Maintaining some domestic capability protects against that risk.

*Capability development*. Specialising in current comparative advantage may prevent building the capabilities needed for higher-value future production (see the dynamic-comparative-advantage discussion). Some industrial-policy interventions deliberately maintain or grow industries that are not currently comparative-advantage to build capabilities for the future.

*Adjustment costs*. Specialising fully according to comparative advantage means shifting workers and capital out of contracting industries into expanding ones. The transition is rarely smooth — displaced workers face real costs of retraining, relocating, and finding new work. Maintaining some industries that would otherwise contract under pure specialisation can be an explicit choice to slow the pace of adjustment.

*Inequality*. Pure specialisation according to comparative advantage can produce uneven distribution of gains. The Stolper-Samuelson result says that trade benefits the abundant factor (in a labour-rich country, low-skill labour) and harms the scarce factor (high-skill labour, in this case). Some moderation of specialisation can be motivated by distributional concerns.

## Why this matters in policy

Choosing how much to specialise is a real policy choice, not a derivation from efficiency theory. Full specialisation maximises static gains; partial specialisation buys various forms of insurance and accommodates other objectives. The right balance depends on how the country values resilience, security, capability-building, and distributional concerns relative to pure efficiency.
