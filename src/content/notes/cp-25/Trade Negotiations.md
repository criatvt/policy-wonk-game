# Trade Negotiations

International trade negotiations have been one of the central institutional features of the post-WWII economic order. The General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT), established in 1947 and succeeded by the World Trade Organization (WTO) in 1995, has provided the multilateral framework for tariff reduction, dispute settlement, and the establishment of common rules across most of the world's economies.

The big multilateral rounds (the Kennedy Round, the Tokyo Round, the Uruguay Round) progressively reduced tariffs across many sectors and many countries. The Uruguay Round in particular extended the rules into agriculture, services, and intellectual property, and established the WTO as a more institutionalised dispute-settlement body. The result was a substantial reduction in average tariff levels across the world and the consolidation of a rules-based trading system.

The Doha Round, launched in 2001 with the ambition of being a "development round" that would address developing-country priorities (especially agricultural subsidies in developed countries), has effectively stalled. Negotiations have proven much harder as the developed-developing country trade-offs have become more contentious, as services and intellectual property have introduced new lines of conflict, and as the rise of major emerging economies (China especially) has changed the balance of negotiating power.

The result has been a shift away from multilateral negotiations and toward bilateral and regional agreements. The Trans-Pacific Partnership (later CPTPP), the EU's bilateral agreements with many partners, the African Continental Free Trade Area, and bilateral US trade agreements have all become more important than multilateral progress over the past two decades. Each operates on its own terms; the aggregate result is a more fragmented trade-rules landscape.

This fragmentation has both benefits and costs. Bilateral agreements can move faster and accommodate specific bilateral concerns. They can also produce overlap, contradiction, and a "spaghetti bowl" of rules that complicates trade with multiple partners simultaneously. The WTO's continued existence provides a baseline; the regional and bilateral agreements provide variation on top of the baseline.

## Why this matters in policy

Practitioners working on trade policy in the 2020s operate in a substantially different environment than the multilateral-progression era of the 1990s. The skills required have shifted toward managing complex bilateral and regional negotiations rather than primarily working through multilateral rounds. Dispute-settlement work has correspondingly become more important as the multilateral rule-making has slowed.
