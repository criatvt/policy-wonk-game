# Trade and Environment

The trade-environment relationship is mixed and depends substantially on the policies in place around the trade. The simplistic argument that trade is bad for the environment because it increases production and transportation has some empirical support — more economic activity produces more emissions, all else equal. The competing argument that trade is good for the environment because it directs production to the most efficient producers also has some support — efficient producers typically use fewer inputs and produce less waste per unit of output. Both effects are real; the net depends on the specific case.

The "race to the bottom" concern is that international competition pressures countries to lower environmental standards to attract or retain industry. The empirical evidence on this is moderate — some downward pressure exists for specific pollutants and specific industries, particularly where the regulated costs are large fractions of total costs. But the dominant pattern across most sectors and most countries has been the opposite: environmental standards have generally risen over the past three decades, not fallen, even in the face of substantial trade competition.

The "race to the top" mechanism explains part of why. Foreign buyers, particularly in OECD markets, increasingly demand environmental standards from their suppliers. Multinational firms that operate in multiple jurisdictions tend to adopt the higher of the standards across their operations rather than the lower, partly for reputational reasons and partly to standardise practices. International value chains thus transmit higher environmental standards from buyer-country regulators to supplier-country producers, even when the supplier-country regulations are weaker.

The most consequential trade-environment interactions are around climate change. International trade is a vehicle through which the carbon-emissions consequences of consumption in one country fall on production in another. Carbon-border adjustments, where importing countries effectively tax the carbon content of imports, are an emerging policy tool aimed at addressing this. Whether they will work as intended — and whether they will be implemented at scale — is one of the live trade-policy questions of the 2020s.

## Why this matters in policy

Trade-environment policy has to engage both mechanisms — the race to the bottom and the race to the top — rather than assuming one or the other dominates universally. Sector-specific analysis identifies where each mechanism is operating. Policy responses include both domestic environmental regulation (raising the floor where competition pressure is downward) and international cooperation (aligning standards across countries to reduce the competitive penalty for stricter regulators).
