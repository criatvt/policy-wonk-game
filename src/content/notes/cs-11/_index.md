# Strategic Studies

This module is about the discipline of strategy: how states organise military, economic, diplomatic, and informational instruments toward political objectives across long timescales. The core proposition, traceable to Clausewitz and refined across many subsequent thinkers, is that *strategy connects ends to means*. Without political ends, military activity is just operational art. Without aligned means, political objectives are just aspirations. Strategy is the discipline that holds the two halves together.

The module's recurring concerns are the failure modes that emerge when the connection between ends and means breaks. Strategy degraded into logistics and management. Tactics divorced from broader objectives. Military success that undermines the political purpose it was supposed to serve. Institutional inertia that prevents adaptation when conditions change. Each of these has well-documented historical cases — Vietnam, Iraq, Afghanistan, less famously many others — and each illustrates why strategy is harder than the textbook formulations suggest.

Topics in this module:

- **Strategic fundamentals** — what strategy is, how it differs from tactics, what grand strategy adds.
- **Power, national interest, and the international system** — the basic working concepts for thinking about state behaviour.
- **Deterrence and nuclear strategy** — assured retaliation, the stability-instability paradox, second-strike survivability.
- **Balance of power, hegemony, and power transition** — patterns of state competition and the moments when they produce conflict.
- **Strategic culture, adaptation, and innovation** — why states with similar capabilities behave differently and why some adapt while others don't.
- **Soft power, statecraft, and economic instruments** — non-military levers and how they combine with military ones.
- **Asymmetric conflict, alliances, and gray-zone competition** — what state competition looks like below the level of open war.
