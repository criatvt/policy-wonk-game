# Strategic Studies

This module is about the discipline of strategic studies — the systematic examination of how states pursue national security through the integrated use of military, diplomatic, economic, and informational instruments. The field sits at the intersection of international relations, military history, political science, and economics, but it has a distinct preoccupation: the relationship between ends and means in a world where coercion, the threat of coercion, and the avoidance of coercion are persistent features of political life.

The texts in this module are Prakash Menon's CS-1 slide deck (which lays out the basic vocabulary — power, politics, interests, policy, strategy, planning), Lawrence Freedman's essay on strategic studies and the problem of power, Hew Strachan's two contributions — *Strategy and the Limitation of War* and *The Lost Meaning of Strategy* — and a short piece from the Institute of World Politics on statecraft as an integrated set of arts. Across the readings, two themes recur. First, strategy is always political: divorced from political purpose, military activity is violence, not strategy. Second, power is harder to use than to accumulate — the gap between capability and outcome is the discipline's central puzzle.

The module's question bank covers thirty-four topics. They cluster as follows:

- **Foundations** — Strategic Fundamentals, Strategic vs Tactical, Strategic Studies, Strategy and Politics.
- **Power** — Power Resources, Power Transition, Soft Power, Power and Legitimacy, Power and National Interest, Hegemony, Balance of Power.
- **Conflict** — Asymmetric Conflict, Containment, Conflict and Cooperation, Strategic Competition, Strategic Surprise.
- **Deterrence and the nuclear age** — Deterrence, Nuclear Strategy, Second-Strike Capability, Strategic Stability.
- **The strategic toolkit** — Statecraft, Economic Statecraft, Grand Strategy, Military Strategy, Strategic Communications, Strategic Alternatives, Strategic Restraint, Innovation and Strategy, Strategic Adaptation.
- **Other foundational concepts** — Alliance, International System, Strategic Culture, Strategic Interests, Spheres of Influence.

The aim across the notes is not encyclopaedic coverage but enough conceptual grip to think clearly about contemporary strategic problems — India's nuclear posture, the US-China competition, the wars in Europe and West Asia, the cyber and space domains — using the analytical vocabulary the discipline has developed.
