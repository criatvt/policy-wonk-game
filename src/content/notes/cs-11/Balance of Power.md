# Balance of Power

The balance of power is one of the oldest principles in the European diplomatic tradition. The claim is that international stability is best preserved when no single state possesses overwhelming preponderance — when the distribution of capability across major powers is roughly even, or when alliances form to offset any state that begins to look dominant. The mechanism is straightforward: a state contemplating aggression must reckon with the resistance of a coalition formed against it, and the prospective costs of that resistance deter the aggression.

The principle has both a descriptive and a prescriptive form. As description, it is the claim that great powers, observing the rise of a potential hegemon, will balance against it by building up their own capabilities (internal balancing) or by allying with each other (external balancing). The European system from the Peace of Westphalia in 1648 to the First World War is the canonical case: repeated attempts at continental hegemony — Habsburg, Bourbon France, Revolutionary France, Napoleonic France, Wilhelmine Germany — were each met with counter-coalitions that eventually defeated them. As prescription, it is the policy advice that states should consciously practise balancing to avoid being dominated and to keep the system stable.

The theoretical version of the argument is associated with Kenneth Waltz and the structural-realist tradition. Waltz argued that anarchy in the international system — the absence of a higher authority — pushes states toward balancing as a survival imperative, and that balance is therefore a recurring feature of international politics, not a contingent choice. Other scholars have noted that bandwagoning — joining the strongest side rather than balancing against it — also occurs, particularly among smaller states with limited options, and that balancing is more reliable among major powers than at the system's periphery.

The contemporary application is the question of how the rise of China will affect the international system. Will the United States, Japan, India, Australia, and others form a balancing coalition against Chinese power? Will smaller Asian states bandwagon, balance, or hedge? The answers shape the geometry of twenty-first century strategic competition. Hegemonic-stability theorists, by contrast, argue that a single dominant power is actually a more stable arrangement than a balanced multipolar one, since the dominant power sets and enforces rules that reduce uncertainty.

A complication for the theory is the role of nuclear weapons. The classical balance of power assumed that aggression could potentially be defeated militarily; nuclear arsenals change this calculation. Among nuclear-armed states, even a state with overwhelming conventional power cannot translate that power into a politically usable threat against another nuclear-armed state. The balance that matters has become nuclear-strategic, not purely conventional.

## Why this matters in policy

For Indian strategy, the balance-of-power frame is the natural lens through which to view the Asian order. The post-Cold War unipolar moment is over; the question is whether the next system is bipolar (US-China), tripolar, or genuinely multipolar (with India, Japan, and others as independent poles). India's choices on alliances, partnerships, and capability investments are bets on which configuration will produce the most security and the most autonomy.
