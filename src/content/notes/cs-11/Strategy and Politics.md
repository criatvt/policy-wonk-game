# Strategy and Politics

The foundational claim of modern strategic thought is Clausewitz's: war is the continuation of politics by other means. The sentence appears in *On War* in slightly different formulations, but the core idea is consistent. War is not an autonomous activity governed by its own internal logic; it is one means among others by which states pursue political objectives, and it is properly evaluated by whether it advances those objectives. Military activity divorced from political purpose is violence, not strategy.

The corollary, equally important, is that political authority is properly the master of military activity. The political principal defines the objective; the military professional advises on whether and how military means can serve the objective and then conducts operations to achieve it. The relationship is hierarchical, with politics on top, and this hierarchy is the basis of civilian control of the military in democratic states and of the broader principle that strategy serves policy rather than the other way around.

The relationship between strategy and politics is not always smooth. Several characteristic problems recur. First, the political objective may be unclear or contradictory, leaving military professionals to invent objectives by inference. Second, the political principal may demand military options without first clarifying the political objective those options are meant to serve, producing operational activity in search of strategic purpose. Third, military operations once begun can produce momentum and constituencies of their own that pull policy in directions the original political authorities did not intend — what Eliot Cohen has called the "supreme command" challenge of maintaining political direction through the duration of operations. Fourth, military advice may be inadequate or misleading, leading political principals to make commitments based on flawed assumptions about what military means can deliver.

Hew Strachan's *Lost Meaning of Strategy* dwells extensively on the contemporary version of these problems. Strachan argues that the post-Cold-War period saw a particular kind of confusion in Western governments, in which the language of strategy proliferated while the substance of strategy — the disciplined connection of political ends to military and other means — eroded. Politicians delegated strategic thinking to defence professionals, who properly insisted on clear political objectives but did not get them; defence ministries produced "strategies" that were really plans, lacking the integrative judgment that strategy requires. The result was significant military activity — operations in the Balkans, Iraq, Afghanistan, Libya — without the strategic coherence that would have made the activity productive.

The Iraq War is a particularly studied case. The political objective of the 2003 invasion was inconsistently articulated — variously about weapons of mass destruction, regime change, the spread of democracy in the Middle East, the broader response to terrorism. The military operation that produced the rapid fall of the Saddam regime was tactically and operationally accomplished. But the absence of a clear political objective, and the resulting absence of an end-state for the occupation, produced a strategic situation in which there was no criterion for success or failure — only ongoing activity that consumed enormous resources without producing the political outcomes that would have constituted strategic victory.

Eliot Cohen's *Supreme Command* (2002) examined the question from the other direction, looking at successful war leadership — Lincoln, Clemenceau, Churchill, Ben-Gurion — and finding that the common feature was an active "unequal dialogue" between political authority and military professionals. The dialogue was unequal because civilian authority was ultimate, but it was a dialogue because military advice was taken seriously, questioned, probed, and integrated into political judgment. Strategy emerged from the conversation. Where the conversation broke down — either through political deference to military professionalism or through political dictate that ignored military reality — strategy degraded.

Strategy and politics are inseparable not only in theory but in practice. The political conditions of the moment shape what strategy can plausibly attempt: a democracy facing low public support for casualties has different strategic options than one facing existential threat; a state with a divided political system has different options than one with unified leadership. The "domestic politics" of strategy is its own field of study, and the failure to integrate domestic political constraints into strategic analysis is a recurring source of strategic disappointment.

## Why this matters in policy

For Indian policy, the strategy-politics relationship has its own particular shape. The civil-military relationship has historically been characterised by deference of military professionals to civilian authority — a strength in terms of democratic stability but sometimes a weakness in terms of the substantive dialogue that produces good strategy. The recent institutional reforms — the CDS, the Department of Military Affairs, the more explicit articulation of doctrine — are attempts to create the conditions for the Cohen-style dialogue that produces strategic coherence. The test will be whether the institutions actually generate the dialogue, or whether the formal arrangements persist alongside continued informal patterns of inadequate strategic conversation.
