# Strategic Adaptation

Strategic adaptation is the capacity of a state to revise its strategy in response to changing circumstances — new threats, new opportunities, changed capabilities, new information about the strategic environment. The capacity is not automatic. Strategies that worked yesterday persist long past their usefulness because of institutional inertia, the political costs of admitting that previous choices were wrong, and the disproportionate influence of constituencies who built their careers around the existing strategy.

The historical record is full of cases where adaptation failed catastrophically. The French in 1940 had built their defence around the Maginot Line and a doctrine of methodical battle that was perfectly suited to the previous war and disastrously unsuited to the German way of armoured warfare that had developed since. The British in the early years of the Second World War struggled to adapt their colonial-policing army to peer-conventional warfare in Europe and North Africa. The Soviet Union throughout the 1980s failed to adapt its strategy to the changing economic and informational environment, and the system collapsed under the weight of strategies that had outlived their preconditions. The US in the 2000s and 2010s struggled to adapt counter-terrorism strategy to the insight that population-centric counter-insurgency would be politically unsustainable for the durations its own doctrine demanded.

The factors that enable successful adaptation are less dramatic than the failures but are increasingly well understood. Flexible institutions — those with internal mechanisms for challenging dominant assumptions, including dissenting analytical units, post-mortem processes, and red-teaming — are better at adaptation than rigid ones. Civil-military dialogue that includes serious examination of strategic premises, not just operational details, surfaces problems before they become crises. Leadership willing to absorb the political cost of acknowledging that the previous approach was failing makes adaptation feasible. And institutional memory that distinguishes between sunk costs (irrelevant to the going-forward decision) and accumulated experience (relevant) prevents adaptation from being either too slow or too fast.

A persistent finding is that the same state can be adaptive in one strategic domain and rigid in another at the same time. The Cold War US adapted relatively well at the strategic level (containment was reinterpreted across multiple administrations) while struggling to adapt at the operational level (counter-insurgency was an enduring weakness). Cold War-era Israeli forces adapted continuously to changing threats from neighbours and non-state actors but struggled to adapt their political-strategic framework for managing the Palestinian population. The unevenness suggests that adaptation is not a single capacity but a collection of practices, each of which has to be cultivated in the specific domain where it matters.

The obstacles to adaptation are also analysable. Institutional inertia — the tendency for organisations to keep doing what they have always done — is built into the basic logic of bureaucracy. Sunk-cost reasoning ("we've already invested so much in this approach that we can't change now") is a recurring fallacy that obstructs strategic learning. Vested interests in the existing strategy — the units that fight with the legacy doctrine, the contractors who supply the legacy equipment, the analysts whose careers depend on the legacy assumptions — actively resist change. Cognitive biases (confirmation bias, motivated reasoning) keep decision-makers from seeing the evidence that should prompt adaptation. Each of these obstacles has institutional countermeasures, but the countermeasures themselves require deliberate construction.

A particularly important capacity is the willingness to question foundational assumptions, not just operational details. Adaptation that revises tactics but preserves an underlying strategy that is itself failing is more dangerous than no adaptation at all, because it produces an appearance of responsiveness without the substance. The willingness to ask "are we trying to do the right thing?" — not just "are we doing the current thing well?" — is the deepest level of strategic adaptation, and it requires the most deliberate institutional design to sustain.

## Why this matters in policy

The Indian strategic system has well-known adaptation challenges: long bureaucratic decision cycles, civil-military boundaries that limit serious dialogue, institutional siloes that compartmentalise information, and a tradition of risk-averse continuity that can shade into stasis. The recent institutional reforms — the CDS, the National Security Strategy, the explicit articulation of doctrines that had previously been implicit — are attempts to build adaptation capacity. The test will be whether the institutions produced by these reforms become the venues for the harder kind of adaptation (questioning foundational assumptions) or whether they reproduce the surface-level responsiveness that has always existed within deeper inertia.
