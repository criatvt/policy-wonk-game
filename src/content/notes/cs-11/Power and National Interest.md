# Power and National Interest

National interest is the orienting concept of foreign policy. Hans Morgenthau, the foundational realist theorist, defined the term as the protection of the state's territorial integrity and political independence, but the practical content has always been broader. Prakash Menon's CS-1 deck names four conventional categories: survival, economic well-being, favourable world order, and promotion of national values. Each category is itself a cluster of more specific interests; together they define what foreign policy is for.

National interests have a usefully ambiguous quality. They are concrete enough to discipline policy choices — once specified, they rule out actions that work against them and identify actions that advance them. But they are general enough to allow reasonable disagreement about how they should be pursued in particular circumstances. Two policymakers can share an account of the national interest in regional stability and disagree sharply about whether a particular intervention serves that interest. The concept does work without producing automatic answers.

The realist tradition, following Morgenthau, treats national interest as objective and measurable, defined primarily in terms of power. The state's interest, in this view, is to maintain and expand its power within the international system; everything else is derivative. The constructivist tradition treats national interest as socially constructed — varying with the state's identity, history, and the prevailing norms of the international system — and points out that what gets counted as an interest varies dramatically across states and over time. Both traditions illuminate different aspects: realists are right that survival and security are universal foundations; constructivists are right that the specific content of interests is shaped by domestic politics and ideological orientation.

The second half of the title — "power" — is where the discipline of strategic studies focuses its most distinctive attention. Lawrence Freedman's essay on strategic studies and the problem of power names the central puzzle: power is unusually hard to measure, accumulate, and convert into political outcomes. The gap between capability and effect is the discipline's working domain. A state that possesses overwhelming military capability does not automatically achieve political compliance; a state with substantial economic resources does not automatically translate them into diplomatic leverage; a state with extensive soft-power assets does not automatically convert admiration into policy support.

The conversion problem has several aspects. Power resources must first be translated into capabilities (recruiting and training militaries, building productive industries, maintaining diplomatic networks). The capabilities must then be deployed in contexts where they have leverage — military force is less useful against terrorism than against conventional armies, economic sanctions are less useful against autarkic adversaries than against open economies. And the deployment must be calibrated to political objectives — too much force, applied indiscriminately, can produce backlash; too little fails to achieve the objective. At every stage, conversion can fail.

A useful corrective to crude power calculations is the recognition that adversaries are also calculating. A state's power is exercised against actors who can respond, adapt, and resist. The same instrument applied to different adversaries can produce very different results because of differences in the adversary's vulnerabilities, alternatives, and resolve. The Cuban Missile Crisis is the classic case of two great powers each calculating the other's resolve and capability, with the outcome depending on the credibility of mutual threats rather than the raw balance of forces.

## Why this matters in policy

For India, the national interest question is more contested than the formal documents suggest. The traditional formulation — strategic autonomy, sovereignty, economic development, peaceful periphery — is widely shared, but the implications for specific choices (alignment with the US versus engagement with Russia and China, response to provocations, scale of defence investment) are genuinely debated. The power side of the equation is the operational question: even where interests are agreed, the conversion of India's growing capabilities into outcomes that advance those interests is a continuous bureaucratic and political challenge. Strategic studies offers the analytical tools; political institutions have to do the work.
