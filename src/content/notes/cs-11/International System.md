# International System

The international system is the structured arrangement within which states interact. Its fundamental feature, identified by Hobbes and elaborated by structural realists from Waltz onwards, is anarchy: there is no central authority above states with the power to enforce rules or settle disputes. Each state is formally sovereign, responsible for its own security, and ultimately judge of its own actions. This is not a claim that international relations are chaotic — they are highly patterned — but a claim about the absence of a hierarchical enforcement mechanism analogous to the state's monopoly on domestic violence.

Within this anarchic structure, states are arranged by the distribution of capability. Systems are conventionally classified as unipolar (one preponderant state, e.g., the US in the 1990s), bipolar (two competing great powers, e.g., the Cold War), or multipolar (several great powers of comparable weight, e.g., Europe before 1914). Theorists debate which polarity is most conducive to stability — Waltz argued bipolarity was most stable because the two principals could clearly identify each other's interests, while John Mearsheimer has argued that multipolarity with a clear regional hegemon can be stable, and others have argued for the stability of unipolarity. The empirical evidence is mixed because each historical case has too many other variables in play.

The system is not just states. It includes international organisations (the UN, the WTO, the World Bank, regional bodies), formal rules (treaties, customary international law), informal norms (the norm against territorial conquest, the norm of diplomatic immunity), non-state actors of varying power (multinational corporations, terrorist groups, ideological movements), and increasingly transnational phenomena that defy state boundaries (cyber, climate, pandemics). A complete account of the international system has to register all of these and how they interact.

The English School of international relations, associated with Hedley Bull and others, distinguishes between an international *system* (mere interaction of states) and an international *society* (states bound together by shared norms, institutions, and conceptions of common interest). The post-1945 order — with its dense web of institutions, norms, and shared expectations — is closer to a society than to a pure anarchic system. The question for the present moment is whether the society is eroding back toward something more system-like, as great-power competition strains the underlying norms and institutions.

The institutions of the system are products of particular historical moments. The UN was designed for a 1945 world; the Bretton Woods institutions reflect the economic preferences of their creators; the global trade regime crystallised in the 1990s. As the underlying distribution of power and economic weight shifts, the institutions feel increasingly out of step — hence calls for UN Security Council reform, the rise of alternative institutional arrangements (BRICS New Development Bank, AIIB), and the broader contest over the future shape of global governance.

## Why this matters in policy

India's relationship to the international system is one of the central questions of its foreign policy. Is India a beneficiary of the existing order who should work to preserve and reform it? A challenger seeking to alter the rules? Or some combination — a status-quo power in some domains, a revisionist in others? The answer shapes choices about which institutions to invest in, which to circumvent, and which to challenge. The institutional architecture matters because it is one of the few mechanisms by which less powerful states can constrain more powerful ones.
