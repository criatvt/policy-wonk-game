# Hegemony

Hegemony is the condition in which a single state possesses sufficient preponderance of power that it can shape the rules, norms, and institutions of the international system and impose its preferences with limited resistance. The hegemon does not necessarily exercise direct rule over other states — modern hegemons typically operate through alliance networks, international institutions, and economic predominance rather than direct political control — but it possesses the capability to do so over significant matters, and other states adjust their behaviour accordingly.

The historical examples are familiar. Britain in the nineteenth century, particularly between 1815 and 1914, exercised maritime hegemony: the Royal Navy was larger than the next two navies combined, the City of London was the centre of global finance, and the Pax Britannica described an international order whose underlying terms were set in Westminster. The United States after 1945 exercised an even more comprehensive hegemony: it was the largest economy by a substantial margin, possessed nuclear weapons before any other state, anchored the global financial system through the dollar and the Bretton Woods institutions, and led the Western alliance. After 1991, the US briefly approached unipolarity — preponderance so complete that no plausible coalition could balance it.

Theorists of hegemonic stability — Robert Gilpin, Charles Kindleberger — have argued that a hegemonic system is not merely a description but an explanation of stability. The hegemon provides public goods (open sea lanes, a stable reserve currency, a functioning trade order) that smaller states could not provide for themselves; the result is a more orderly international system than alternative configurations would produce. The counter-argument, from balance-of-power theorists, is that hegemony is inherently unstable: other states balance against the dominant power, and the hegemon is gradually drawn into peripheral commitments that exhaust its resources.

The empirical record suggests both views capture something. Hegemonic periods do tend to coincide with relative international order — the long nineteenth-century peace under British hegemony, the post-1945 order under American leadership. But hegemony is also impermanent: the costs of maintaining preponderance accumulate, the diffusion of economic and technological capability lifts other states, and the hegemon eventually faces challengers it cannot suppress. The 1914 collapse of the British-led order and the contemporary erosion of American unipolarity are both data points.

A useful distinction is between hegemonic capability and hegemonic legitimacy. Capability is the material underpinning; legitimacy is the willingness of other states to accept the hegemon's leadership as acceptable rather than oppressive. American hegemony in the late twentieth century was relatively legitimate because much of the world accepted the basic terms of the US-led order. The legitimacy is more contested today — partly because the relative capability has eroded, partly because the conduct of the hegemon (Iraq, Libya, withdrawal from agreements) has cost it credibility, partly because rising powers have alternative visions of order that command genuine support in parts of the world.

## Why this matters in policy

The current strategic moment is a transition: the unipolar order of the early 1990s is gone, but what replaces it is unsettled. The leading possibilities are a US-China bipolarity, a more genuinely multipolar order, or a fragmented system with no clear leader. For India, the configuration matters enormously: a multipolar order plays to Indian strengths (strategic autonomy, balancing across great powers), while a bipolar US-China contest forces harder choices. Indian policy has consistently preferred the multipolar option and worked to encourage its emergence.
