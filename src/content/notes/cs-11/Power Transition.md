# Power Transition

Power transition theory, developed by A. F. K. Organski in the 1950s and elaborated since, claims that the international system is most prone to war at moments when a rising power approaches parity with the dominant power in the system. The mechanism is straightforward: a satisfied dominant power and a rising challenger both have reasons to act. The dominant power may consider preventive war to lock in its advantage before it slips further. The challenger may consider opportunistic war to lock in its gains before the dominant power adjusts. Each side's perception of the trajectory shapes its willingness to take risks, and the result is a particularly dangerous moment in the relationship.

The label "Thucydides trap", popularised by Graham Allison, makes the point through the canonical historical example. The Peloponnesian War of the fifth century BCE was fought between Sparta, the long-dominant land power in Greece, and Athens, the rising commercial and naval power. Thucydides, the contemporaneous historian, wrote that the war was caused by "the growth of Athenian power and the fear it inspired in Sparta". Allison's empirical exercise looks at sixteen historical cases of rising-power-meets-declining-hegemon and finds that twelve produced war. The data are contested — the sample is small, the cases are not independent — but the underlying logic is recognisable.

The theory's relevance to the contemporary moment is the obvious reason for its current prominence. China has risen rapidly in economic, technological, and military capability over the past four decades, and the United States is the dominant power whose preponderance is being challenged. The structural conditions that power transition theory identifies as dangerous are present. The strategic question is whether the dynamics that produced war in twelve of sixteen historical cases can be managed differently this time — through institutional binding, mutual deterrence, careful diplomacy, and the constraint of economic interdependence.

Several factors might modulate the risk. Nuclear weapons substantially raise the cost of major war, making the historical analogies less binding. Economic interdependence between the rising and declining powers — unprecedented in scale between the US and China — creates incentives for both to manage the relationship. The architecture of international institutions provides forums for negotiated accommodation that did not exist in earlier eras. None of these factors eliminate the risk, but each plausibly reduces it.

Other factors might worsen the risk. Domestic politics in both rising and declining powers can produce leaders who calculate differently from the theory's assumed rational actors. The information environment — fast-moving, distorted, full of misperceptions — can produce escalatory dynamics that better information might have avoided. The competition extends into domains (cyber, space, AI) that lack established rules of the road and where escalation can happen faster than diplomacy can manage. And the global system being transitioned through is more complex than the bilateral systems of historical analogues — third parties (India, Europe, Russia) have their own preferences and can amplify or dampen the central dynamic.

A productive read of power transition theory is as a warning rather than a prediction. The historical record establishes that the structural conditions of rising-power-meets-declining-hegemon have repeatedly produced war, and that managing the transition successfully requires deliberate, sustained effort. The theory does not say that war is inevitable; it says that the default in such situations is bad, and that avoidance is a strategic achievement, not a presumption.

## Why this matters in policy

The US-China transition is reshaping the strategic environment within which all other states, including India, operate. For India, the central choice is how to position itself in a system whose central dynamic is a great-power transition: as a balancer, a hedger, a third pole, or some combination across issue areas. Each option has different implications for which partnerships to deepen, which capabilities to invest in, and which institutional arrangements to support. The choice is not one to be made once but is being made continuously through the accumulation of policy decisions.
