# Military Strategy

Military strategy is the use of armed force, and the threat of armed force, to achieve political objectives. The foundational statement of the field is Carl von Clausewitz's *On War*, written in the early nineteenth century and posthumously published in 1832. Clausewitz's central claim is that war is the continuation of politics by other means: the use of force is always instrumental to political purposes, and military activity divorced from political purpose is violence, not strategy. This subordination of military activity to political purpose is the analytical core of the entire field.

The implication is consequential. Military success in itself does not constitute strategic success. A campaign can win every battle and still produce strategic defeat if its operations do not advance the political settlement the war is meant to produce. The Vietnam War is the canonical illustration: US forces won tactically in almost every engagement they fought, and yet the political objective — a viable non-communist South Vietnam — receded rather than approached. The strategic failure was a failure to connect military activity to a politically achievable end, not a failure of operational execution.

Clausewitz introduced several conceptual distinctions that remain essential. The *trinity* — composed of the people, the army, and the government — frames war as the interaction of passion (the population's commitment), chance and probability (the army's operational domain), and reason (the government's political direction). Strategy works when all three elements are in balance and aligned; it fails when one is exhausted or out of step with the others. Clausewitz also distinguished *absolute war* (a theoretical pole of unlimited violence) from *real war* (the constrained, partial, politically directed conflicts actually fought), and warned against the seductive idea that any real war could approach the absolute pole without losing political coherence.

The relationship between strategy and operations is a recurring source of confusion. Strategy is concerned with the use of war (or the threat of war) to achieve political ends; operational art is concerned with the design and conduct of campaigns; tactics is concerned with the conduct of individual engagements. The three levels are nested, but the analytical move from one to the next is non-trivial. A brilliantly conducted tactical operation that does not contribute to operational success is wasted; a flawlessly executed operational campaign that does not advance the strategic purpose is wasted; and a militarily successful strategy that does not produce the political settlement is wasted.

Hew Strachan's contribution to this module argues that the boundary between strategy and policy has become blurred in contemporary practice. Politicians delegate strategic decisions to military professionals, who properly insist that political ends must be defined; political principals respond by demanding "military options" without specifying the political objective those options are supposed to serve. The result is military activity in search of political purpose, rather than political purpose using military activity. The cure, Strachan argues, is to restore the conversation between political and military leadership at the strategic level — what he calls "an unequal dialogue" in which civilians have ultimate authority but military advice is unsentimentally engaged.

Modern military strategy must also account for the changes wrought by nuclear weapons. Between nuclear-armed states, the use of force at the level of total war has become irrational; military strategy is increasingly about deterrence, crisis management, limited war, and the threats short of war that constitute strategic competition. The result is that the classical Clausewitzian framework still applies but in a more constrained form: the political objectives that war can advance are narrower, the risks of escalation are larger, and the place of military strategy within grand strategy has shifted.

## Why this matters in policy

Indian military strategy is being actively reshaped. The integration of the three services under a Chief of Defence Staff, the move toward theatre commands, the renewed emphasis on doctrine — all are attempts to bring Indian military strategy into closer alignment with national political purposes. The institutional gap between political principals and military professionals — long a feature of the Indian system — has narrowed but not closed. The Clausewitzian challenge of producing genuine strategic dialogue between political and military leadership is, in India as elsewhere, an unfinished project.
