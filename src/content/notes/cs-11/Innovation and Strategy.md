# Innovation and Strategy

Innovation reshapes the conditions under which strategy is made. New technologies do not just provide better tools for old purposes; they sometimes alter the basic logic of what is possible. The introduction of gunpowder ended the era of fortified castles and aristocratic cavalry; the railway transformed the speed of military mobilisation in the nineteenth century; aircraft and tanks restructured warfare in the twentieth; nuclear weapons made total war between great powers irrational; and the contemporary cycle — cyber, space, autonomous systems, artificial intelligence — is shaping the strategic possibilities of the twenty-first.

The strategic challenge is not simply to acquire new technology but to understand what kinds of strategy the new technology enables and forecloses. Andrew Marshall, the long-time director of the US Office of Net Assessment, made the term "revolution in military affairs" current in the 1990s to describe the combined impact of precision-guided munitions, networked sensors, and information processing on the conduct of war. The diagnostic claim was that these technologies would reward militaries that organised differently — flatter command structures, joint operations, information dominance — and punish those that retained Cold War mass-army organisation.

The strategic literature on innovation distinguishes between sustaining innovation (incremental improvement of existing capabilities) and disruptive innovation (new capabilities that change the rules of the contest). Militaries are characteristically better at the former than the latter, because the institutional incentives reward incremental improvement of legacy systems. Disruptive innovation usually requires either external pressure (a battlefield defeat that forces reconsideration) or unusual internal leadership (a commander or political principal willing to override institutional resistance). The German interwar embrace of armoured warfare, the US Navy's adoption of aircraft carriers in the 1930s, and the Israeli adaptation to network-centric warfare are studied cases of successful disruptive innovation.

A persistent puzzle is the gap between technological capability and strategic effect. Possessing the technology is necessary but not sufficient. The same precision-strike capability that won the 1991 Gulf War decisively could not produce political settlement in Iraq after 2003 or in Afghanistan over twenty years. The same cyber capabilities that have produced impressive operational effects (Stuxnet, NotPetya) have not obviously translated into durable strategic outcomes. Technology amplifies the gap between what can be done and what it produces.

Innovation also shifts the offence-defence balance, and through that, the security environment. Some technologies favour the defender (fortifications, anti-ship missiles, anti-access bubbles); others favour the attacker (precision long-range strike, cyber penetration). The balance shapes whether the system is conflict-prone or stable. The current technological moment is contested: some analysts argue that long-range precision weapons and information dominance favour the offence, others that anti-access/area-denial capabilities favour the defence. The answer matters for whether the US-China contest in the Western Pacific is stable or escalation-prone.

A final consideration is that innovation in adversaries is as consequential as innovation at home. A state that develops a new capability can find its competitive position transformed, but a state whose adversary develops one can find its strategy invalidated. Strategic studies pays close attention to the diffusion of capability — how quickly does the new technology spread, who acquires it, how does it change the calculations of those who do and do not have it.

## Why this matters in policy

India is a technology importer and exporter, a producer of indigenous defence capability in some areas and a buyer in others, with an active interest in emerging technology domains (space, cyber, AI). The strategic question is whether the Indian system can identify and prioritise the technologies that will matter most over the next decade and adapt institutions to use them effectively. The historical record on this — across the DRDO, the public-sector defence undertakings, and the broader research-and-development ecosystem — is uneven, and the question of how to do better is one of the live debates in Indian defence policy.
