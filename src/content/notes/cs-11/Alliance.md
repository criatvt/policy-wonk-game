# Alliance

An alliance is a formal agreement between two or more states to cooperate on security matters, typically through commitments to mutual defence, coordinated military planning, or joint action against a defined threat. The motive is pragmatic: states form alliances because their individual capabilities are insufficient for the objectives they want to pursue, or insufficient to deter the threats they face. A weak state gains security by attaching itself to a stronger one; a strong state gains influence, basing rights, and force-multipliers by binding allies to its strategic preferences.

The classic functions of an alliance are aggregation and signalling. Aggregation pools military and economic capability against a shared adversary: NATO during the Cold War combined the resources of a dozen states to deter the Soviet Union, achieving a balance that none of the members could have produced alone. Signalling tells potential adversaries that an attack on one member will draw a response from the whole — the credibility of that signal is the basis of extended deterrence. Article 5 of the NATO treaty is the canonical example of a signalling commitment, and its credibility is a constant subject of allied debate.

Alliances are instruments, not ends. They serve interests, and interests change. The Anglo-Japanese alliance of 1902 was a critical Asian balancing arrangement before the First World War; it had outlived its purpose by 1922. The Sino-Soviet alliance of 1950 fractured within a decade as Chinese and Soviet interests diverged. The Atlantic alliance has reinvented its rationale repeatedly — anti-Soviet containment, post-Cold War democratic enlargement, post-9/11 counter-terrorism, post-2014 deterrence of Russia. Alliances that fail to adapt to new interests either dissolve or persist as hollow forms.

Alliance management is its own strategic craft. The chief difficulties are free-riding (junior partners under-contribute, expecting the senior to bear disproportionate costs), entrapment (commitments drag a state into conflicts it would otherwise have avoided), and abandonment (a partner declines to honour commitments when the test comes). Great-power alliance leaders spend enormous energy on credibility — through bases, joint exercises, security guarantees, and the constant rhetorical reaffirmation of commitment.

For most of its post-independence history, India avoided formal alliances under the doctrine of non-alignment, reasoning that the costs of binding commitment outweighed the benefits. That calculation has shifted in recent decades as the strategic environment has changed: the 1971 Treaty of Peace, Friendship and Cooperation with the Soviet Union showed that India was willing to enter quasi-alliance arrangements when interests required, and the contemporary Quad framework (US, Japan, India, Australia) operates in the space between alliance and partnership.

## Why this matters in policy

The alliance question is one of the perennial strategic choices: whether to bear the costs of binding commitment to gain capability, or to preserve flexibility at the cost of capability. Indian strategic thinking has historically leaned toward flexibility, but the rise of a more capable China has reopened the calculation. Policy debate now turns on how much commitment is enough to deter without entrapment, and how to extract maximum balancing benefit from partnerships short of full alliance.
