# Strategy versus Tactics

The classic distinction between strategy and tactics tracks the difference between long-term political objectives and immediate operational engagements. A *strategic* objective might be "secure regional pre-eminence" or "deny adversary access to a key region." *Tactics* are the specific operations — which units move where, in what formation, with what timing, against which targets — that pursue the strategic objective.

The distinction matters because the two levels can come unstuck. A military force can win every tactical engagement while losing the strategic contest, if the tactical victories don't add up to the strategic objective or if they impose costs that erode the broader political position. The classic illustrations include the American experience in Vietnam (tactical dominance with strategic defeat) and various counterinsurgency campaigns where successful village-clearing operations cumulatively turned the population against the foreign forces.

The reverse case is also real: tactical setbacks that nevertheless advance the strategic objective. Soviet planners after Stalingrad accepted ongoing tactical losses while the broader strategic position shifted in their favour. The British in WWII similarly accepted substantial tactical setbacks while building the strategic conditions for eventual victory.

## Why this matters in policy

Practitioners at the strategic level have to keep the strategy-tactics distinction visible. Successful operations that don't advance the objective should worry the strategists. Operational setbacks that occur while the strategic position improves should be tolerated. Either error — confusing one level for the other — produces decisions that look right at one level and wrong at the other.
