# Strategic vs Tactical

The distinction between strategic and tactical levels of action is one of the foundational analytical moves in military thought, and from there has spread into general usage. Strategy concerns the overall political objectives a state is trying to achieve and the integrated use of means to achieve them. Tactics concerns the conduct of immediate engagements — the specific manoeuvres, deployments, and actions through which strategic plans are operationally executed. Strategy answers "what are we trying to achieve and how"; tactics answers "what do we do in this particular engagement to advance the strategy".

Between the two levels sits the operational level, which concerns the design and conduct of campaigns. A war contains multiple campaigns; each campaign contains multiple battles; each battle is an engagement at the tactical level. The strategic level deals with why the war is being fought and what its political objective is; the operational level deals with how the war is being conducted across its campaigns; the tactical level deals with how each individual engagement is conducted. The three-level framework is a heuristic, not a precise typology, but it captures the hierarchical nesting of military activity within political purpose.

The classical illustration of the relationship is the contrast between Clausewitz and his rival theorist Antoine-Henri Jomini. Jomini's writings on the conduct of Napoleon's campaigns emphasised the operational and tactical dimensions: the geometric arrangement of forces, the manoeuvre to gain position, the concentration of force at the decisive point. Clausewitz's *On War* engaged the same material at the tactical and operational levels but constantly subordinated them to the strategic question of how military operations served political purpose. The Clausewitzian view eventually prevailed in the discipline because it captured the real hierarchy: tactics serve operations serve strategy serve policy.

The practical implication is that brilliant tactics cannot rescue a flawed strategy. The US military in Vietnam was tactically superior in nearly every engagement it fought; the strategic outcome was nonetheless failure because the operational and strategic levels were incoherent. The German Wehrmacht of 1940–41 was tactically brilliant — the campaigns in Poland, France, and the early operations in the Soviet Union showcased tactical and operational virtuosity — but the strategic objective (the conquest and consolidation of a German-dominated Europe) was beyond the reach of even the most virtuoso tactical execution. The point is not that tactics do not matter — bad tactics can lose battles that good tactics would have won — but that good tactics serving bad strategy produces battlefield victories without strategic effect.

The distinction also has reverse implications. Tactical actions can produce strategic effects, particularly when they cross thresholds that change the political situation. A tactical incident on a contested border can escalate into strategic crisis; a small intelligence operation can produce strategic embarrassment; a single act of terrorism can produce strategic responses out of proportion to the immediate event. The competent strategist anticipates these threshold effects and avoids actions whose tactical convenience produces unwanted strategic consequences.

The misuse of strategic language is also relevant to the distinction. Hew Strachan's *Lost Meaning of Strategy* argues that "strategic" has come to mean "important" rather than indicating the actual strategic level of action. A "strategic communications strategy" within a marketing department is using both words in the loose sense; the resulting activity is communications planning, not strategy in any meaningful sense. The dilution of language obscures the distinctions that mattered when the terms were carefully used, and one of the disciplinary tasks of strategic studies is to retain the precision the field originally had.

## Why this matters in policy

For policymakers, the strategic-vs-tactical distinction is a regularly useful discipline. Many policy problems present themselves as tactical (what to do about this specific incident, how to respond to this specific provocation) when the harder and more consequential question is strategic (what is the overall direction of policy, and how does this specific incident relate to that direction). Asking the strategic question — what are we trying to achieve, and does this tactical move advance it — prevents the accumulation of tactically reasonable actions that add up to a strategically incoherent posture. Indian foreign and defence policy, like that of most states, would benefit from more deliberate attention to this discipline.
