# Strategic Fundamentals

Strategy is the art and science of planning to achieve political objectives, particularly under conditions of conflict, uncertainty, and resource constraint. The most economical definition, drawn from Prakash Menon's CS-1 slide deck, is that strategy is the bridge that connects ends with means. Each side of the bridge matters: the ends are the political objectives the state is trying to achieve, the means are the resources and instruments it has at its disposal, and the strategy is the way of using the latter to produce the former.

The economy of the definition matters because it points to where strategy actually fails. Strategies fail when the ends are unclear or contradictory (no coherent objective is being pursued), when the means are inadequate to the ends (the resources cannot deliver what is being asked of them), or when the bridge between ends and means is poorly constructed (the way of using resources does not produce the intended outcome). Most strategic failures, on close inspection, fall into one of these three categories.

Menon's deck lays out a sequence of concepts that structure strategic thought: power, politics, interests, policy, strategy, planning. Power is the basic capacity to act — the resources and capabilities a state can bring to bear. Politics is the process by which power is distributed and used within and between states. Interests are the desired end states that motivate state action — survival, prosperity, favourable world order, promotion of values. Policy is the broad course of action that guides decisions in pursuit of interests. Strategy is the connecting framework between policy and the deployment of resources. Planning is the disciplined process of working out the ends-ways-means details that strategy requires. Each concept feeds the next; together they describe how political purpose gets translated into operational reality.

A productive way to think about strategy is as a theory of victory. A strategy is implicitly a claim about how the chosen actions will produce the desired outcome — what causal chain runs from current activities to future political result. Strategies that lack a coherent theory of victory tend to produce activity without direction: things get done because they can be done, not because they will produce the intended effect. Hew Strachan's *Lost Meaning of Strategy* argues that much contemporary "strategy" — in defence ministries, in foreign-policy documents, even in business — has degraded into precisely this: action without a coherent theory of victory, "strategy" as branding for routine activity.

Strategy is fundamentally about choice under constraint. If a state had unlimited resources and no opposition, strategy would be unnecessary — it could simply do everything. Strategy is the discipline of choosing what to do and what not to do, because resources are limited and adversaries are actively trying to frustrate the chosen objectives. The choices include which interests to prioritise, which means to invest in, which actions to take and which to forgo, which risks to accept and which to avoid. Each choice has an opportunity cost, and the discipline of strategy is to make the choices deliberately rather than by drift.

Strategy is also inherently competitive. The objectives a state pursues are usually pursued against the active opposition of others. This is what distinguishes strategy from planning. Planning assumes a passive environment that can be analysed and worked through; strategy assumes an active adversary who is also planning, and whose plans will frustrate yours unless you anticipate and counter them. The classical formulation, from Helmuth von Moltke, is that "no plan survives first contact with the enemy" — meaning that strategy has to be more adaptive than static planning, capable of responding to what the adversary actually does rather than what we hoped they would do.

## Why this matters in policy

Strategic fundamentals are the analytical starting point for thinking about any concrete policy problem. The questions to ask in sequence: what is the political objective; what are the resources and instruments available; what is the theory of victory by which the resources will produce the objective; what is the adversary trying to do, and how does our strategy account for their actions; what are the risks and costs, and are they acceptable. The questions are simple to state but unforgiving in application — most strategic difficulties, in policy as in war, come from skipping or mis-answering one of them.
