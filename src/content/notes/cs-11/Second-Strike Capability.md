# Second-Strike Capability

Second-strike capability is the ability of a nuclear-armed state to absorb a nuclear first strike from an adversary and still deliver a retaliatory nuclear response severe enough to inflict damage the adversary considers unacceptable. The concept was developed in the early Cold War, most influentially by Albert Wohlstetter in his 1959 *Foreign Affairs* essay on "the delicate balance of terror", and it has remained the technical foundation of stable nuclear deterrence ever since.

The logic is simple but consequential. If a state's nuclear arsenal can be destroyed in a first strike, then an adversary contemplating a disarming attack has an incentive to strike first — the attacker can eliminate the deterrent before it can be used, gaining a decisive advantage. Conversely, if the arsenal can survive a first strike, no such advantage exists: any attack would invite retaliation that the attacker cannot avoid. Survivability eliminates the first-strike incentive on both sides, and the strategic relationship stabilises around mutual vulnerability.

Survivability is engineered through dispersal, mobility, concealment, and hardening. The three traditional legs of a survivable nuclear force are land-based intercontinental ballistic missiles (typically in hardened silos or on mobile launchers), strategic bombers (which can be dispersed and put on alert), and submarine-launched ballistic missiles (which are the most survivable because submarines on patrol are hard to find and destroy). The submarine leg is widely considered the most reliable second-strike platform; for that reason, all major nuclear powers either operate or aspire to operate ballistic-missile submarines. India's INS Arihant and its successors represent this leg of the Indian nuclear deterrent.

The implication of second-strike capability for arsenal size is important: deterrence does not require numerical superiority over the adversary. A smaller force that can reliably survive and retaliate provides at least as much deterrent effect as a larger force that might be vulnerable to a first strike. This is why India's nuclear doctrine of "credible minimum deterrence" emphasises survivability over numbers: the goal is enough force to assure unacceptable retaliation, not enough to win an exchange. The doctrine reflects a deliberate choice not to pursue parity in numbers but to pursue sufficiency in effect.

A complicating factor is that the technologies of detection, precision strike, and missile defence keep evolving. Submarines that were undetectable in one decade may become trackable in the next. Hardened silos that survived earlier generations of warheads may be vulnerable to newer ones. Ballistic-missile defences, if effective, can erode the credibility of second-strike retaliation by suggesting that some retaliatory missiles might be intercepted. Each technological development that potentially undermines second-strike survivability invites compensating investment from states whose deterrence depends on it, and the resulting dynamic can be destabilising.

The political-military significance is that second-strike capability is the difference between stable and unstable nuclear deterrence. A state with first-strike capability but vulnerable second-strike forces is in a dangerous position; an adversary may rationally consider a disarming attack, and the state itself may consider preemption to use its forces before they are destroyed. A state with assured second-strike capability faces neither incentive, and the relationship with its nuclear adversary settles into the relatively stable equilibrium of mutual assured destruction.

## Why this matters in policy

India's nuclear posture is built around credible second-strike capability. The triad of land-based, air-launched, and submarine-launched delivery systems is the structural commitment; the no-first-use declaration is the doctrinal commitment that follows from it. The policy rationale is that survivable retaliation is the most stable and least expensive form of deterrence, and that pursuing numerical parity or counterforce capability would invite a more dangerous arms-race dynamic without commensurate gain. The challenges are continuing investment in survivability (new submarines, mobile missiles, hardened command-and-control) and managing the introduction of new technologies that may strain the survivability assumption.
