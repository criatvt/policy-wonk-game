# Power and Legitimacy

Legitimacy is the quality that makes power acceptable to those over whom it is exercised. A legitimate authority commands compliance without continuous coercion: subjects do as the authority directs because they accept that the authority is entitled to direct them. An illegitimate authority must compel compliance at every turn, which is exhausting, expensive, and politically corrosive. The distinction matters as much in international politics as in domestic politics, and the management of legitimacy is one of the underrated tasks of strategic statecraft.

Max Weber's classical typology identified three sources of legitimacy: traditional (accepted because it has always been so), charismatic (accepted because of personal qualities of the leader), and rational-legal (accepted because the authority follows from established rules and procedures). Modern international legitimacy is mostly of the rational-legal type, conferred through procedures that are recognised as appropriate — UN resolutions, treaty arrangements, the consent of relevant institutions and audiences. A use of force authorised by the UN Security Council is more legitimate than one taken unilaterally; an intervention supported by the affected region is more legitimate than one opposed by it.

Legitimacy is a force multiplier for power. A state that can act legitimately faces less resistance, gathers allies more easily, and sustains its operations at lower political cost. The United States acted with relatively high legitimacy in the Gulf War of 1991 — UN authorisation, regional support, a clear casus belli — and assembled a broad coalition that shared the costs. The same state acted with much lower legitimacy in the 2003 invasion of Iraq — no UN authorisation, regional opposition, contested justification — and bore most of the costs alone, with consequences for its strategic position that took years to recover from.

Conversely, illegitimate exercises of power tend to be self-undermining. Coercion that is widely seen as unjust generates resistance not only from its immediate targets but from third parties whose interests are not directly engaged. The Russian invasion of Ukraine in 2022 is a contemporary example: the legitimacy deficit galvanised opposition far beyond the directly affected parties, produced sanctions of unprecedented scope, and reshaped the broader strategic environment in ways that imposed costs on Russia far in excess of any direct material response to its actions. The legitimacy dimension was not separate from the strategic outcome; it was a major driver of it.

Within domestic politics, the same logic operates. A government with high legitimacy can extract resources, demand sacrifice, and maintain cohesion under stress. A government with low legitimacy must repress, and the repression itself further erodes legitimacy. The same state, with the same material resources, projects very different amounts of usable power depending on its legitimacy at home. Authoritarian regimes that lack democratic legitimacy must constantly invest in alternative legitimacy sources — performance, nationalism, repression — and the investment is itself a tax on usable power.

A complication for international politics is that legitimacy is partly conferred by audiences whose preferences differ. An action legitimate to one audience may be illegitimate to another. The international system has no single arbiter of legitimacy; what looks legitimate to the United Nations may look otherwise to the African Union, and vice versa. Strategic actors who manage legitimacy well are usually careful about which audiences matter for which actions, and they work to keep the relevant audiences aligned with their objectives.

## Why this matters in policy

India has historically derived significant power from legitimacy. The independence movement's moral authority carried into the early decades of Indian foreign policy, where India spoke with influence disproportionate to its material capability. The strategic question for the present is how to maintain a substantial legitimacy premium as India's material power grows — to be the kind of rising power that other states are willing to support rather than balance against. This requires attention to the conduct of policy, not just to its substance: the manner of action shapes the legitimacy of action, and the legitimacy shapes how much the action achieves.
