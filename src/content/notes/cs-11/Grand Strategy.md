# Grand Strategy

Grand strategy is the highest level of strategic thinking. Where military strategy concerns the use of armed forces in war, and operational strategy concerns the management of campaigns within a war, grand strategy concerns the integration of all instruments of national power — military, diplomatic, economic, informational, cultural — over the long term, toward objectives that define what the state is trying to be in the world. The horizon is decades, not months; the instruments span the whole of government and beyond; and the test is whether the combination of activities is coherent enough to produce political results that justify the resources invested.

The classical formulation comes from Liddell Hart, who defined grand strategy as the coordination and direction of all the resources of a nation toward the attainment of the political object of the war. Modern usage broadens this from wartime to peacetime as well: grand strategy is the persistent framework that orients foreign policy across administrations, that defines which threats are existential and which are tolerable, which interests are core and which are peripheral, which partners are essential and which are convenient.

Three historical examples illustrate the concept. American grand strategy during the Cold War organised four decades of policy around the containment of Soviet power — military, economic, ideological, and institutional instruments all aligned on this single integrating purpose, with enough flexibility to adapt as circumstances changed. British grand strategy in the nineteenth century pursued naval supremacy, free trade, and balance-of-power diplomacy on the European continent, sustained from Pitt to Salisbury despite shifting domestic politics. Bismarck's grand strategy after German unification was to consolidate the new German position without provoking a balancing coalition — a delicate diplomatic engineering that worked while he was in office and unravelled rapidly after his dismissal.

Three difficulties recur. First, grand strategy is hard to sustain across democratic political cycles: each new administration is tempted to redefine national purpose, and continuity depends on bureaucratic and institutional memory more than on stated commitments. Second, grand strategy requires the integration of instruments controlled by different parts of government — diplomats, generals, treasury officials, intelligence services — whose institutional cultures and incentives diverge. The coordination cost is high. Third, the world being strategised about keeps changing: a grand strategy designed for one strategic environment can persist into a different one through institutional inertia, with the strategy becoming actively counter-productive.

Hew Strachan's *Lost Meaning of Strategy* argues that contemporary Western governments have largely lost the capacity for grand strategy. Strategy has become a synonym for any organised activity — "education strategy", "communications strategy" — and the integrative political judgment that grand strategy demands has been crowded out by bureaucratic process. Without a sense of overall purpose, the various instruments of state power proliferate and operate, often impressively in isolation, but without combining into anything that resembles a coherent direction. Whether this diagnosis is right is contested, but it captures a real anxiety about the discipline.

The Indian context is instructive. K. Subrahmanyam, the doyen of Indian strategic thought, argued for decades that India lacked an articulated grand strategy and that this was a structural disability. The recent emergence of more deliberate strategic documents — the nuclear doctrine, the Indo-Pacific framework, the explicit prioritisation of strategic autonomy — represents at least a partial corrective. Whether the underlying coherence has caught up with the documents is a separate question.

## Why this matters in policy

For any state, the test of grand strategy is whether the daily decisions across diplomacy, defence, trade, and aid add up to something rather than cancelling each other out. The discipline of grand-strategic thinking is to ask, persistently, whether each individual action is consistent with the overall direction. The discipline is unglamorous and easy to neglect — until a major strategic surprise reveals that the various instruments were not in fact pulling in the same direction.
