# Spheres of Influence

A sphere of influence is a region where one state exercises primary political influence and expects other major powers to respect that influence. The arrangement is typically informal — there is rarely a treaty assigning spheres — but it is understood by the relevant actors and shapes their behaviour. The dominant state's influence may operate through alliance arrangements, economic ties, ideological affinity, military presence, or the simple geographic fact of proximity. Other states may pursue commercial relationships within the sphere but are expected to avoid actions that would challenge the dominant state's standing.

The historical record is long. The Monroe Doctrine of 1823 established the Americas as a US sphere of influence, with European powers expected to refrain from new colonisation or political intervention in the Western Hemisphere. Tsarist and Soviet Russia maintained spheres in eastern Europe and central Asia, sometimes formal (the Warsaw Pact) and sometimes informal (post-Soviet "near abroad"). Britain operated extensive spheres of influence through the nineteenth century, in the Persian Gulf, parts of Africa, and the maritime margins of Asia. Imperial Japan's Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere was the explicit articulation of a sphere project in the 1930s and 1940s.

The contemporary status of spheres of influence is contested. The post-1945 normative order, particularly as it developed after 1990, claims that spheres of influence are illegitimate — that each state has the right to determine its own foreign policy and alignments, and that no other state has a privileged claim over its choices. The expansion of NATO eastward after 1991, the EU's neighbourhood policy, and the US "freedom agenda" of the 2000s all reflect this anti-spheres-of-influence position. Russia's position throughout has been that the post-Soviet space is its legitimate sphere of influence and that NATO expansion violates an implicit understanding from the end of the Cold War.

The 2022 invasion of Ukraine can be read as Russia attempting to re-impose a sphere-of-influence claim that the post-1990 order denied it. The conflict's outcome will shape whether spheres of influence remain a recognised feature of international politics or are formally repudiated in favour of universal sovereign-equality principles. The empirical reality, regardless of the normative debate, is that great powers act as if spheres exist: the US response to Cuban or Venezuelan alignment choices, the Chinese response to Taiwan and South China Sea questions, the Indian sensitivity to other powers' engagement with South Asia and the Indian Ocean — all reflect implicit sphere-of-influence thinking even by states that formally reject the concept.

Spheres of influence interact with several other strategic concepts. They overlap with the regional dimension of grand strategy — every great power has to manage relationships in its immediate neighbourhood differently from relationships with distant states. They overlap with the alliance question — alliances are sometimes the formal expression of sphere arrangements. They overlap with the balance-of-power question — competition over influence in particular regions is one of the principal mechanisms by which great powers balance against each other. The result is that the concept, even where formally repudiated, remains analytically useful for describing how international politics actually works.

A complication is that spheres are not stable. The states within a sphere have their own preferences and may seek to escape the dominant state's influence, particularly when alternative options open up. The smaller states of central and eastern Europe escaped the Soviet sphere quickly when given the option. South Asian states have varying degrees of comfort with Indian regional preeminence and use external partners to balance it. The dominant state in a sphere must continuously work to maintain its position, and the work is not always successful.

## Why this matters in policy

India's strategic environment is shaped by overlapping sphere-of-influence claims. South Asia is widely treated as an Indian sphere, but the Indian capacity to enforce it is contested by China's deepening engagement with India's neighbours. The Indian Ocean is the focus of explicit Indian doctrine — "neighbourhood first", "SAGAR" (Security and Growth for All in the Region) — that has implicit sphere-of-influence content. The strategic challenge is to maintain the sphere where it matters most while accepting that the era of any state holding an exclusive sphere is probably over.
