# Economic Statecraft

Economic statecraft is the deliberate use of economic instruments — trade access, financial-system access, investment flows, development aid, technology transfer, control of critical inputs, sanctions, tariffs — to advance foreign-policy objectives. The Institute of World Politics text in this module lists it among the major arts of statecraft: trade, aid, development, finance, foreign financial security policy, technology security policy, strategic materials policy, and defence-industrial infrastructure. The aggregate makes economic statecraft far broader than the popular focus on sanctions suggests.

The theoretical question that has occupied scholars since at least Albert Hirschman's *National Power and the Structure of Foreign Trade* (1945) is whether economic interdependence reduces or increases the likelihood of conflict. The liberal hypothesis is that interdependence makes war costly enough to be irrational — countries that trade extensively have so much to lose from disruption that they prefer to settle disputes peacefully. The realist counter is that asymmetric interdependence creates power: the less dependent side can coerce the more dependent one, and the prospect of disruption can itself be an instrument of coercion rather than a constraint on it. The First World War is the classic empirical embarrassment for the liberal view — Britain and Germany were each other's largest trading partners, and the trade did not prevent the war.

Sanctions are the most visible instrument and the most studied. The empirical record is mixed. Sanctions imposed on small target states with limited alternatives sometimes produce policy change — the financial pressure on South Africa contributed to the end of apartheid. Sanctions on larger or more autarkic targets often fail to produce the demanded policy change while imposing real economic costs on the target's population. The Iraq sanctions of the 1990s, the Iran sanctions of the 2000s and 2010s, the Russia sanctions since 2014 and especially since 2022 — each illustrates both what sanctions can and cannot do. They can isolate, raise costs, slow capability development, and signal resolve; they cannot easily compel a determined adversary to abandon what it considers a core interest.

A more recent dimension is the militarisation of finance and supply chains. The US dollar's role as the world's reserve currency gives the US extraordinary leverage over global financial flows, exercised through sanctions designations and the threat of exclusion from the dollar-denominated system. Control of semiconductor supply chains has become a strategic chokepoint, with the US, the Netherlands, Japan, and Taiwan as the critical nodes. The implication is that economic statecraft is no longer a soft, peacetime alternative to coercive instruments; it is one of the principal coercive instruments of modern competition.

The blunt instrument problem is real. Sanctions impose costs on civilians and businesses in target states, often most heavily on the politically powerless. Trade restrictions hurt firms and workers in the sanctioning state too. The reputational costs of weaponising economic interdependence may erode the long-term trust on which the financial system depends — encouraging targets and observers to develop alternatives. A serious account of economic statecraft has to weigh the immediate effects against these systemic costs.

## Why this matters in policy

For India, economic statecraft has both inbound and outbound dimensions. Inbound: India must navigate sanctions regimes imposed by others (US sanctions on Iran and Russia complicate India's energy supply, for instance) and avoid being on the wrong side of secondary sanctions enforcement. Outbound: India has used economic statecraft cautiously — trade restrictions on Pakistan, lines of credit to friendly governments, aid to neighbours — and the country's growing economic weight is gradually expanding what is possible. The lesson from the comparative record is that economic statecraft works best when integrated with diplomatic and military instruments in a coherent strategy, not when used alone.
