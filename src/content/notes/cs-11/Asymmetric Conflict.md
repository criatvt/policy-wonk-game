# Asymmetric Conflict

Asymmetric conflict is warfare between actors of grossly unequal conventional capability, in which the weaker side compensates for material inferiority by adopting tactics, weapons, and strategies that the stronger side is not designed to counter. The stronger side typically possesses better technology, more resources, and trained professional forces; the weaker side responds with insurgency, terrorism, guerrilla warfare, propaganda, and the political exploitation of any harm the stronger side inflicts on civilians.

The logic is straightforward. A weaker actor cannot win a set-piece battle against a conventional military, so it refuses to fight one. Instead, it disperses among the population, attacks logistics and infrastructure, ambushes patrols, and waits. The strategic theory of asymmetric conflict is that time favours the weaker side: the stronger side has the harder political problem of sustaining domestic and international support for a long, inconclusive war with mounting casualties, while the weaker side needs only to survive. Mao Zedong's writings on protracted war, Vo Nguyen Giap's Vietnamese campaigns, and the Algerian FLN's struggle against France are the classical templates.

The recent historical record is instructive. The United States lost in Vietnam despite overwhelming tactical and material superiority because the political objective — a viable non-communist South Vietnam — could not be produced through military operations. The Soviet Union failed in Afghanistan in the 1980s; the United States and NATO failed in the same country in the 2010s, against insurgents with a fraction of the coalition's firepower. France lost in Algeria. The strategic question is not why the weaker side wins so often, but why the stronger side keeps assuming that material superiority will translate into political outcomes.

Terrorism is a particular subspecies of asymmetric strategy. Its targets are not military but psychological — the production of fear in populations and the political reactions that fear induces. The 9/11 attacks killed roughly three thousand people and triggered two decades of military deployment, hundreds of thousands of casualties, and several trillion dollars of expenditure. By this measure, terrorism is one of the highest-leverage activities a small organisation can undertake against a great power, which is why its persistence in international affairs is a structural feature, not an aberration.

The harder lesson for strategists is that conventional strength is sometimes itself the problem. A great power's forces are designed for a kind of war the adversary refuses to fight. Adaptation to asymmetric warfare — counter-insurgency doctrine, civil-military coordination, intelligence integration, restraint on collateral damage — is hard precisely because it cuts against the institutional preferences of conventional militaries.

## Why this matters in policy

India's strategic environment has been shaped by asymmetric conflict since independence — in Kashmir, in the northeast, and across multiple internal insurgencies. The doctrinal challenge has been the same as elsewhere: how to apply force discriminately enough that it does not radicalise the population it is meant to protect, while still imposing costs on insurgents and their sponsors. Policy choices about counter-terrorism, internal security, and cross-border response all flow from how the asymmetric-conflict puzzle is framed.
