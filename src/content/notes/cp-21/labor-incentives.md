# Property Rights and Labour Incentives

Governor William Bradford's account of the early Plymouth colony in the 1620s is a standard case study in the incentives literature. The Pilgrims initially worked the land communally; everyone contributed to a common stock, and everyone received from it. Productivity was disappointing. Bradford writes that "young men, that were most able and fit for labour and service, did repine that they should spend their time and strength to work for other men's wives and children, without any recompense." The work was being done, but slowly and resentfully.

After a few seasons of poor harvests and growing discontent, the colony moved to a system of individual land plots, with each family responsible for working its own plot and keeping the proceeds. Productivity rose sharply. Bradford notes that "women now went willingly into the field, and took their little ones with them to set corn, which before would allege weakness, and inability." The same people, in the same conditions, with the same tools, produced much more once they captured the benefits of their own effort.

The case is rhetorically convenient for advocates of private property, and it is sometimes used carelessly to argue that private property always outperforms communal arrangements in every domain. The more careful reading is narrower: where labour effort is hard to observe and where individuals can capture the marginal product of their own effort, private property tends to produce more effort than communal arrangements that distribute output evenly. The conditions don't always hold — common-pool fisheries, ecological commons, and many shared infrastructures need different governance — but in the agricultural setting Bradford was describing, they did.

## Why this matters in policy

Property-rights design is a quiet but consequential lever in many policy domains. Land tenure reforms, water rights, intellectual property, even informal recognition of property in the urban informal sector all shape who captures the rewards of effort and therefore how much effort is exerted. Policy that ignores the question of who captures returns tends to find that the activity it was trying to encourage doesn't materialise.
