# Unintended Consequences

The cobra effect — also called the snake bounty story — is a useful tag for unintended consequences in incentive design. In the standard telling, British colonial administrators in Delhi were concerned about venomous snakes and offered a bounty for each cobra killed. The scheme worked initially: people brought in dead snakes and collected the reward. Then enterprising locals began breeding cobras specifically to kill and turn in for the bounty. When the colonial administrators discovered the breeding industry and cancelled the scheme, the breeders released their now-worthless snakes, and the wild cobra population was higher than before the programme started.

The story may be partly apocryphal but the structure recurs across documented cases. A bounty on rats in Hanoi produced rat-farming. A subsidy for fossil discovery led to suspiciously frequent fossil "discoveries" by the same prospectors. A US programme paying farmers per hectare of cotton not planted produced careful selection of the worst hectares to leave unplanted. Any incentive scheme that pays for an observable proxy creates pressure to produce the proxy by whatever means, including ones the scheme didn't anticipate.

The general principle is that incentive schemes must model actors' responses *to the scheme itself*, not just to the underlying problem. Designers tend to model how rational actors would behave in the absence of the scheme and assume the scheme will shift them toward the desired outcome. Intelligent actors actually behave with the scheme in mind, looking for the lowest-effort path to maximising the reward — which is frequently not the path the designers had in mind.

## Why this matters in policy

Robust incentive design includes "war-gaming" the scheme: explicitly asking, under these new rules, what will each affected actor try to do? Are there unintended paths to the reward? If so, can the scheme be amended to close them without losing the intended effect? Schemes that survive this stress-test tend to perform closer to their design. Schemes that don't tend to produce the famous, embarrassing case studies that the next generation of practitioners studies.
