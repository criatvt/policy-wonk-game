# Deadweight Loss

Deadweight loss is the welfare cost that arises when resources are not allocated efficiently. The classic case is a tax that pushes the price consumers pay above the marginal cost of production, reducing the quantity exchanged below the efficient level. Some transactions that would have created value for both parties (because the buyer was willing to pay more than the cost to the producer) do not happen because the tax wedge has made them unprofitable. The value that *would have been created* by those transactions, but isn't, is the deadweight loss.

The same logic applies to other interventions. Price ceilings can produce deadweight loss when they push prices below the equilibrium level and create unmet demand. Price floors can produce it when they push prices above equilibrium and create unsold supply. Subsidies can produce it when they encourage consumption beyond the efficient level. Monopoly power produces it when the monopolist restricts output below the competitive level to raise prices.

Deadweight loss is not the same as the cost of the intervention to the parties involved. A tax produces a transfer from consumers and producers to the government — that transfer is a redistribution, not a loss in aggregate welfare. The deadweight loss is the *additional* welfare destruction beyond the transfer: the value of transactions that would have happened in the absence of the tax but don't happen because of it. The transfer can be redistributed; the deadweight loss is just gone.

The size of the deadweight loss from any given intervention depends on the elasticities involved. If demand and supply are both very inelastic, the intervention barely changes the quantity exchanged, and the deadweight loss is small. If they are elastic, the quantity changes substantially and the deadweight loss is large. This is why economists tend to recommend taxing inelastic goods (low deadweight loss per rupee of revenue) and avoiding interventions that affect highly elastic markets (high deadweight loss).

## Why this matters in policy

Deadweight-loss analysis is useful for comparing the efficiency cost of alternative interventions. A tax that raises the same revenue from an elastic market is more costly in welfare terms than the same tax on an inelastic market. A subsidy that produces the same behaviour change with a smaller distortion is preferable to one that produces the same change with a larger distortion. The analysis doesn't settle distributional and equity questions, but it does help reformers see which interventions cost more in welfare terms than they need to.
