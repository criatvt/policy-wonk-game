# Non-Monetary Incentives

The kinds of non-monetary incentives that show up most reliably in policy work are *status*, *recognition*, *social approval*, *time cost*, and the *psychic cost or reward* of acting on internalised values. None of them is fully captured by money; all of them can be deliberately shaped by policy design.

Status and recognition are powerful in domains where public visibility is feasible. School league tables, hospital quality ratings, public ranking of municipal performance, and visible awards for civil servants all use status as a motivator. The mechanism works because the relevant actors care about how they appear to peers and to the public. Where the underlying activity is opaque (so the public can't see who is performing well), status-based incentives can't take hold; transparency is a precondition for them to work.

Social approval and norm-following operate through the same channel but are diffuse rather than concentrated. Public-health campaigns that establish a new behaviour as "what good people do" — seatbelts, handwashing, vaccination — change behaviour cheaply when they work. They work when the new norm visibly displaces the old and when most people in the relevant community appear to be following it.

Time and hassle cost are underrated as policy levers. People don't only respond to price; they respond to how complicated and irritating the process is. Making welfare benefits easier to claim, making tax compliance less burdensome, or making low-emission alternatives more convenient changes uptake substantially even when the direct monetary terms are unchanged. The reverse is also true — deliberately making something inconvenient is a policy lever, used (intentionally or not) in licensing regimes and bureaucratic procedures across many domains.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes that combine monetary and non-monetary incentives typically outperform programmes that rely on one alone. Tax compliance built on visible enforcement plus public norms about civic duty does better than enforcement alone. Behaviour-change programmes built on convenience, status, and price together do better than price alone. Designing the mix deliberately is part of the policy work.
