# Consumer Surplus

Consumer surplus is the additional value consumers get from a purchase, measured as the difference between the maximum they would have been willing to pay and what they actually paid. If you would have been willing to pay 500 rupees for a meal but only had to pay 300, your consumer surplus from that purchase is 200 rupees — the welfare gain you captured because the market price was below your willingness to pay.

Aggregated across all consumers in a market, total consumer surplus is the area under the demand curve and above the market price. Lower prices typically expand consumer surplus by drawing in marginal consumers who wouldn't have bought at higher prices and by giving inframarginal consumers (those who would have bought anyway) more of a gap between their willingness to pay and what they actually paid. Higher prices contract consumer surplus through the reverse mechanism.

The concept is useful for evaluating the welfare effects of policies that move prices. Taxes raise consumer prices and reduce consumer surplus. Subsidies lower consumer prices and expand consumer surplus. Price ceilings can produce mixed effects (lower prices for those who can buy at the ceiling, but reduced quantity available means some consumers can't buy at all). Each can be analysed by tracking what happens to total consumer surplus, total producer surplus, and government revenue.

The standard caution is that consumer surplus is one welfare metric among several, not the only thing that matters. Equity considerations, externalities, market failures, and dynamic effects (innovation, investment) all interact with the static consumer-surplus analysis. Maximising consumer surplus alone, for instance, would argue for price controls in many markets where the long-run dynamic effects on supply and innovation are actually negative.

## Why this matters in policy

Consumer-surplus analysis is the workhorse of welfare-economics policy evaluation. Properly applied, it gives a defensible first-order estimate of how much consumers gain or lose from a policy change. Improperly applied — taking it as the only consideration, ignoring distributional and dynamic effects — it produces recommendations that look rigorous but fail in implementation.
