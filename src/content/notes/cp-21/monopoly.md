# Monopoly

A monopoly exists when a single firm controls the supply of a good or service and faces no effective competition. In a competitive market, firms cannot raise prices above their marginal cost for long because competitors will undercut them; the discipline of competition keeps prices close to costs and quantities at efficient levels. In a monopolised market, the single firm can profitably restrict output and raise prices above marginal cost because consumers have nowhere else to go.

The welfare consequences are several. Consumers who would have bought at competitive prices either don't buy at all (deadweight loss) or buy at the monopolised price (transfer from consumer surplus to monopolist profit). Producers' total revenue rises, but so does inefficiency, because the quantity exchanged is below the efficient level. The combination is a classic market failure: the market produces less of the good than the social optimum would imply, and at a higher price.

Monopolies arise from several sources. *Natural monopolies* exist where the technology of production makes it most efficient for a single firm to serve the entire market (water utilities, electricity distribution, fixed-line telecom in older eras). Here the problem is structural and the standard response is regulation or public ownership. *Created monopolies* exist where a firm has acquired market power through patents, government-granted licences, or anticompetitive practices. Here the response can be reform of the underlying source — patent reform, licensing reform, anti-trust enforcement. *Network monopolies* exist where the value of the product depends on how many other people use it, producing winner-take-all dynamics (social media platforms, payment networks).

Anti-trust policy is the standard response to created and network monopolies. The aim is to maintain competitive market structures by preventing mergers that would create monopoly power and by breaking up firms that have acquired it through anticompetitive conduct. Regulation is the standard response to natural monopolies — setting prices to mimic the competitive outcome while allowing the single firm to operate.

## Why this matters in policy

Monopoly power is a perennial source of welfare loss, and the policy response varies by source. Recognising whether a particular market is naturally monopolised, monopolised by regulation, or monopolised by anticompetitive conduct determines whether the right response is regulation, deregulation, or anti-trust enforcement.
