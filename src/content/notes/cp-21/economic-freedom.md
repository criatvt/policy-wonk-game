# Economic Freedom

Economic-freedom indices try to measure the extent to which economic decisions are made by individuals and firms rather than by the state. The components typically include the protection of property rights, the freedom to trade internationally, the size of government in the economy, monetary stability, and the regulatory burden on business. Cross-country comparisons consistently show that countries with higher economic-freedom scores tend to have higher per-capita incomes, more rapid growth, and more innovation than countries with lower scores. The correlation is real and reasonably robust.

The interesting question is the causal mechanism. The straightforward reading is that economic freedom *causes* prosperity — markets coordinate dispersed decisions better than planners, individuals invest more when they can capture the returns, and innovation thrives where entrepreneurs can keep what they create. The alternative reading is that economic freedom is *correlated with* prosperity through institutional quality: prosperous countries have the rule of law, secure property rights, and effective courts, and these are what let economic freedom function. The instrument is rule of law; economic freedom rides on top of it.

The distinction matters for policy. The simple causal reading suggests that liberalisation will deliver prosperity in any context: reduce the state's role, free up markets, watch the gains arrive. The institutional reading suggests that liberalisation without institutional infrastructure produces different outcomes: predatory privatisation, regulatory vacuum, oligarchic capture, and the kind of disastrous transitions seen in some former Soviet states. Both readings are partly true, and the right calibration depends on the specific institutional starting point.

For policy, the upshot is that the relationship between economic freedom and prosperity is not a free lunch. Building the institutional foundations — courts that enforce contracts, regulators that work, property rights that hold up — is the slower work that has to accompany or precede liberalisation for the gains to materialise. Where the foundations are weak, pure liberalisation often produces worse outcomes than the status quo.

## Why this matters in policy

Reform programmes informed by the economic-freedom literature work best when they pay attention to institutional preconditions. Pure deregulation programmes in weak-institution settings tend to disappoint or backfire; combined programmes that build institutional capacity alongside market opening tend to outperform either approach alone.
