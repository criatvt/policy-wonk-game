# Behavioural Response to Incentives

The empirical literature on how people respond to incentives has accumulated a few stable patterns worth keeping in mind. First, response is *real and substantial*: across many domains and many populations, changing the costs and rewards of an activity changes how much of it people do. Demand for petrol falls when its price rises. Smoking declines under higher cigarette taxes. Recycling increases when curb-side collection is free and convenient. The price-quantity relationship is so general that economics has built much of its analytical machinery on it.

Second, response is often *delayed*. People do not adjust instantly to new prices and rules; they take time to learn, experiment, and switch. Short-run elasticities are typically smaller than long-run elasticities. A petrol-price increase produces modest reduction in driving in the first months and substantial reduction in petrol-using vehicle purchases over years. Policy that judges effectiveness by the first month of data typically underestimates the long-run effect.

Third, response is *non-uniform* across actors. Different people, firms, and households respond differently to the same incentive depending on their elasticities, their substitution possibilities, and their information environments. Average elasticity masks substantial variation: most of the response often comes from a minority of actors with the most flexibility, while inflexible actors barely respond at all.

Fourth, response is *creative*. People find paths that the rule-makers didn't anticipate. They substitute toward unregulated alternatives, find loopholes in regulations, and discover whole new business models in response to new incentive structures.

## Why this matters in policy

Designing for behavioural response means setting reasonable expectations about how much, how quickly, and through what channels actors will adjust. Programmes that expect uniform, instant, predictable response tend to disappoint. Programmes that build in time for response, monitor for unexpected creative paths, and stay open to mid-course adjustment tend to perform better.
