# Demand Response to Price

When the price of something rises substantially — petrol, electricity, food staple, a regulated service — observers often expect either no response or a dramatic collapse in consumption. The empirical pattern is rarely either. People typically adjust at the margin, finding smaller changes that reduce consumption without abandoning it entirely.

Higher petrol prices produce carpooling, trip-combining, slightly slower driving, working from home one extra day a week, and, over the medium term, more fuel-efficient vehicle choices. Each adjustment is small but the cumulative reduction in petrol consumption is substantial. Higher electricity tariffs produce less air-conditioning, more careful appliance use, switching to LED bulbs, and over time investments in insulation and efficient appliances. The same pattern repeats across staple foods, regulated services, and most categories of consumer behaviour.

The economic vocabulary is *substitution* and *income effects*. Substitution effects shift consumers toward cheaper alternatives within the same broad category (carpooling instead of solo driving). Income effects shift them toward different categories when their effective real income falls (fewer leisure activities; smaller portions). Both effects operate together, and their combination determines the elasticity of demand for the good in question.

The point for policy is that demand response is graduated, not binary. Price-based interventions that produce small per-actor effects across millions of actors can produce large aggregate changes. The reverse is also true: large per-actor effects on a small number of actors may produce modest aggregate change. The right calibration depends on the distribution of behaviours and the substitution possibilities available.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy designs that assume "people will stop consuming this entirely" or "people will not change behaviour" both miss the empirical reality. People will adjust, often substantially, often creatively, but usually through margin-level changes rather than all-or-nothing decisions. Designing for the actual response pattern produces more accurate predictions and better-calibrated interventions.
