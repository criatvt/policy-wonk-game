# Creative Responses to Incentives

A recurring lesson from the incentives literature is that actors facing constraints find creative responses the regulators didn't anticipate. The classic illustration is the Chilean bread-price-control episode. Under Allende's price controls, bakers were required to sell standard loaves below their cost of production. The economically rational response was to stop selling bread, but the regulation tried to prevent that. So bakers found other paths. They reduced loaf size to within the rules. When that became regulated, they sold raw dough rather than baked bread. When the rules tried to catch this, they added water to the dough to meet minimum weight requirements while reducing actual bread content. Each move was legal under the current rules and absurd against the intent.

The pattern is not specific to Chile or to bread. Rent control in many cities has produced reductions in maintenance, shifts to short-term rentals, refusal to rent to less-favoured tenants, and outright withdrawal of units from the market. Performance-pay schemes in schools have produced teaching to the test, narrowing of curricula, and outright cheating. Tax-shelter rules have produced industries devoted to inventing structures that meet the letter while bypassing the intent. The dynamic is general: where the rules constrain one variable, actors who care about the outcome push on the unregulated variables.

The implication for policy design is that the regulator is always playing against intelligent actors who will read the rules and find paths through. A rule that closes one path opens another. A rule that closes multiple paths simultaneously may be hard to write without unintended overreach. A rule that addresses the underlying incentive — rather than chasing surface behaviours — is often more durable than one that tries to enumerate every prohibited action.

## Why this matters in policy

The intelligent-adversary assumption is the right working frame for most policy. Rule-makers who assume passive compliance produce regulations that get gamed in ways that look obvious in retrospect. Rule-makers who model active response build regulations that hold up better, often by addressing the underlying incentive structure rather than its surface manifestations.
