# The Kidney Shortage

The shortage of kidneys for transplant in countries like the United States is a recurring case study in incentive design because the underlying economics are clear and the policy response is constrained by ethical considerations the economics alone cannot settle. Each year, thousands of patients die waiting for kidneys that never arrive. The supply, dependent on altruistic donation from living donors and the families of the deceased, falls short of demand by a substantial margin. Each year the waiting list lengthens. The pattern has held for decades and shows no signs of resolving itself within the current institutional framework.

The economic diagnosis is straightforward. The supply of kidneys is constrained at the altruistic level because no monetary compensation is permitted. Demand is constrained only by the medical urgency of the patient — those who need a transplant cannot easily substitute another good or service. The gap between supply and demand is the shortage, allocated by waiting time and clinical urgency rather than by price.

The standard economic prescription would be to allow some form of compensation for donation. Even modest payments to living donors, or to the families of deceased donors, would substantially increase supply if the experience of other countries (Iran has had a regulated paid-kidney market for decades) is generalisable. The shortage would shrink, more lives would be saved, and the cost of kidney transplant programmes overall would fall (because patients on dialysis cost more per year than transplant patients in steady state).

The political and ethical resistance is also substantial. Critics argue that paid markets in organs commodify the human body, exploit the poor as suppliers, change the character of donation from a gift to a transaction, and may produce harm to donors that they did not fully understand. The arguments are not weightless even when they are contested.

## Why this matters in policy

The case sharpens the question of when economic efficiency should yield to other values. Most policy contexts don't reach this intensity, but the kidney debate makes the trade-off visible. It is also a useful reminder that incentive design can change outcomes dramatically, and that choosing not to use incentives is itself a policy choice with consequences — in this case, ongoing deaths on the waiting list.
