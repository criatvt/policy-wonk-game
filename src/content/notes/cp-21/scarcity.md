# Scarcity

Scarcity is the starting condition for almost everything in economics. The combination of limited resources and effectively unlimited wants means that choices have to be made: any rupee, any hour, any acre of land used one way is unavailable for use another way. Most policy problems are scarcity problems in disguise — how to allocate limited budgets across many programmes, limited bureaucratic attention across many issues, limited public time across many priorities.

Scarcity is what makes opportunity cost a real thing. If resources were unlimited there would be no need to pick between alternatives; you could have all of them. Because resources are limited, every choice forecloses other choices, and the value of what is foregone is the cost of what is chosen. The concept of cost only makes sense in a world of scarcity.

The way scarcity is allocated matters. Markets allocate scarcity through prices: limited supply meets demand, prices rise until quantity demanded equals quantity supplied, and the resource flows to whoever values it most in monetary terms. Queues allocate scarcity through time: limited supply meets demand, queues form, and the resource flows to whoever is most willing to wait. Lotteries allocate through chance. Bureaucratic discretion allocates through judgement, and often through political pressure. Each mechanism produces different distributions of who gets the scarce resource.

For policy, the choice of allocation mechanism is consequential. Price allocation is efficient in the technical sense but distributes by ability to pay. Queue allocation distributes by ability to wait. Lottery allocation is even but unrelated to need or merit. Bureaucratic allocation can target need but is vulnerable to capture and corruption. Most actual systems mix mechanisms — partial pricing, eligibility rules, lotteries for excess demand, and so on — depending on the values the policy is trying to express.

## Why this matters in policy

Pretending that scarcity doesn't exist is a recurring failure mode in policy. Programmes that promise universal benefits without fiscal capacity to deliver them tend to ration through other channels (delays, paperwork, geographic exclusion). Honest engagement with scarcity asks: what is the budget, what are the priorities, who gets what, and on what basis is the rationing being done?
