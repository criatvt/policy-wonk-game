# Elasticity

Elasticity is the technical term for how responsive quantity is to changes in price. *Elastic demand* moves substantially in response to small price changes; *inelastic demand* barely moves even when prices change a lot. The distinction matters because almost every price-based policy lever — taxes, subsidies, fees, charges — produces effects whose magnitude depends on elasticity.

A demand that is highly elastic responds eagerly to price changes. Discretionary goods, goods with close substitutes, goods consumed on long-time-horizons (vacations, cars, restaurant meals) tend to have elastic demand. Higher prices produce substantial quantity reduction; lower prices produce substantial quantity increase. Taxes on elastic goods reduce consumption sharply but raise modest revenue, because the tax base shrinks as the tax rises.

A demand that is highly inelastic barely responds. Essential goods, goods with no close substitutes, addictive goods, and goods that consumers need urgently in the short run tend to be inelastic. Insulin demand is highly inelastic. Petrol demand is inelastic in the short run and more elastic in the long run as people change vehicles and locations. Tobacco demand is moderately inelastic because of addiction. Taxes on inelastic goods raise substantial revenue while producing modest behavioural change.

The same good can have different elasticities across different time horizons and different populations. Short-run elasticities are typically smaller than long-run ones because adjustment takes time. Low-income elasticities for staple goods are often lower than for the same good in higher-income consumers because the necessity bites harder.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy that uses prices as a lever requires elasticity estimates to predict the effect. A sin tax expecting large behaviour change requires elastic demand; if demand is inelastic, the tax raises revenue without changing behaviour much. A subsidy expecting large consumption response also requires elasticity; if demand is inelastic, the subsidy produces a transfer rather than a behaviour shift. Getting the elasticity estimate right is upstream of getting the design right.
