# Time Cost

Time is a resource with its own opportunity cost. Every hour spent on one activity is an hour not spent on another, and the people whose time is most valuable to them — working adults, primary caregivers, small-business owners — face the highest time costs when interacting with any time-consuming process.

This matters for policy more than it usually gets credit for. A welfare benefit that requires three hours of queueing at a district office to apply, plus more time to collect monthly, imposes a substantial time cost on the beneficiaries. The poorest beneficiaries may have the most flexible time and the lowest time cost; working-poor beneficiaries, the people most in need of the benefit, may face the highest time cost per hour foregone. Programmes that ignore the time burden tend to find that uptake is concentrated among those who can afford the time, not those who most need the benefit.

The same logic operates in regulation and compliance. A regulatory regime that requires firms to file extensive paperwork imposes time costs that fall most heavily on small firms with no compliance staff. Tax-compliance regimes that require careful record-keeping fall hardest on the smallest filers. Permit regimes that require many in-person visits impose time costs that bias toward applicants with paid intermediaries.

The corollary is that *convenience is a policy lever*. Making welfare benefits easier to claim, making tax filing simpler, making permits issuable through a single window — each of these reduces time cost and increases effective uptake without changing the formal terms of the programme. Pre-filling tax returns, direct cash transfers, and digital-first service delivery are recent examples of convenience-as-policy in operation.

## Why this matters in policy

The most efficient way to reduce uptake of a programme without admitting you want to is to make it time-expensive to engage with. The reverse is also true: the most efficient way to expand uptake without changing the programme's substance is to reduce the time it takes to use. Either direction is a policy choice with real consequences.
