# The Price Mechanism

Friedrich Hayek's deepest contribution to economic thought is the observation that no central authority can possibly hold all the knowledge needed to allocate resources well. The knowledge is *dispersed* — held in the heads of millions of producers and consumers, distributed across regions and sectors, perishable in the sense that what is true today may not be true tomorrow. Yet somehow, in functioning market economies, resources flow toward their highest-value uses. Hayek's answer to how this happens is the price mechanism.

Prices are signals that summarise vast amounts of dispersed information. When demand for steel rises somewhere in the world, the price of steel rises everywhere it is traded. Producers of steel-using goods see the higher price, learn that steel has become more valuable, and adjust their production. None of them needs to know *why* steel prices rose — drought, war, new factory demand somewhere distant — to respond correctly. The price tells them everything they need to know for their own decisions.

The system has features that no central planner could replicate. It is continuous: prices update minute by minute as conditions change. It is decentralised: no one needs to be in charge for the whole thing to work. It is robust to error in any single piece: one mistaken producer or consumer cannot derail the system because everyone else continues responding to the correct signal. And it works on partial information: each actor needs to know only enough for their own decisions, not the whole picture.

The mechanism has well-known limits. When prices fail to capture true costs (externalities), the signal is misleading. When information is sharply asymmetric, prices don't equilibrate well. When markets are missing entirely (public goods, future goods), the mechanism cannot operate. Policy intervenes in these failure cases, but should do so understanding what the price mechanism is doing well, not just where it fails.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy that interferes with price signals does so at a cost — the loss of the information the prices were carrying. Sometimes the cost is worth paying because the prices were wrong (externalities) or because distributional concerns override efficiency (essential goods). But the cost is real, and policymakers should at least notice they are paying it.
