# Gains from Trade

A voluntary trade is one in which both parties choose to participate; neither is coerced. The minimal observation is that, since both parties are choosing freely, each must expect to be better off after the trade than before. Otherwise they would not have agreed to it. This is the basic claim that voluntary trade is *positive-sum*: it increases the welfare of both participants.

The principle scales. Trade between two individuals is positive-sum for both. Trade between two firms is positive-sum for both, otherwise they wouldn't have struck the deal. Trade between two regions or two countries is, in aggregate, positive-sum if the underlying transactions are voluntary. This is one of the more robust results in economics — and one of the more counter-intuitive, because it doesn't feel like a zero-sum world should have positive-sum exchanges built into it.

The intuition behind the result is that traders value goods differently. The seller values cash more than the good they hold; the buyer values the good more than the cash they hold. Each gets something they value more in exchange for something they value less. The total value of holdings, measured by the participants' own preferences, has risen even though no new goods have been produced.

The qualification is that the gains are not always distributed evenly. A trade in which one party has much more bargaining power or much better information can be positive-sum overall while concentrating most of the gains on one side. A trade between actors with similar bargaining power and information typically distributes gains more evenly. Trade between national economies follows the same pattern — both gain, but the distribution within each country can be very uneven, with some industries and workers losing even as the country as a whole gains.

## Why this matters in policy

The gains-from-trade result is the foundation of the case for trade liberalisation, but it does not by itself settle distributional questions. Policy that opens trade can be net beneficial while still hurting specific groups whose industries face import competition. Honest trade policy combines the liberalisation with adjustment support for affected workers and communities — recognising both the aggregate gain and the within-country distributional consequences.
