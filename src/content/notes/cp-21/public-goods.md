# Public Goods

A public good has two defining properties. It is *non-excludable*: it is hard or impossible to keep non-payers from benefiting once it is provided. It is *non-rival*: one person's use does not reduce the amount available to others. Classic examples include national defence, lighthouses, clean air, fundamental scientific research, and street lighting. Each has both properties, and markets undersupply them as a result.

The mechanism is straightforward. If you cannot exclude non-payers, you cannot charge for the good in the usual way. Each potential buyer reasons that they will benefit from the good regardless of whether they pay, so they hope someone else will pay. If everyone reasons this way, no one pays, and the good is not produced — even though everyone would have been better off if it had been produced and they had all paid a fair share. The free-rider problem is the structural reason markets fail in this domain.

The standard policy response is public provision funded by mandatory taxation. The mandatory part is what overrides the free-rider problem: everyone pays whether they would have voluntarily paid or not. The public-provision part is what actually delivers the good. National defence, basic research, clean-air regulation, and public broadcasting are all examples where the public-provision model is the dominant policy answer.

The model is not the only one. Charitable funding can support some public goods where collective action problems are smaller. Public-private partnerships can deliver some public goods where partial excludability is feasible (toll roads, paywalled research, gated parks). Innovation in property rights can sometimes convert non-excludable goods into excludable ones (DRM, patents, paywalls), though usually at the cost of some non-rivalry benefits.

## Why this matters in policy

The public-goods category gives the strongest economic case for state intervention. Markets cannot produce these goods at efficient levels because of the structural free-rider problem; only an actor with the power to mandate contribution (the state) can solve it. The question is not whether to provide them publicly but how much to provide, how to fund the provision, and how to ensure the provision actually serves the public interest rather than narrower constituencies.
