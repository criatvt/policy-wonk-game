# Information Asymmetry

Information asymmetry is the market failure that arises when one party to a transaction knows materially more than the other. Sellers know more about the quality of what they're selling than buyers; buyers know more about their own risk profile than insurers; borrowers know more about their likelihood of repayment than lenders. The asymmetry can undermine the market in two well-known ways: adverse selection (the unobservable difference between buyers or sellers means the wrong sample participates) and moral hazard (the unobservable behaviour after the transaction means actors do things they would not have done if monitored).

The used-car market is the classic case (George Akerlof's "lemons" paper). Sellers know whether a car is good or bad; buyers cannot tell from inspection. If buyers offer a price reflecting the average quality, owners of good cars decline (they would lose money) while owners of bad cars accept eagerly. The pool of cars on offer shifts toward lemons. Buyers, anticipating this, lower their bids further; more good-car owners drop out; the cycle continues. The market for high-quality used cars can collapse entirely even though both sides would have benefited from honest trade.

Similar dynamics appear across many domains. Health insurance markets struggle with adverse selection (sicker people are more likely to seek insurance) and moral hazard (insured people may take less care). Credit markets struggle with the same patterns. Markets for professional services (legal advice, medical diagnosis, financial planning) operate under substantial information asymmetry, with the professional usually knowing much more than the client.

The standard policy responses try to reduce or compensate for the asymmetry. *Disclosure regulations* require sellers to provide specific information about quality. *Certification regimes* let third parties vouch for quality (used-car warranty schemes, medical board certification, food-safety inspections). *Mandatory standards* require minimum quality regardless of what buyers know. *Liability regimes* shift risk back to the better-informed party. *Reputation systems* (online reviews, brand investments) let sellers signal commitment to quality through stakes they would lose if quality is bad.

## Why this matters in policy

Information failures are pervasive in modern economies — financial products, healthcare, housing, professional services, complex consumer goods. Each calls for its own combination of disclosure, certification, regulation, and liability. Designing the combination well is much of what consumer-protection, financial-regulation, and professional-regulation policy actually does.
