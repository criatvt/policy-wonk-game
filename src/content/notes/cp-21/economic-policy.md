# The Addictive Nature of Bad Policies

A recurring observation in the policy-reform literature is that bad policies are addictive — not in any moral sense, but in the political-economy sense that, once in place, they create constituencies whose interests are bound up with their preservation. Subsidies build farmer constituencies. Tariffs build manufacturer constituencies. Public-sector hiring builds union constituencies. Entitlements build beneficiary constituencies. Each constituency becomes a voice for the continuation of the policy regardless of whether the policy still serves its original purpose.

This is why reforms that look easy on the analytical merits prove so hard in practice. A subsidy that has outlived its useful life remains in place because removing it would impose visible costs on a concentrated, organised constituency. The diffuse beneficiaries of the savings (taxpayers, consumers paying lower prices) are too dispersed to mobilise. The political math, summed across these visible-cost-and-organised-resistance versus diffuse-benefit-and-no-resistance dynamics, runs against reform almost regardless of the size of the inefficiency.

The pattern compounds. Once an entitlement programme builds a constituency, the constituency lobbies for expansion, which builds a larger constituency, which lobbies for further expansion. Once a tariff protects a domestic industry, the industry invests in production capacity tuned to the protected market, making removal even more disruptive. Once a regulation creates a compliance industry, the compliance industry becomes a constituency for the regulation. Each piece of policy machinery accumulates institutional and political weight that makes it harder to dismantle.

The reform implications are sobering. Pure analytical critique rarely shifts the equilibrium. Successful reforms typically combine analytical critique with political-economy engineering: building coalitions, compensating losers, sequencing reforms to bank early wins, and exploiting windows when the existing constituency is temporarily weakened.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy designers who ignore the downstream constituency-building effects of their proposals tend to design programmes that become entrenched in ways they didn't intend. Designers who anticipate the effects can sometimes design with sunset clauses, automatic expirations, or compensating mechanisms that reduce the future cost of unwinding the programme if it turns out badly. The discipline is rare, because the political incentive at the moment of design is usually to claim the credit for creation, not to plan for eventual ending.
