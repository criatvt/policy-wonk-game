# Incentives Matter

"Incentives matter" is the closest thing economics has to a slogan, and it deserves the prominence. The claim is that people respond to changes in costs and rewards by adjusting their behaviour — often more creatively and more rapidly than the people running the system expected. Changing the incentive changes the behaviour, often without changing the people involved.

The most striking historical example comes from late-eighteenth-century convict transportation from Britain to Australia. For decades, ship captains were paid per convict loaded in Britain, regardless of how many arrived alive. Mortality on the voyages was catastrophic: a third or more of the convicts died at sea, and conditions were appalling. When the payment scheme was changed to per convict arriving alive in Australia, mortality collapsed almost overnight — not because the captains became more moral, but because their financial interest now aligned with the convicts' survival. They suddenly had reason to feed and ventilate the prisoners properly.

The example is famous because it isolates the variable. Same captains, same ships, same routes, same prisoners. Only the incentive structure changed. The behaviour changed dramatically because the incentive structure was, all along, the binding constraint on the outcome. Many policy problems look similar once you isolate the incentive structure from everything else.

## Why this matters in policy

The "incentives matter" frame asks designers to start with the question: under this proposal, what does each affected actor have reason to do? If the answer is "what we want them to do," the design has a good chance. If the answer is "something different from what we want them to do," the design will be reshaped by their responses, often into something the designers won't recognise as their proposal.
