# Comparative Advantage

Comparative advantage is the principle that two actors — whether individuals, firms, or countries — can both benefit from trade if each specialises in what they have the lowest *opportunity cost* of producing. The key word is "opportunity": comparative advantage is about relative trade-offs within each actor's production possibilities, not about who is absolutely better at what.

The classic illustration uses two countries and two goods. Suppose England is more productive than Portugal at both cloth and wine — England has *absolute advantage* in both. The naive conclusion is that England should produce everything and Portugal should produce nothing. The correct conclusion, due to David Ricardo, is that even though England is more productive at both, the *ratio* of how much wine England gives up to produce more cloth differs from Portugal's ratio. If Portugal sacrifices less wine per unit of cloth, Portugal has comparative advantage in cloth and should specialise in cloth, while England specialises in wine. Both gain from the trade.

The principle is counter-intuitive but durable. It explains why countries with apparently weaker industries still benefit from international trade — they can find areas of comparative advantage even when they have no absolute advantage. It also explains why outsourcing some functions can benefit firms even when those functions could be done in-house: the firm's comparative advantage may be elsewhere.

Comparative advantage is shaped by many factors: natural resources, climate, labour skills, capital stock, technology, infrastructure, and institutional quality. Some are fixed (climate, geography); many are slowly changeable (skills, technology, institutions). Policy can shape comparative advantage over time through education, infrastructure, and industrial policy, though the timescales are long.

## Why this matters in policy

The comparative-advantage frame is what underpins the case for trade liberalisation. Pretending that countries should produce everything they consume forgoes the productivity gains of specialisation. The frame also has implications for what kinds of industrial policy work: building comparative advantage in particular sectors over time is feasible; protecting industries that have neither absolute nor comparative advantage typically isn't.
