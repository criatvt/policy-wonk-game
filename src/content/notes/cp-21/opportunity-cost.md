# Opportunity Cost

The cost of doing something is the best alternative you gave up to do it. The money you paid is one component of that — sometimes the largest, sometimes not. The opportunity cost of an hour spent in a meeting is everything else the participants could have done with that hour. The opportunity cost of using land for a parking lot is the apartment building it could have been. The opportunity cost of an hour spent reading this note is the walk you didn't take.

Opportunity cost is the most useful cost concept in economics because it is the cost that actually shapes behaviour. People do not generally choose based on whether something is "worth it" in some absolute sense; they choose based on whether it is worth more than the next-best alternative. When the next-best alternative is attractive, the cost of the chosen activity is high even if the cash outlay is low. When the next-best alternative is unattractive, the cost is low even if the cash outlay is substantial.

Some opportunity costs are easy to observe (the market price of the same resource in alternative use). Others require imagination (the value of activities you didn't undertake, the foregone career path, the unexplored option). The harder cases are where opportunity-cost reasoning often goes wrong: people focus on the visible accounted-for costs and miss the invisible foregone alternatives.

For public policy, the discipline of asking "what is this displacing?" is one of the most useful analytical moves. A rupee of public spending is also a rupee that didn't go to schools, or wasn't returned to taxpayers, or wasn't deployed elsewhere in the economy. An hour of bureaucratic attention spent on one programme is an hour not spent on another. Every public action has an opportunity cost, and assessing it is part of honest policy analysis.

## Why this matters in policy

Programmes are often defended on the basis of their visible benefits without explicit comparison to what the same resources could have done elsewhere. Opportunity-cost reasoning forces the comparison. A subsidy that produces some benefit is not automatically good policy; the question is whether the resources would have produced more benefit somewhere else.
