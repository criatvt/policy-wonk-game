# Incentive Design

Incentive design is the policy craft of structuring rules so that the actions that benefit the individual actor also produce the outcomes the policy is trying to achieve. Done well, the policy doesn't have to fight the actors' interests; the actors voluntarily produce the desired outcomes because doing so is in their interest. Done badly, the actors find paths through the rules that produce the rewards without producing the outcomes — gaming.

The fundamental challenge is that actors are intelligent. They read the rules, identify the lowest-effort path to whatever the rules reward, and take it. The school principal whose evaluation depends on test scores teaches to the test. The hospital whose budget depends on patient throughput discharges patients early. The tax collector whose performance is measured by collections under-investigates compliant filers and over-investigates politically vulnerable ones. In each case the rule-makers intended one thing; the rules incentivised another; the actors followed the incentives, not the intent.

Good incentive design begins with explicit modelling of how each affected actor will respond to the proposed rules. What is each actor's objective function? What are the lowest-effort paths to maximising it under the proposed rules? Do any of those paths produce the desired outcome, or do they all produce something else? If they produce something else, the rules need to be redesigned before deployment, not after.

The discipline is uncomfortable because it requires the designer to think adversarially about people who are supposed to be partners in the policy. Teachers, doctors, civil servants, and citizens are not adversaries; they are actors with interests, and the rules they operate under affect what those interests recommend. Treating their responses as predictable is what produces designs that survive contact with them.

## Why this matters in policy

Incentive design is the most leveraged work in policy because the same set of actors can produce qualitatively different outcomes under different incentive structures. Programmes that get incentive design right produce results far in excess of programmes with the same budget but worse incentive design. Practitioners learn to spend time on this step that the textbook framework doesn't always emphasise.
