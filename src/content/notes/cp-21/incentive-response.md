# Monetary and Non-Monetary Incentives

A common misreading of economics is that it claims only money matters. The literature is clearer than the reputation: incentives are anything that changes the costs and rewards an actor faces, and these include non-monetary considerations — social status, approval and disapproval from peers, professional pride, fear of legal sanction, time costs, hassle, and the satisfaction of acting on values one holds. Money is one input to the calculus, not the whole calculus.

The reason economists focus on monetary incentives is methodological convenience rather than philosophical preference. Money is measurable, comparable across situations, and adjustable through policy levers. Status is harder to quantify, conscience is harder to measure, and time costs vary across actors in ways that complicate aggregate analysis. Working with monetary incentives lets the discipline make tractable models and clear predictions, and the predictions often turn out to be reasonable even though the model omits much of the picture.

The omitted dimensions matter, though, especially in policy contexts. Civil-service compensation cannot be set on monetary considerations alone; professional pride, social respect, and the satisfaction of public service do real work in keeping competent people in government jobs that pay less than private alternatives. Compliance with tax law depends substantially on the perceived legitimacy of the state, not only on the probability and severity of audit. Effective public-health interventions often work through social norms (everyone is wearing a seat belt) as well as through monetary penalties.

## Why this matters in policy

Designs that lean only on monetary incentives miss the leverage that non-monetary ones provide. Designs that lean only on social norms or appeals to conscience miss the discipline that monetary incentives offer. The mix matters: most effective policy instruments use both, sometimes deliberately and sometimes by accident.
