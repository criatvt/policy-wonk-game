# Price Discovery

Price discovery is the process by which a market settles on prices for the goods and services traded in it. The market price is not set by any single actor; it emerges from the cumulative interaction of many buyers and sellers, each acting on their own information and preferences. Where the supply curve and demand curve intersect, the market clears: every unit produced finds a willing buyer, every willing buyer finds an available unit.

The process is remarkable when you think about it. None of the participants needs to know what the "right" price is in advance. Buyers come to the market with reservation prices (maximum they will pay) shaped by their own preferences and budgets. Sellers come with reservation prices (minimum they will accept) shaped by their own costs and alternatives. Through bids, offers, and adjustments, the market converges on a price that simultaneously equates demand and supply. The price is the *output* of the market process, not an *input* to it.

Price discovery works best when many independent buyers and sellers participate, when information is reasonably symmetric, and when the goods being traded are reasonably comparable. Stock exchanges, commodity markets, and well-functioning consumer goods markets typically have efficient price discovery in this sense. Markets with few participants, opaque information, or unique goods (a custom-built house, a unique work of art) have noisier price discovery and may produce prices that depart substantially from underlying values.

Government intervention in prices can disrupt discovery. Price ceilings and price floors fix prices at values that don't reflect underlying supply-demand conditions; they produce shortages, surpluses, and rent-seeking behaviour as actors try to operate around the imposed prices. The intervention may be justified for distributional or other reasons, but the discovery function is being suppressed, and that has costs.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy that operates *with* the price mechanism (taxes, subsidies that shift the curves but let the market discover the new equilibrium) tends to produce predictable outcomes. Policy that *overrides* the price mechanism (fixed ceilings, fixed floors, allocation by non-price mechanisms) tends to produce less predictable outcomes, including the well-documented pathologies of shortages, queues, parallel markets, and rationing.
