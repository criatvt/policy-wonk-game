# Economic Growth

Long-run economic growth is the increase, over time, in the productive capacity of an economy. The mechanism is not money creation — printing currency only raises prices unless real output is rising. The mechanism is the increase in real production: more capital per worker, better technology, better-trained workers, more efficient allocation of resources, and the institutional infrastructure that lets all of these work together. Sustained growth changes lives: countries that grow at modest annual rates for several decades transform from poor to middle-income to prosperous; countries that don't grow stay where they are.

The standard explanations of where growth comes from emphasise several ingredients. *Physical capital accumulation* — factories, infrastructure, machinery — gives workers more to work with and raises their productivity. *Human capital accumulation* — education, health, training — makes workers more productive in their own right. *Technological progress* — new techniques, new products, new processes — shifts the production possibility frontier outward. *Institutional quality* — secure property rights, working courts, effective regulation — determines how well the other ingredients combine. Each is necessary, none is sufficient on its own.

Growth is not an end in itself. The deeper argument the development literature makes is that growth matters because it expands human possibilities — reducing poverty, expanding choices, supporting public services. Growth processes that bypass the poor, destroy environmental conditions, or undermine social institutions that enable further growth can defeat their own purpose. The right framing is that growth is a means to transformation, and the character of growth — who participates, who gains, what gets damaged — is part of the evaluation, not separate from it.

## Why this matters in policy

Pro-growth policy at the macro level focuses on the standard ingredients: investment, education, infrastructure, technology, institutions. At the political-economy level, it has to address the distributional questions that determine whether growth gets broadly shared and whether the political consensus for continued reform holds. Programmes that produce headline GDP growth without the distributional and institutional layers tend to produce backlash; programmes that get the layers right tend to be sustainable.
