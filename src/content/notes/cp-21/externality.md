# Externalities

An externality is a cost or benefit that an economic activity imposes on third parties — people who did not choose to participate in the underlying transaction and who are not compensated (in the negative case) or charged (in the positive case) for the effect. Externalities are the most common form of market failure in policy contexts, and they show up across nearly every domain of economic life.

*Negative externalities* — costs imposed on third parties — are the more familiar form. Air pollution from a factory imposes health costs on downstream residents. Noise from a nightclub imposes sleep costs on neighbours. Carbon emissions from transport impose climate costs on everyone. In each case, the producer and the consumer benefit from the transaction, but third parties bear costs they did not agree to. Markets do not price these costs, so they get under-counted in private decisions and the activity is over-supplied relative to the social optimum.

*Positive externalities* — benefits to third parties — are less salient but equally real. Basic research produces knowledge that spills over far beyond the firm that funded it. Education improves the productivity not just of the educated person but of those around them. Vaccination protects not only the vaccinated but the broader community through reduced transmission. Markets under-supply positive externalities for the same reason they over-supply negative ones: the third-party benefits don't flow back to the actors whose decisions produce them.

Policy responses to externalities follow the same logic in reverse of the market's bias. For negative externalities, the standard moves are taxes that internalise the external cost into the producer's price (carbon taxes, pollution charges), regulations that limit the activity directly (emissions standards, zoning), or tradable-permit systems that combine both. For positive externalities, the standard moves are subsidies (R&D tax credits, education subsidies, vaccine programmes) or direct public provision.

## Why this matters in policy

Externalities are the most common justification for state intervention in markets that otherwise function well. Recognising the externality, estimating its size, and designing an intervention that internalises it without producing larger distortions elsewhere is much of the day-to-day work of environmental, public-health, and innovation policy.
