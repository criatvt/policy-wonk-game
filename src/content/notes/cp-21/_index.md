# Public Economics — Incentives

The unifying theme of this module is that people respond to incentives — and that they often respond in ways their designers didn't anticipate. The classic examples accumulate quickly. Convict-ship captains' payment changed from per-loaded to per-arrived-alive, and mortality rates collapsed. The Pilgrims moved from communal to private farming and discovered that the same people worked much harder when they could keep what they produced. Chilean bakers facing price ceilings sold bread by a different name. A snake bounty in colonial India produced a thriving snake-farming industry. Across these cases, the surface lesson is the same: behaviour follows incentives, and changing the incentive changes the behaviour, often without changing the people.

The deeper lesson is that incentive design is policy design. Almost every policy instrument — taxes, subsidies, regulations, price controls, public provision — works by shifting the costs and rewards that actors face. Understanding *how* actors respond, including their capacity for unexpected creative adjustment, is what separates good policy design from policy that gets gamed.

The module also introduces the standard market-failure vocabulary: externalities, public goods, information asymmetry, monopoly. These are the cases where private incentives diverge from social welfare and where policy intervention can, in principle, do useful work — though the literature is careful to remind us that government action has its own failure modes.

Topics in this module:

- **Incentives and behaviour** — convict ships, Pilgrims, Chilean bakers, kidney shortages, and what they all teach.
- **Marginal thinking and opportunity cost** — the basic economic moves that drive most decisions.
- **Demand and supply response** — elasticity, consumer surplus, and how markets actually adjust.
- **Market failure** — externalities, public goods, information asymmetry, monopoly, and what each implies for intervention.
- **Economic freedom, growth, and the political economy of bad policies** — why some policies persist long past their useful life.
