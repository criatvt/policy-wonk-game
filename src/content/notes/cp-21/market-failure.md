# Market Failure

Market failure is the economist's term for situations where competitive markets, left to themselves, fail to produce efficient outcomes. The standard taxonomy identifies four main failure modes, each with characteristic features and characteristic policy responses.

*Externalities*: when an economic activity imposes costs on (or provides benefits to) parties not involved in the transaction. Air pollution is the canonical case — the factory benefits from the production, the buyers benefit from the products, and downstream residents bear the health costs without compensation. Markets ignore externalities by default; policy can correct by pricing them (taxes for negative externalities, subsidies for positive ones) or by regulating them (limits, standards, tradable permits).

*Public goods*: goods that are non-excludable (can't keep non-payers from benefiting) and non-rival (one person's use doesn't reduce another's). Defence, lighthouses, basic research, and clean air all share these properties. Markets undersupply public goods because no individual buyer captures enough benefit to justify paying for them. The standard policy response is public provision funded by taxation.

*Information asymmetries*: when one side of a transaction knows materially more than the other. Used-car markets, insurance markets, and credit markets all have versions of this. The standard responses are disclosure regulations, certification regimes, and regulatory frameworks that try to reduce or compensate for the asymmetry.

*Monopoly and market power*: when a single seller or small group controls supply, prices rise above competitive levels and quantities below. Anti-trust law, price regulation in natural-monopoly utilities, and structural separation rules are the typical responses.

The framework is descriptively useful but worth applying with care. A market failure is a *case* for considering intervention, not an *automatic justification*. Government intervention has its own failure modes (bureaucratic inefficiency, capture, unintended consequences), and the comparison is between imperfect markets and imperfect government, not against an idealised alternative.

## Why this matters in policy

The market-failure framework disciplines policy analysis by asking which specific failure justifies the proposed intervention and whether the intervention addresses it. Interventions that don't map onto a clear failure are often vulnerable to critique. Interventions that map cleanly are easier to design well and defend against opponents.
