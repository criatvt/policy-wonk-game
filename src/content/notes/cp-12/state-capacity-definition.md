# Defining State Capacity

If state capacity is the ability of a state to do what it sets out to do, the definition becomes useful only when we are specific about *what* it is trying to do. Different things require different capabilities, and a state can be excellent at one and poor at another without contradiction. Pritchett's flailing-state argument insists on this: capacity must be defined per function, not as an overall property.

A workable analytic decomposition looks roughly like this. *Extractive capacity* is the ability to collect taxes and revenues — relatively concentrated, requires monitoring of identifiable economic actors, easier in urban areas. *Coercive capacity* is the ability to enforce laws and maintain order — needs reach into the territory, requires legitimate authority structures. *Regulatory capacity* is the ability to monitor and shape behaviour by private actors — depends on information systems and legal infrastructure. *Distributive or delivery capacity* is the ability to provide public services like education, health, and infrastructure — requires dispersed presence and many street-level workers. *Informational capacity* underlies all of these — the ability to know what's going on in the territory.

These different capacities draw on overlapping but distinct resources. Tax administration relies on accountants, banking systems, and legal enforcement. School delivery relies on teachers, supervision, materials, and a working chain of bureaucratic accountability that reaches into the village. A state can build the first while failing at the second because the binding constraints are different.

## Why this matters in policy

The diagnostic gain from a multidimensional definition is large. Saying "the state is weak" tells you almost nothing actionable. Saying "delivery capacity is weak in this sector because the principal–agent chain breaks at the supervisory level" tells you where to look for fixes. The first formulation invites pessimism; the second invites work.
