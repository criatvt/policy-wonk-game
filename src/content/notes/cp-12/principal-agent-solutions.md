# Solving for Principal–Agent Misalignment

The principal–agent literature offers a standard toolkit for reducing misalignment between principal and agent. Each tool addresses a piece of the problem, and each has predictable failure modes.

*Transparent performance metrics* are the most popular tool. The principal specifies what good performance looks like, measures it, and rewards or sanctions based on the measurements. This works when the metrics genuinely capture what the principal cares about and when they are hard for the agent to game. It fails when the metrics are gameable — as they almost always are when the agent has any local information advantage. The result is "teaching to the test" pathologies: agents optimise for the measurement at the expense of the underlying goal.

*Accountability mechanisms* — audits, inspections, citizen feedback — increase the principal's visibility into what the agent is doing. They work when they actually reach the agent's activity and have credible consequences. They fail when audits are routinised and predictable, when inspectors are co-opted, or when citizen feedback channels are choked.

*Performance-based pay* aligns financial incentives with desired outcomes. It works for tasks where outcomes are clearly measurable, individually attributable, and not subject to factors beyond the agent's control. It fails badly for complex, multi-actor, long-horizon tasks — most of which describe public-sector work.

*Selection and culture* — recruiting people whose values already align with the principal's, and sustaining a professional culture that reinforces those values — works where it can be sustained but is slow to build and easy to erode.

## Why this matters in policy

No single tool fixes principal–agent problems. The well-designed bureaucracies of mature states use combinations: clear metrics for the parts of the work that are measurable, accountability mechanisms for the parts that aren't, performance pay only where individual contribution is attributable, and substantial investment in selection and professional culture for everything else. The mix matters as much as the individual instruments.
