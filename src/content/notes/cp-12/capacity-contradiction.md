# The Capacity Contradiction

A particular pattern shows up repeatedly in middle-income states: they extract resources reasonably well while delivering services badly. Tax-to-GDP ratios are decent. The military is funded. Central administration runs. But primary education outcomes are poor, basic health delivery is uneven, and welfare programmes leak between budget and beneficiary. The combination produces a kind of capacity contradiction: the state can collect the money but cannot turn the money into outcomes.

There are structural reasons for this asymmetry. Extractive capacity benefits from concentration. Tax authorities focus on identifiable, visible economic actors — large firms, urban professionals, salaried employees — and can deploy specialised expertise in central offices to extract revenue. Delivery capacity, by contrast, demands dispersion. Schools, clinics, and welfare offices must be present everywhere the citizens live, including remote areas where each unit of presence is expensive and supervision is hard. The same state can therefore master the concentrated task while struggling with the dispersed one.

The contradiction is uncomfortable because it produces a political economy that resists reform. If the state can collect resources reliably from a smaller, more visible base — and can convert those resources into outcomes that the politically powerful actually want (defence, central administration, urban infrastructure, prestige projects) — then the incentive to fix the delivery side is muted. The bargain works for the people who matter politically. The dispersed citizens at the wrong end of the delivery chain are precisely those whose voices are hardest to organise.

## Why this matters in policy

Recognising the contradiction directs attention to the political-economy reforms that strengthen the voice of those who depend on delivery: information rights, citizen monitoring, electoral accountability for delivery outcomes, and visible measurement of service quality. These tend to be more powerful than additional resources poured into the same broken chains.
