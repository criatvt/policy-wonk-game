# State Extraction

Extraction is the part of state capacity that historically came first and stayed central. A state that cannot tax cannot do anything else. The political-economy literature has long argued that the extractive imperative shapes the rest of the state: institutions get built to support extraction, and institutions not aligned with extraction tend to stay underdeveloped.

The pattern is easiest to see when you look at *where* extraction concentrates. Modern states mostly extract from visible, accountable actors: registered firms with audited accounts, salaried employees on payrolls, formal property owners, and the urban commercial economy generally. Tax administrations build expertise in these areas because the marginal rupee of effort here produces the most return. Informal economies — small-scale rural enterprise, casual labour, agricultural smallholding — produce small per-actor flows and are administratively expensive to reach, so extractive capacity there stays thin even when the underlying economic activity is substantial in aggregate.

The selectivity has knock-on effects. The institutions built to extract from formal urban actors are the institutions the state has the most internal expertise in. These are also the institutions that can be enlisted to deliver services to the same formal sector — court systems, infrastructure planning, regulatory frameworks. Dispersed delivery to non-extracting populations requires different institutional capabilities that the state has had less reason to build.

## Why this matters in policy

The extraction-delivery asymmetry is not random; it is the outcome of where institutional investment has gone over decades. Closing the asymmetry usually requires deliberately building the dispersed-delivery institutions that extraction does not produce as a by-product — and accepting that this work is slower, costlier per unit of population reached, and produces less politically visible return than building more extraction.
