# Strategic Delegation

A puzzle inside the principal–agent literature is that principals knowingly delegate to agents whose information advantage they cannot eliminate. Why? Because delegation is not chosen *despite* the information asymmetry; it is chosen *because of* it. Principals delegate to agents who have specialised knowledge, skills, or local presence that the principal lacks. The whole point of delegation is to put the work in the hands of someone who knows more about how to do it than the principal does.

This produces a structural trade-off. The more specialised the agent's expertise (technical, local, professional), the more useful the delegation — but also the harder the monitoring, because the principal cannot easily distinguish competent performance from incompetent performance in a domain they don't understand. Doctors, teachers, engineers, lawyers, and field officers all sit on this trade-off in different forms. The principal delegates to them because they know things the principal doesn't, and is then partly at their mercy in evaluating their work.

Strategic delegation acknowledges this and designs accordingly. Where the agent's expertise is essential and the work is hard to monitor, the design leans on selection (recruit the right people), professional culture (build internalised standards), peer accountability (use members of the same profession to evaluate each other), and outcome metrics where they exist. Where the agent's expertise is less essential and the work is easier to monitor, the design can lean more directly on direct supervision.

## Why this matters in policy

The design choice between professionalised autonomy and direct supervision is one of the harder questions in public-sector governance. Move too far toward autonomy and the agent's incentives drift away from the principal's; move too far toward supervision and the value of the agent's expertise is lost. Most well-functioning systems — judiciaries, scientific agencies, central banks, public-hospital systems — find some workable mix specific to their domain.
