# Institutional Determinants of the Tightrope

Not every tightrope is equally thin. Some institutional environments give policymakers wide room for manoeuvre; others reduce the feasible space to a narrow wire. The thickness depends on identifiable institutional features that policy practitioners can read in advance.

The first is the number of *veto players*. A veto player is any actor whose agreement is required to enact a policy change. In a unicameral parliamentary system with a strong majority government, the number is small — the cabinet effectively decides. In a system with a strong second chamber, federal sub-units with vetoes, an active judiciary, and influential regulators, the number is large. More veto players means more constituencies to satisfy and a narrower feasible policy.

The second is the *geographic distribution* of veto players. Federal systems disperse veto players across states or provinces. In a federation where coalition partners control state governments, central-government policy that requires state-level implementation faces serious constraints even with a strong central majority. Unitary systems concentrate veto players in fewer locations and produce different (often narrower in different ways) feasible policy sets.

The third is the *capacity* of each veto player to actually block. A veto player who can delay but not reverse a decision is less constraining than one who can entirely block. Judicial veto players can typically block; legislative ones can typically delay; bureaucratic ones can typically implement-around. The mix shapes the tactics available to the reformer.

## Why this matters in policy

The institutional landscape is what determines how a reform should be designed and sequenced. The same reform that succeeds in one institutional environment fails in another not because it was a worse idea but because the wire was thinner. Reading the institutional landscape before designing the reform — counting the veto players, mapping their incentives, identifying which coalitions need to hold — is what distinguishes practitioners who succeed at reform from those who get repeatedly surprised.
