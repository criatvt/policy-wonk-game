# Bureaucratic Structure and Information

The standard structure of representative government separates political principals — ministers, elected officials — from administrative agents — career civil servants. The political principals set direction; the bureaucratic agents implement. The split is sensible: elected officials hold democratic legitimacy but turn over with elections; career bureaucrats hold operational knowledge and continuity but are not directly accountable to voters. The combination works when each plays its role.

The combination also produces a structural information asymmetry. Career bureaucrats know things ministers do not: how the file actually moves, where the operational constraints bite, which procedures can be bent and which cannot, what the field is reporting, what previous attempts at the same problem looked like. Ministers know things bureaucrats do not: what the political coalition will support, what the electoral logic demands, which policies will get traction in the press, which constituencies will mobilise around what. Each side sees a different picture of the same domain.

The information asymmetry is not a defect to be eliminated; it is the structure that produces the division of labour. But it does mean that ministers and bureaucrats can give each other very different answers to the same question, and that a politically driven decision and a technically driven decision can diverge sharply on the same file. Most reform initiatives have to navigate this tension explicitly.

## Why this matters in policy

Reform programmes that ignore the political-bureaucratic split tend to fail. Politically driven reforms imposed against bureaucratic resistance get watered down in implementation. Bureaucratically designed reforms that lack political backing struggle to get budgets or get blocked by veto players. The well-run reform is one in which the political principal and the bureaucratic agent have aligned interests strongly enough that each is willing to use their own information to support the project.
