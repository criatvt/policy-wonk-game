# Bureaucratic Incentives

Career bureaucrats operate inside an incentive structure that the typical citizen barely sees. Their promotions, postings, transfers, performance ratings, and post-retirement options depend overwhelmingly on relationships within the bureaucratic hierarchy — pleasing supervisors, building cross-departmental goodwill, avoiding controversies, and keeping powerful political actors happy. None of these is directly tied to outcomes for the citizens the bureaucracy nominally serves.

The structure is not necessarily dysfunctional. Internal hierarchical accountability has its uses: it produces cohesion, allows long-term institutional learning, and gives senior bureaucrats real authority over how their teams perform. But it produces predictable distortions when divorced too far from public-outcome accountability. Bureaucrats optimise for what the rating system measures, which tends to be process compliance, file movement, conformity to instructions, and absence of trouble — not service-delivery outcomes.

The well-documented consequence is that bureaucrats become risk-averse on public-outcome questions and active on internal-hierarchy questions. Asking a careful subordinate to take a risk on behalf of citizens (try a new approach, depart from procedure, escalate a problem) typically requires personal investment from a senior who can absorb the political risk if the experiment fails. Without that backing, the standard career advice is to follow procedure, write the file carefully, and let problems persist rather than get blamed for new ones.

## Why this matters in policy

Reforming bureaucratic incentives is some of the highest-leverage work in state-capacity building, and some of the slowest. The standard interventions — performance management systems, transfer policy reforms, citizen-feedback inputs to ratings, third-party performance evaluations — are easy to announce and hard to embed. When they work, the gains are substantial because the same people are now optimising for different outcomes.
