# Revealed Preferences of the State

A useful test of any government's actual priorities, as distinct from its announced ones, is to look at where the money and the personnel go. Budgetary allocation, ministry size, and bureaucratic deployment are the *revealed preferences* of the state — the picture of what the state actually values, regardless of what it claims to value.

Pritchett applies this test to Indian commitments to education and health. The announced commitment is high; education and health appear in every five-year plan, every party manifesto, every official speech as central priorities for national development. The revealed commitment, measured in shares of public expenditure and personnel deployed, is more modest. India's public spending on health as a share of GDP has remained low for decades, well below the levels of peer countries with comparable income. Public spending on education has been similarly constrained. The bureaucratic cadres assigned to these sectors are thinly staffed relative to the scale of the task.

Compared to defence, central administration, and infrastructure-as-prestige, the revealed pattern is that human-development sectors have received less of the state's attention than the rhetoric implies. This is not a moral judgement; it is a description of where the state has, over decades of decisions taken by many different governments, chosen to put its resources. The revealed preferences are reasonably stable across political shifts, which suggests they reflect deeper political-economy constraints rather than the preferences of any individual government.

## Why this matters in policy

Reading revealed preferences alongside announced priorities is what allows honest analysis of state action. A state can announce a priority repeatedly while showing through its budget that the priority is not, in practice, where the political economy is willing to put resources. Distinguishing the two prevents wasted effort: pushing for marginal expansion within an existing allocation often works; pushing for a fundamental shift in revealed priorities requires the underlying political economy to change.
