# The Grammar of Anarchy

The phrase "the grammar of anarchy" comes from B. R. Ambedkar's speech on 25 November 1949, delivered to the Constituent Assembly on the eve of the Indian Constitution's adoption. Ambedkar's argument is that constitutional democracy is fragile and can be destroyed not only by dictatorship from above but also by the daily habits of citizens who pursue legitimate goals through unconstitutional means — bandhs, hartals, satyagraha against duly elected governments, mob action, communal mobilisation against the rule of law.

Ambedkar's warning has two halves. The first is that civil disobedience may have been justified against colonial rule, when the legitimate channels for democratic redress were closed; it is not justified against a constitutional democracy that provides such channels. The second is that the routine choice to operate outside the constitution — through coercion, mobilisation, or street action — erodes the habits of constitutionalism even when each individual act is intended for a defensible cause. Over time, citizens come to treat the constitution as a constraint to be worked around rather than the channel through which legitimate political action runs. That, for Ambedkar, is "the grammar of anarchy."

The argument is less about anarchism in the political-philosophy sense and more about the practical *grammar* — the unwritten rules — of how a constitutional order is sustained or unmade by everyday political behaviour. State capacity, in this framing, depends not only on what the state can do but on whether citizens are willing to operate inside constitutional procedures rather than around them.

## Why this matters in policy

State capacity sits inside this grammar. A state whose laws are routinely bypassed by extra-constitutional mobilisation is, regardless of its formal apparatus, weakened in its actual capacity. Building state capacity is therefore partly about strengthening the constitutional channels — courts, elections, parliamentary deliberation — so that the legitimate routes for political action are credible enough to compete with the extra-constitutional ones.
