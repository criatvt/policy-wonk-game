# Performance-Based Legitimacy

Legitimacy in modern democracies operates on two channels that get evaluated together: procedural legitimacy (the way decisions are made — fair, transparent, lawful) and substantive legitimacy (the results those decisions produce — visible outcomes that meet citizens' baseline expectations). A state can run elegant procedures and still lose legitimacy if outcomes are persistently terrible. A state can produce respectable outcomes and still lose legitimacy if the procedures look unfair or corrupt.

The grammar-of-anarchy reading reminds us that citizens continuously evaluate both. Procedural failures — visible corruption, evident favouritism, judicial backsliding, electoral manipulation — generate one kind of legitimacy loss. Substantive failures — services that don't work, infrastructure that doesn't function, security that doesn't protect — generate another. Both kinds add up. A state that performs poorly on both is in trouble; a state that performs well on both is in unusually good shape.

The mix matters for how reforms get designed. A government facing a procedural-legitimacy crisis (corruption scandals, judicial controversies, election integrity questions) needs to address procedure visibly — independent investigations, institutional reforms, demonstrably fair administration. A government facing a substantive-legitimacy crisis (failing schools, broken hospitals, infrastructure decay) needs to address outcomes visibly — measurable improvements in the domains citizens are angriest about. Confusing one for the other tends to produce reform efforts that fail because they're aimed at the wrong target.

## Why this matters in policy

Legitimacy management is part of the operational work of running a state, not a separate communications problem. The procedural and substantive dimensions both have to be tended. Policy choices that bend procedure for short-run substantive wins (or that prioritise procedural appearance over substantive delivery) tend to dent the underlying legitimacy on which everything else depends.
