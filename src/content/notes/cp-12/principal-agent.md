# The Principal–Agent Problem

The principal–agent problem is the foundational concept for thinking about bureaucracies. In its abstract form: a principal (someone who wants something done) delegates the doing to an agent. The principal has limited ability to observe what the agent actually does. The agent has interests of their own, which may or may not align with the principal's. Because of this information asymmetry, the agent has both opportunity and motive to deviate from the principal's preferred course of action.

Almost every aspect of how a state operates is shaped by principal–agent dynamics. The minister delegates implementation to the secretary, who delegates to directors, who delegate to district officers, who delegate to field staff. Each link in the chain is a principal–agent relationship with its own information asymmetry and its own potential for divergence. A policy that survives a journey from minister's office to village school has typically been reshaped at each link by agents whose incentives differ from the original principal's.

The framework is useful because it generates specific predictions. Tasks that are easy to observe and measure will be performed reasonably well; tasks that are hard to observe will tend to be neglected. Multi-task agents will spend more time on the measured tasks at the expense of the unmeasured ones. Top-down accountability based on metrics will be gamed, producing better metrics without better underlying performance. Agents will resist information systems that reduce their information advantages. None of this requires bad people; it follows from the structure.

## Why this matters in policy

The principal–agent framework recasts much of public-sector reform. Building accountability is not about producing more rules; it is about reducing the information asymmetries that let agents diverge from principals. Performance metrics are useful only when they are hard to game and measure what the principal actually cares about. The whole apparatus of accountability — audit, evaluation, citizen feedback, performance data — is principal–agent infrastructure, and gets designed best when the framework is the explicit starting point.
