# Democracy and State Capacity

Democracy and state capacity stand in an awkward relationship. Democratic procedures generate demand for state capacity by promising responsive government, fair service delivery, and the protection of rights. At the same time, democracy raises veto-player counts, requires public consent for many actions, slows down decision-making, and disperses authority across multiple accountable institutions. The same features that make democracy desirable make it harder for any single political actor to build state capacity unilaterally.

The empirical record suggests this is a real tension, not just a theoretical one. Some of the most rapid state-capacity expansions in modern history (East Asian developmental states, post-Mao China, postwar Singapore) happened under regimes that were not full electoral democracies. Some of the most durable capacity (Northern European welfare states) was built within democracies, but over long periods of cross-party consensus and incremental institutional accretion. Building capacity under conditions of frequent partisan change, deep ideological disagreement, and easy reversal is genuinely hard.

This is not an argument against democracy. The same features that complicate capacity-building — voice, accountability, contestation — also produce the legitimacy that lets the state operate with consent rather than coercion. Authoritarian capacity-building has its own well-documented pathologies (poor information feedback, unaccountable bureaucracies, predictable late-stage decay). The point is that democratic state-building is a different operation: incremental, consensus-built, often slow, and reliant on institutional design that survives political turnover.

## Why this matters in policy

Democratic state-builders are doing constrained-optimisation. They have to build capacity through institutions that will survive their own party's exit from office. That favours independent institutions with their own mandates, durable bureaucratic structures, statutory rules over discretionary practices, and capabilities that benefit broad enough coalitions to be politically defended across electoral cycles.
