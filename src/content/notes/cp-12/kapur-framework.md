# Kapur's Domain Framework

Kapur's methodological proposal is straightforward: when analysing state capacity, do not assign the state an overall score. Instead, look domain by domain — taxation, defence, education, health, infrastructure, regulation, policing — and ask how the state is doing on each. The framework refuses the simplifying urge to say "the state is failing" or "the state is competent" and forces the analyst into specifics.

The pay-off is that the analysis becomes immediately useful. Consider the Indian state by Kapur's domains. Defence: substantial capability, with the costs of an enormous standing military partially offsetting that. Space and atomic energy: world-class within their niches. Taxation: increasingly capable, especially for direct taxes on formal-sector entities. Macroeconomic management: respectable, with a credible central bank. Higher education research: highly uneven, world-class at the IITs and AIIMS but mediocre at most state universities. School-level learning: chronically poor outside a few states. Primary health delivery: chronically poor across most states. Police: institutionally underfunded, under-trained, and politically captured.

The same state shows up as multiple different states depending on which domain you ask about. The framework doesn't tell you why each domain is where it is — that requires its own diagnosis — but it forces honest accounting. Lumping these into "the Indian state" produces either pollyanna boosterism or unwarranted despair; the truth lives in the disaggregation.

## Why this matters in policy

Domain-level analysis is what policy reform actually requires. Reform of taxation has nothing to do with reform of school delivery; the binding constraints, the political economy, the institutional structures, and the available levers are all different. Reformers who treat capacity-building as a single project tend to design too generally; reformers who go domain by domain tend to design measures that match the problem.
