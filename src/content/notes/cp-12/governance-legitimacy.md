# Governance and Legitimacy

Legitimacy is the working consent of the governed: the willingness of citizens to comply with state authority because they accept the right of the state to issue commands, not because they fear punishment. A state with high legitimacy can govern with relatively light coercion. The same state without legitimacy needs much more coercion to produce the same compliance — and the additional coercion is expensive, politically costly, and tends to erode legitimacy further.

This is why legitimacy functions as a form of state capacity. A government whose commands are accepted as a matter of course is much *more* powerful per unit of fiscal and personnel cost than a government whose commands are only obeyed under direct enforcement. Tax compliance, regulatory compliance, civic order, public-health behaviours, and election participation all rest substantially on legitimacy. Without it, each has to be coerced individually, at scale, which no state has the resources to do well.

Legitimacy itself rests on a combination of procedural fairness (citizens accept the way decisions are made) and substantive performance (the decisions produce outcomes that work). A state can win legitimacy through honest elections, transparent processes, and visible respect for rights — even when outcomes are mediocre. It can also lose legitimacy by delivering well in narrow domains while failing visibly in others. The mix varies across regimes, but both halves usually need to be doing some work.

## Why this matters in policy

Legitimacy is hard to build and easy to dent. Procedural transgressions — corruption, evident favouritism, visible elite capture, abusive policing — degrade it faster than substantive successes can rebuild it. For policy actors, this means that the procedural fundamentals (fair administration, honest courts, credible elections, regulated police) are not just democratic luxuries; they are inputs to the operational capacity of the state itself.
