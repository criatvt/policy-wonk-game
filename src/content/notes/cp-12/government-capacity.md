# Government Capacity in Developing Democracies

Government capacity in developing democracies is constrained by a familiar combination: limited fiscal space, thin civil services with inconsistent quality, weak information systems, and a political environment that often makes the timeframes for capability-building shorter than the building itself requires.

The fiscal constraint is the obvious one. Tax-to-GDP ratios in developing democracies typically run well below those in OECD economies. The revenue gap shows up as undermanned bureaucracies, undermaintained infrastructure, undercompensated frontline workers, and the entire apparatus running closer to its limits than the equivalent richer-country apparatus does.

The personnel constraint is less obvious but at least as important. Civil services in many developing democracies have been static or contracting for decades while the populations they serve have grown. Recruitment is intermittent, training is patchy, and the cadres that exist often face an unfavourable ratio of demand to capacity. The state literally does not have enough trained people to do all the things it has been asked to do.

The information constraint is the most underdiscussed. Effective government requires knowing what is happening — how many children are in school, how many are learning, where the budget is actually being spent, which interventions are working. Many developing democracies have weak measurement infrastructure, so basic operational questions go unanswered. Without information, even competent administrators end up flying blind.

## Why this matters in policy

The three constraints interact and reinforce each other. More money without more people is not very useful; more people without information about what to do with them isn't either. The standard recipe — sequential improvement in fiscal capacity, civil-service capability, and information systems, with deliberate attention to how they interact — tends to outperform single-lever interventions, even though it is slower and politically less photogenic.
