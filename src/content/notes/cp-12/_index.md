# State Capacity

This module is about why states do what they do, and — more often — why they don't. The unifying puzzle is that capacity is not a single thing. A state can run a space programme and still fail to deliver clean water. A bureaucracy can collect taxes well and still teach badly. The literature in this module gives us a vocabulary for talking about this unevenness without retreating either to triumphalism or to despair.

Lant Pritchett's "flailing state" gives us the picture: a strong head, weak limbs — central institutions of real capability connected by spaghetti-like administrative chains to field-level delivery that doesn't work. Devesh Kapur disaggregates this further, examining the Indian state across capability domains and finding success and failure side by side. The principal–agent literature explains the mechanism: information asymmetries between political principals and bureaucratic agents that turn even well-designed policies into something else by the time they reach the citizen. Ambedkar's "Grammar of Anarchy" speech reminds us that state authority depends on consent, not just coercion. The "tightrope walker" framing reminds us that the people running all this are operating under constraints far thicker than the textbook acknowledges.

Topics in this module:

- **State capacity and its variation** — Pritchett's flailing state, Kapur's selective capacity, and why the Indian state both succeeds and fails.
- **Principal–agent problems** — information asymmetry, monitoring, and why top-down accountability often produces gaming rather than performance.
- **Authority and legitimacy** — Ambedkar on the grammar of anarchy, and the negotiated character of state authority in democracies.
- **The constraints policymakers actually face** — the tightrope-walker model and the political economy of reform.
- **Service delivery and the periphery** — why states reach unevenly across territory and sector, and what that does to whose lives the state actually touches.
