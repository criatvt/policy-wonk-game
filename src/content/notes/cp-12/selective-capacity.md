# Selective State Capacity

Devesh Kapur's contribution is to take the multidimensional view of capacity one step further and ask *why* particular capabilities concentrate where they do. His answer is that selective capacity emerges where three conditions overlap: clear, measurable goals; insulation from political interference; and specialised institutions with control over their own personnel.

The Indian Space Research Organisation has all three. Putting a satellite in orbit is a clear, measurable goal. The institution has been substantially insulated from routine political pressures since its founding. It controls its hiring, its career structure, and its internal accountability. The result is capability of a kind that the Indian state has demonstrably produced.

By contrast, school education has none of the three. The goal — well-educated children — is hard to define, harder to measure in any politically salient way, and the relevant measurement (learning outcomes) becomes available only years after the input. The institution is highly exposed to political interference: teacher transfers, hiring, and curricula are political prizes. And the specialised institutions that exist (state education departments) do not control their own personnel; teachers belong to broader civil-service cadres with state-wide career incentives.

The selective-capacity diagnosis is descriptively powerful and politically uncomfortable. It implies that the Indian state's strong areas are strong partly because they were *removed from politics*, and that its weak areas are weak partly because politics has too much purchase on them. Strengthening delivery, on this view, requires reforming the political-economy conditions of service delivery — not just adding budgets, materials, or training.

## Why this matters in policy

Selective capacity gives reformers a checklist. To build capability in a new area, the design needs clear measurable goals, structural insulation from political interference where feasible, and a specialised institution with control over its own personnel. The absence of any of these tends to predict failure regardless of how much money is thrown at the problem.
