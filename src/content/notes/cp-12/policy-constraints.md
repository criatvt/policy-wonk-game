# Veto Players and Policy Compromise

Of all the constraints a policymaker faces, the most consequential are the *veto players* — actors who can block a policy change unless their concerns are addressed. The list usually includes coalition partners, opposition parties with strong-enough numbers to block legislation, federal sub-units required for implementation, judiciary willing to strike down legislation, regulators with statutory authority, and powerful organised interests that can mobilise public opposition or political donations.

Veto-player analysis is a useful tool for diagnosing the feasible reform space. The number of veto players, their preferences relative to the policymaker's, and the cost of compensating each one for accepting change together determine what reforms are possible. The wider the gap between the policymaker's ideal and the veto players' ideals, and the more veto players there are, the smaller the feasible reform.

The standard tactics for working with veto players include: (a) building broader coalitions that change the count by adding allies on the policymaker's side; (b) bundling reforms so each veto player gets something they want in exchange for accepting something they don't; (c) sequencing reforms to address each veto player at a different moment when they are most amenable; (d) using public pressure to shift veto player incentives; and (e) waiting for veto-player composition to change through elections, appointments, or retirements.

## Why this matters in policy

Reformers who ignore veto players tend to produce reform proposals that look good on paper and die in the legislature, the court, or in implementation. Reformers who map their veto-player landscape carefully tend to design proposals that are individually less elegant but actually clear the wire. The art of governance, in this view, is partly the art of veto-player management.
