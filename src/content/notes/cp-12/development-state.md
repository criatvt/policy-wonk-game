# Development and the Flailing State

The standard development story is that countries climb a ladder: low income, weak institutions, poor outcomes at the bottom; high income, strong institutions, good outcomes at the top. As GDP per capita rises, the state matures, capacity grows, and outcomes improve across the board. India's experience does not fit this story neatly. By many development indicators — GDP growth, industrial output, urbanisation, technological sophistication — the country has progressed substantially. By other indicators — child malnutrition, learning outcomes, sanitation, maternal mortality — the gains have been modest or absent.

The flailing-state framework explains the disjunction. A country can build modern institutions in *some* domains while leaving others in their inherited dysfunctional state. The space programme can succeed without primary health succeeding; financial markets can mature without learning outcomes improving; manufacturing can scale up without the school system improving. The components of the conventional development bundle do not move together.

This matters for two reasons. First, it warns against the assumption that economic growth alone will pull human-development outcomes along behind it. The Indian experience over four decades suggests that the connection is weak unless deliberately strengthened. Second, it reframes the policy task. The question is not "how do we develop?" but "how do we build state capacity specifically in the domains where outcomes are stuck?"

## Why this matters in policy

Recognising the disjunction redirects investment. Standard "investment in growth" approaches assume that the rising tide will eventually float the human-development boat. The empirical record suggests it doesn't always. Programmes that directly attack the binding constraints in education, health, and basic services — at the level of delivery mechanisms, not just budgets — tend to produce more durable gains in the outcomes that matter.
