# The Policy Implementation Gap

The implementation gap is the persistent, predictable difference between what a policy was designed to achieve and what it actually achieves in the field. The gap is not random noise; it has structural causes that show up across policies and across countries with broadly similar profiles.

The first cause is capacity. Policies are usually designed to be implementable by a bureaucracy that performs at typical OECD levels — adequately staffed, adequately trained, adequately equipped, adequately motivated. The actual bureaucracy in many developing states meets none of those conditions across most of its footprint. Capacity-constrained delivery cannot produce the outcomes that capacity-optimistic policy assumed.

The second cause is principal–agent slippage. Across long delegation chains, intent gets rewritten. The minister's vision and the field worker's actual practice are connected by many intermediaries, each with their own incentives, and each contributing to drift.

The third cause is resource limits. Even competent bureaucracies cannot deliver well-designed policy with inadequate budgets, missing materials, broken information systems, or insufficient personnel. Policy that assumes resources it does not actually have produces implementation that those resources cannot support.

The fourth cause is unanticipated friction: local political opposition, judicial intervention, citizen non-cooperation, environmental constraints, or simply unexpected complexity in the operational reality. Policy designs rarely anticipate everything that the field throws at them.

## Why this matters in policy

The implementation gap is the *normal* condition, not the exception. Good policy design assumes it will exist and builds in capacity-realistic targets, robust instruments, monitoring, feedback, and willingness to revise. Policy design that assumes the gap won't appear tends to produce headlines on announcement and disappointment on delivery — a pattern that is itself part of why developing-state policy reform is so often discouraging.
