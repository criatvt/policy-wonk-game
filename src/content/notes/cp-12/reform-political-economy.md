# The Political Economy of Reform

A useful diagnostic frame is to read existing policy as a *revealed political-economy equilibrium*. Whatever is currently in place is sustained by particular interest configurations: who pays, who gains, who can mobilise, who can veto. The fact that the policy persists is evidence that the underlying political-economy structure has, so far, prevented change. Anyone proposing reform should expect that same structure to assert itself when the proposal moves from concept to legislation.

This reframes reform as a problem of *coalition rearrangement* rather than as a problem of policy design. The reform succeeds not when the policy is more elegant on the merits but when the political-economy coalition shifts enough to displace the existing arrangement. That can happen because the previously dominant coalition fragments, because new constituencies enter the political mix and tip the balance, because external shocks change the relative costs of action and inaction, or because a political champion is willing to spend enough capital to override the existing equilibrium.

Most reformers do at least some of this work implicitly. They build coalitions, lobby legislators, find sponsors, and time their proposals. The case for doing it explicitly is that the implicit version often underestimates how much coalition work the proposal will need. A reform may technically be modest while the political-economy lift is enormous; conversely, a substantively major reform may move easily because the political-economy conditions are already favourable. The technical scale and the political-economy scale are different things.

## Why this matters in policy

Practitioners who treat reform as primarily a design problem (what is the right policy?) tend to be repeatedly surprised by political-economy resistance. Practitioners who treat it as primarily a coalition problem (which configuration of interests would accept the policy?) tend to design proposals that have a chance of clearing the political bar.
