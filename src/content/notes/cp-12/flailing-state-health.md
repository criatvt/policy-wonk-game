# The Flailing State in Public Health

Indian public health is the canonical illustration of the flailing state because both sides of the pattern are clearly visible. On the achievement side, India has run remarkable disease-elimination campaigns — polio eradication, smallpox before that, near-elimination of guinea-worm — that required reaching every child in every village with a credible vaccine logistic chain. The institutional capability to do this exists; the apparatus has demonstrably delivered.

On the failure side, the same country's primary health centres are routinely understaffed, undersupplied, and underused. Doctors are absent. Drugs are out of stock. Equipment doesn't work. Maternal mortality, neonatal mortality, and chronic-disease management are far worse than India's per-capita income would predict. The same state that delivered polio drops to two hundred million children fails to deliver routine obstetric care in many districts.

The pattern is informative. Vertical campaigns succeed because they have the conditions selective capacity needs: a clear goal (every child vaccinated against polio), a specialised institutional structure, time-limited focus, and explicit external accountability (the WHO and donor scrutiny). Primary health care fails because it has none of these: diffuse goals, embedded in a general bureaucratic structure, continuous in time, and accountable to no one in particular.

The diagnosis suggests that adding more money to primary health is unlikely to fix it without simultaneous structural change. The institutional conditions for delivery are not present; better-funded versions of broken chains still produce broken delivery.

## Why this matters in policy

The lesson for health-system design is to take selective capacity seriously. Programmes designed with the conditions for success — clear measurable goals, dedicated institutional structure, accountability mechanisms that work at the field level — outperform programmes that simply inherit the existing bureaucratic chain. Some of the most consequential public-health gains in India over the past two decades have come from grafted-on dedicated structures (the National Health Mission, focused state-led missions in particular states) rather than from incremental improvements to the inherited apparatus.
