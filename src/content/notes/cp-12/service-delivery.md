# Service Delivery

Service delivery is the part of the state's job that depends on the dispersed, long, supervised chain that runs from headquarters to village. The chain is structurally fragile. Each link has its own incentives, its own information advantages, and its own opportunities to deviate. At the far end, in the school or the clinic or the welfare office, a single absent teacher or absent doctor produces a service failure that the headquarters may not learn about for months.

Rural service delivery is especially hard because the cost of presence per citizen is high. A teacher in a remote village school may serve forty children; the same teacher in an urban school may serve four hundred. Supervision is also more expensive: a school inspector covers fewer schools per day in dispersed terrain. Materials cost more to move; communication takes longer. Every operational task is harder per unit served.

These structural conditions are amplified by political-economy features. The rural delivery beneficiary is politically dispersed and not well organised. Their voice in budget allocation and accountability is weaker than that of urban consumers or commercial actors. Service-delivery failures rarely produce sharp political consequences for the politicians who oversee them. The combination — high cost of delivery, low political accountability — produces predictable, persistent underperformance.

## Why this matters in policy

The combination is also what makes service-delivery reform unusually high-leverage when it works. Programmes that find ways to make delivery quality politically visible — through citizen scorecards, social audits, public dashboards, or community-led monitoring — produce outsized improvements because they activate the missing political-economy condition. Programmes that try to fix delivery only through administrative reform, leaving the political-economy gap untouched, tend to produce thinner gains.
