# Multiple Equilibria

A useful framework for thinking about why dysfunctional bureaucracies stay dysfunctional is the multiple-equilibria framing from game theory. The same set of actors, with the same set of preferences, can settle into qualitatively different stable patterns of behaviour depending on what everyone expects everyone else to do. A bureaucracy in a "low-trust, high-rent-seeking" equilibrium and the same bureaucracy in a "high-trust, low-rent-seeking" equilibrium can have identical formal structures and identical individual capacities, but produce wildly different outcomes.

In the bad equilibrium, each actor's individual best response to what others are doing is to keep doing what they're doing. The bureaucrat who tries to refuse a bribe loses the kickback while incurring the social cost of breaking ranks; their refusal does not by itself change the system. The supervisor who tries to enforce standards faces resistance from subordinates, lack of support from peers, and little upside if outcomes were not measured before. The citizen who tries to demand accountable service is told that's not how things work and learns to stop trying. Each individual is rational given everyone else's behaviour, and the bad equilibrium reproduces itself.

The implication is that incremental reform is hard. Moving to a better equilibrium requires *coordinated* change — enough actors changing their behaviour simultaneously that the new behaviour becomes the rational response. This is why one-off anti-corruption initiatives often fail: they don't shift expectations enough to move the equilibrium. Reforms that succeed in changing equilibria typically combine simultaneous moves on multiple fronts — strong public signal, credible enforcement, visible early wins — that together shift expectations about what other actors will do.

## Why this matters in policy

Designing reforms with the multiple-equilibria framework in mind means investing in the coordination problem, not just the substantive problem. Public signals, credible commitment devices, visible champions, and early demonstration successes all serve to shift expectations across the system. Without that coordination work, individually well-designed reforms often get absorbed back into the existing equilibrium.
