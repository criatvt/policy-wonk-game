# Selectivity Bias in Whom the State Reaches

The distributional consequence of selective state capacity is that some populations effectively get more state than others. Those who live in well-administered cities, work in formal-sector jobs, and benefit from organised political voice receive substantially more competent state services than those in rural areas, informal-sector work, or politically marginal communities. This is not usually the result of explicit policy; it is the by-product of where capacity happens to concentrate.

The mechanism is visible in concrete examples. Urban middle-class taxpayers receive functioning courts, organised emergency services, and (eventually) infrastructure complaints addressed. Rural smallholders receive functioning schools and clinics only when local conditions happen to favour delivery — strong panchayat leadership, accountability initiatives, or particular political moments. The same state, with the same budget, in the same year, produces qualitatively different experiences for citizens depending on where they live and which institutions touch them.

This selectivity reproduces inequality through state action. Public services are notionally universal but in practice are rationed by administrative geography. The people most dependent on public delivery — the poor, the rural, the marginalised — are precisely those served by the weakest delivery chains. The people best able to substitute private services — the urban middle class — are those whose interactions with the state are most likely to work.

## Why this matters in policy

Reducing selectivity bias is not just a matter of moral concern; it is structurally important to state legitimacy. When the state visibly works for some and not others, the legitimacy of the state-citizen bargain frays along the lines of who is and isn't served. Policy responses include strengthening delivery in underserved areas, building accountability mechanisms that the underserved can actually use, and making service-quality data visible across geographies so the gaps are politically uncomfortable to ignore.
