# Moral Economy and State Authority

The phrase "moral economy" comes from E. P. Thompson's writing on eighteenth-century English food riots. He argued that crowds protesting against high grain prices were not acting out of mere hunger but enforcing a set of community norms — about fair prices, reciprocal obligation between landlord and tenant, customary access to the market — that they understood as binding on everyone. When sellers were perceived to have broken those norms, crowd action to enforce them seemed legitimate to the participants even when it was illegal.

The framework transfers usefully to state authority. State actions are evaluated by citizens against locally understood standards of reciprocal obligation — implicit expectations about what the state owes its citizens and what it can reasonably ask in return. A state that honours those expectations (visible attempts to deliver services, fair access to officials, respect for customary entitlements) is read as acting within the moral economy. A state that flouts them (sudden withdrawal of expected services, evident favouritism, abuse of position) is read as acting outside the moral economy, regardless of whether each individual action is technically lawful.

When the state is read as having broken the moral economy, two things happen. Compliance becomes optional in the eyes of citizens — they feel justified in evading taxes, dodging regulations, or supporting resistance because the state has, in their view, broken its side of the bargain first. And alternative authorities (community leaders, religious figures, militant groups) gain credibility as enforcers of the violated norms.

## Why this matters in policy

The moral-economy reading explains why some apparently minor policy actions produce disproportionate political backlash: they hit perceived violations of reciprocity. It also explains why legitimacy work cannot be conducted purely through formal channels — informal expectations matter as much as written contracts. Effective state action requires attention to the implicit terms of the bargain, not just to the explicit policy.
