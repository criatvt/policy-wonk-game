# Capability Approach to State Capacity

The capability approach to state capacity — adapted by Kapur and others from Amartya Sen's broader work on human capabilities — asks not "is the state strong?" but "what is the state actually capable of doing?" The shift in framing is consequential. A state's capabilities are the specific things it can reliably deliver: assess and collect a particular tax, run a credible election, provide functional schools, enforce a particular regulatory regime. Capabilities are observable; "strength" is not.

The advantage of capability-based analysis is that it's empirically tractable. You can measure how reliably tax assessments are produced, how often elections proceed without major incident, how often students at a given grade level are reading at expected proficiency. The data may be incomplete but the questions have answers. Aggregate "capacity" rhetoric, by contrast, tends to be unfalsifiable.

The framework also lets you notice that capabilities can be cultivated independently. A state that wants to improve its tax administration does not need to first achieve some overall capacity level; it can work directly on the capability — by reforming personnel, investing in information systems, redesigning audit procedures. The same is true for elections, for procurement, for environmental enforcement. Each is a specific capability that responds to specific reforms.

The risk of the framework is that it can underweight cross-cutting issues — civil service quality, fiscal space, political stability — that affect many capabilities at once. A capable-tax-administration project will struggle if the broader civil service is dysfunctional. But the right response is not to retreat to aggregate analysis; it is to add cross-cutting capabilities (such as personnel management) to the inventory.

## Why this matters in policy

Capability-based diagnosis turns capacity-building from an exhortation into a programme of work. Each capability is a project with measurable outcomes. Cumulative reforms add up to higher overall performance even when no one is trying to engineer "state capacity" as a whole.
