# State Capacity

State capacity is the term political scientists use for a state's ability to do what it sets out to do — to collect taxes, deliver services, enforce laws, maintain order, run elections, defend territory. The temptation, especially in journalism and politics, is to treat capacity as a single number: this state is strong, that state is weak. The literature in this module pushes back on the temptation. Capacity, when you look at it closely, is *multidimensional*.

Devesh Kapur's framing on India is the standard reference here. The Indian state is, by various measures, simultaneously extraordinarily capable and embarrassingly weak. It runs a successful space programme, maintains a credible nuclear arsenal, manages an enormous democracy, conducts the largest elections in human history, and operates a sophisticated tax administration in major cities. It also, in many districts, fails to ensure that children learn to read in primary school or that primary health centres have working toilets. The same state, by different measures, looks like a great-power apparatus and a development failure.

The way out of this paradox is to disaggregate. Capacity is not a single attribute of "the state" considered as a unit. It is a bundle of attributes — extractive capacity, regulatory capacity, distributive capacity, coercive capacity, informational capacity — and these vary independently across domains and across geographies within the same state. A useful diagnosis asks which capacities are working, where, and why.

## Why this matters in policy

Treating capacity as a bundle changes the policy question from "is the state strong?" to "which capacities, in which domains, are limiting performance here?" The second question is answerable; the first usually is not. Most operational policy work, once it gets past the diagnostic stage, is about identifying and strengthening the specific binding constraints.
