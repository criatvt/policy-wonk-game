# Principal–Agent Chains in Bureaucracies

Real bureaucracies are not single principal–agent relationships; they are stacks of them. A national programme runs from minister to secretary to additional secretary to joint secretary to director to deputy commissioner to block development officer to village-level worker. Each link is itself a principal–agent relationship with its own information asymmetry, its own scope for misalignment, and its own potential for the agent's local incentives to override the principal's stated intent.

The asymmetries compound multiplicatively, not additively. If each link loses 20% of policy intent — a generous assumption — eight links produce a final implementation that retains only 17% of the original. The arithmetic is illustrative rather than literal, but the structural point holds: long chains tend to lose more than the sum of individual losses because each agent reshapes the policy through their own filter and their own information advantage before passing it down.

This is why centrally designed policies often arrive at the field looking very different from the intent. It is not necessarily anyone behaving badly. Each level adjusts the policy to fit local constraints, local political conditions, local resource availability, and local relationships. By the time it reaches the citizen, the policy may bear only thematic resemblance to the original. Field implementation has become the policy that matters.

## Why this matters in policy

The chain structure points to two complementary strategies. The first is to *shorten chains* where possible — direct benefit transfers, for instance, skip several intermediate principal–agent relationships by moving money directly from central treasury to beneficiary. The second is to *strengthen monitoring at every link*, so that each principal can observe enough of what each agent does to keep policy intent reasonably intact. Neither strategy is cheap, and both depend on information systems that the broader bureaucracy may resist.
