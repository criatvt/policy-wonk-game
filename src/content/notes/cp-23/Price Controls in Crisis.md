# Price Controls in Crisis

The COVID-19 pandemic produced a natural experiment in price-control policy across many goods. Hand sanitiser, surgical masks, and certain medical supplies all saw sharp demand surges meeting fixed short-run capacity. Prices started to spike. In India, the United States, and many other countries, governments imposed price controls — sometimes formally as price ceilings, sometimes informally as anti-price-gouging enforcement — to prevent what was framed as exploitative pricing.

The standard supply-demand analysis predicts what happened: shortages. Shelves emptied. Goods became hard to find. Allocation shifted from price-based to who-arrived-first, who-knew-the-store-staff, and who could navigate informal markets. The supply response that would have been induced by the price spike — new producers entering the market, existing producers expanding capacity, idle capacity in adjacent industries being repurposed — was muted because the price signal that would have rewarded those moves was suppressed.

The political defence of the controls was that letting prices spike would have allocated essential goods by ability to pay, leaving the poor without. The economic critique was that the controls allocated by ability to wait and inside information, which was not obviously fairer and was clearly slower at producing supply expansion. The honest middle ground is that both perspectives have merit and the right choice depends on what the policy is most trying to protect.

The cases also illustrated the value of *targeted* support over *general* price controls. Programmes that subsidised purchases for vulnerable populations, or that distributed essential goods directly through public channels at controlled prices while leaving private markets free to clear at market prices, achieved much of the protective intent without suppressing the supply response. The general price control on all sales achieves the redistributive goal less precisely while suppressing supply expansion more broadly.

## Why this matters in policy

Crisis price controls are politically attractive because they're visible and signal concern. They are also economically costly because they suppress the supply response. Targeted alternatives — vouchers, direct distribution, surplus rationing — can usually achieve the protective intent with less efficiency cost. Designs that lean on the targeted instruments tend to outperform pure price controls in resolving the underlying shortage.
