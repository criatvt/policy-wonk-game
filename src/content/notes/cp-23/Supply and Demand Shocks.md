# Supply and Demand Shocks

A supply or demand shock is a sudden, large shift in one of the curves, producing rapid price and quantity changes. Shocks are interesting because they isolate the curve that moved — when supply collapses unexpectedly with demand unchanged, the resulting price spike and quantity reduction are clearly attributable to the supply shock. Similarly when demand surges with supply unchanged.

The COVID-era egg-price spike in several regions is a good case study. Egg supply was disrupted as farms faced labour shortages, transportation problems, and direct outbreaks of disease in flocks. Many egg-laying operations went offline temporarily. Egg demand, by contrast, was relatively stable — eggs are a staple, and aggregate household consumption didn't change dramatically. The supply curve shifted sharply left while the demand curve stayed roughly in place. The new short-run equilibrium was at substantially higher prices and lower quantities than the pre-shock equilibrium.

The eventual resolution came from supply recovery. As farms restored operations, brought new flocks online, and worked through the disrupted logistics, the supply curve shifted back to something like its pre-shock position. Prices fell back over months. The transient price spike was the market's way of allocating the temporarily reduced supply across consumers; once supply recovered, the allocation question became less acute and prices normalised.

Shocks happen across many categories of goods. Oil-supply shocks (OPEC actions, Middle East conflicts, hurricanes hitting refineries) produce rapid changes in oil prices. Agricultural supply shocks (droughts, floods, pest infestations) produce sharp moves in food prices. Demand shocks (sudden new uses of a product, fashion shifts, regulatory mandates) shift the demand curve sharply. The pattern is similar across all of them: the shock produces a price spike, the spike signals adjustment, and the market resolves over time as the adjusting side responds.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy responses to shocks face a familiar dilemma. Letting prices spike inflicts immediate pain on consumers but draws in the supply response that eventually resolves the shortage. Capping prices reduces the immediate pain but suppresses the supply signal, prolonging the underlying shortage. The right balance usually depends on how vulnerable the affected population is and how quickly supply can plausibly respond.
