# Market Distortions and Deadweight Loss

Deadweight loss is the welfare cost of policies that prevent mutually beneficial transactions from happening. The classic case: a tax raises the price consumers pay above the marginal cost of production. Some transactions that would have created value for both buyer and seller (because the buyer was willing to pay more than the cost to the producer) no longer happen because the tax wedge has made them unprofitable. The value of those forgone transactions is the deadweight loss.

The same logic applies to other interventions. Price ceilings create shortages: high-value consumers who would have bought at the equilibrium price cannot buy at the lower regulated price (because the supply isn't there); their forgone consumption is deadweight loss. Price floors create surpluses: low-cost producers who would have sold at the equilibrium price are now selling above demand at the floor, so the units they don't sell represent forgone production. Quantity restrictions cap output below the efficient level; transactions that would have happened in the excluded portion of the market are deadweight loss.

The size of deadweight loss from any given intervention depends on elasticities. Inelastic demand and supply produce small quantity responses to price changes, so the deadweight triangle is small. Elastic demand and supply produce large quantity responses, so the deadweight triangle is large. This is part of why taxing inelastic goods produces more revenue per unit of deadweight loss than taxing elastic goods.

Deadweight loss is distinct from the *transfer* effects of policy. A tax transfers money from consumers and producers to government — that transfer is redistribution, which may be wanted or unwanted on distributional grounds, but does not by itself reduce total welfare. The deadweight loss is the *additional* loss beyond the transfer: the welfare destroyed because transactions that would have happened do not.

## Why this matters in policy

Deadweight-loss analysis lets you compare the efficiency cost of alternative interventions and ask whether the same policy goal could be achieved with less destruction of welfare. Often it can: shifting from price controls to targeted income support, or from broad-based taxes to taxes on inelastic goods, can achieve similar distributive goals with smaller deadweight losses.
