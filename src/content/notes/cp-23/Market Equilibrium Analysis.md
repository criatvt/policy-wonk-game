# Welfare Analysis of Market Equilibrium

The market-clearing price has a useful welfare property: total consumer surplus plus producer surplus is maximised at this point. Consumer surplus (the area between the demand curve and the price, summed across all units sold) and producer surplus (the area between the price and the supply curve) together measure the value the market is creating for participants. At equilibrium, this total is as large as it can be given the underlying demand and supply curves.

Any deviation from equilibrium — caused by taxes, price controls, monopoly power, or quantity restrictions — reduces total surplus. The portion of the lost surplus that is *transferred* from one party to another (consumers to producers, market participants to government) is redistribution rather than destruction. The portion that is just *gone* — neither consumers nor producers nor government receives it — is the deadweight loss covered in the relevant note.

The welfare analysis is particularly useful for evaluating price controls. A binding price ceiling lowers consumer surplus for those who would have bought at the higher price (transferring it to other consumers who can now buy at the lower price), reduces producer surplus (because they sell less at lower prices), and creates a deadweight loss because the transactions between high-cost producers and low-value consumers that would have happened at the equilibrium price don't happen at all. The total welfare effect is negative, even though some consumers gain.

A binding price floor produces the mirror image: producers gain (those who can still sell at the higher price), consumers lose, and a deadweight loss arises from the transactions that no longer happen. Agricultural price-support programmes show this pattern: farmers gain, consumers pay higher food prices, and the production above the equilibrium quantity is socially wasteful.

## Why this matters in policy

Welfare-based comparison of policy alternatives gives a defensible first-order estimate of the efficiency consequences of price controls and other market interventions. The analysis doesn't settle distributional and equity questions — those depend on whose surplus you weight how — but it does show which interventions cost more in welfare terms than they need to and which might be more efficient with different design.
