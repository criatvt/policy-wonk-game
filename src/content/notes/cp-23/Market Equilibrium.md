# Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium is the price at which the quantity that consumers want to buy equals the quantity that producers want to sell. Graphically, it's the point where the demand curve and supply curve intersect. At any other price, there is either excess demand (shortage) or excess supply (surplus), and the resulting pressure tends to push the market toward the equilibrium price.

The mechanism is straightforward. At a price above equilibrium, more is offered than demanded — surplus inventory accumulates, and sellers reduce their prices to clear it. The reduced prices increase quantity demanded and decrease quantity supplied, moving the market toward equilibrium. At a price below equilibrium, more is demanded than offered — buyers compete for limited supply, bidding prices up. The higher prices decrease quantity demanded and increase quantity supplied, moving the market toward equilibrium. From either side, the market converges.

"Equilibrium" should not be read as describing the only state a market can be in. Real markets are in flux — demand and supply curves are constantly moving because the underlying conditions (incomes, preferences, costs, technology) keep changing. The equilibrium price is the point the market is tending toward at any moment given current conditions, even though the conditions are changing fast enough that the market may rarely reach the exact equilibrium before the conditions change again.

The framework's most useful feature is that it lets analysts predict the direction of price and quantity changes from changes in the underlying conditions. A rightward shift in demand (income rises) with unchanged supply produces a higher equilibrium price and higher equilibrium quantity. A rightward shift in supply (costs fall) with unchanged demand produces a lower equilibrium price and higher equilibrium quantity. The framework is mechanical in this sense and reliably so.

## Why this matters in policy

The equilibrium framework is the workhorse of microeconomic policy analysis. Most policy interventions — taxes, subsidies, regulations — can be analysed as shifts in supply, demand, or both. The framework predicts the direction (and with elasticity estimates, the magnitude) of the resulting price and quantity changes. Designs that survive contact with the framework tend to perform as expected; designs that don't tend to produce surprises.
