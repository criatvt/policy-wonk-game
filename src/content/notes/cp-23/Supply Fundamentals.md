# Supply Fundamentals

The law of supply states that, holding other things constant, the quantity of a good that producers want to sell is positively related to its price. When the price rises, quantity supplied rises; when the price falls, quantity supplied falls. The relationship is the mirror image of the law of demand, and equally robust empirically.

The mechanism is straightforward. Producers face costs that vary across units — some units are cheap to produce (the easy-to-extract resources, the low-cost producers), others are expensive (the marginal mines, the higher-cost producers). At a low price, only the cheap-to-produce units are profitable and quantity supplied is correspondingly low. As the price rises, progressively more expensive units become profitable, drawing more production into the market. The supply curve traces out this pattern: higher prices, more quantity supplied.

The standard graphical representation is a supply curve sloping upward from lower-left to upper-right, with price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal. Each point represents the quantity producers in aggregate would want to supply at the corresponding price.

A useful distinction is short-run vs long-run supply. In the *short run*, productive capacity is fixed: existing factories, existing mines, existing inventories. Supply can expand only by running existing capacity harder, which is usually expensive at the margin. The short-run supply curve is steep. In the *long run*, capacity itself can adjust: new factories, new mines, new entrants. Supply can expand much more in response to higher prices. The long-run supply curve is flatter.

## Why this matters in policy

Policies that affect supply — input taxes, regulatory burdens, infrastructure, subsidies for production, licensing — operate through the supply-curve mechanism. Their magnitude depends on supply elasticity. Their direction is reliable: more cost or more friction shifts supply left (less at every price); easier production shifts supply right (more at every price).
