# Dynamic Market Responses

Static supply-and-demand analysis describes equilibrium after all the adjustments have happened. Real markets pass through dynamic adjustment paths that often overshoot equilibrium before settling. The dynamics arise because producers and consumers respond to current prices and to *expectations* about future prices, and the expectations themselves are shaped by recent prices in self-reinforcing ways.

The classic dynamic pattern after a supply shock is overshoot. The shock pushes prices sharply higher. Producers see the high prices and assume they will continue, so they invest aggressively in new capacity. Consumers see the high prices and switch to substitutes, reducing demand at the high price. By the time the new capacity comes online, demand has shifted away and the original shock has dissipated, so the now-larger supply meets reduced demand at lower prices than the pre-shock equilibrium. Producers who invested at the peak now face losses and shrink supply. The market eventually settles at the new long-run equilibrium, but the path includes overshoot and undershoot before stabilisation.

The same dynamic pattern appears after demand shocks, regulatory changes, and changes in expectations. Commodity-cycle markets — agriculture, mining, semiconductor manufacturing — show characteristic boom-bust cycles driven by these dynamics. The fundamentals don't change as rapidly as the cycles imply; the cycles are partly artefacts of how expectations interact with capacity adjustment lags.

The lesson for policy is that what looks like a stable equilibrium often is the end of a dynamic adjustment that included substantial overshooting. Policy interventions that target the apparent equilibrium without recognising the dynamic path may overshoot in their own right or amplify the existing cycle. Policy that takes the dynamics seriously builds in expectations management — clear forward guidance, smoothing mechanisms, sequencing of interventions — to dampen rather than amplify the cycles.

## Why this matters in policy

Markets that show boom-bust cycles often respond worse to interventions during the bust than during the boom. Stabilisation policy that smooths the cycle — by buffering inventories, supporting investment during the bust, or moderating expectations during the boom — can reduce the overshooting and produce smoother long-run paths. Interventions that ignore the dynamics tend to add to the volatility they were trying to address.
