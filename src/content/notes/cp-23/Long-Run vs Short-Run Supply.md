# Short-Run vs Long-Run Supply

A consistent pattern across markets is that supply is much more elastic in the long run than in the short run. The reason is the distinction between *intensive* and *extensive* margins of adjustment. In the short run, producers can only adjust the intensive margin — running existing capacity harder or softer. Existing factories can run more shifts, existing mines can extract faster, existing crews can work longer hours. But these adjustments have steeply rising marginal costs, so the short-run supply curve is fairly steep.

In the long run, the extensive margin opens up. New factories can be built. New mines can be opened. New firms can enter the market. Workers can be trained. Capital can be redeployed. The full set of adjustments is available, and the marginal cost of additional supply is much lower because the marginal supply comes from new capacity built specifically for the higher-demand environment. The long-run supply curve is much flatter.

The implication for shocks is that short-run price responses substantially exceed long-run price responses. A demand surge against fixed short-run capacity produces a sharp price spike. As the long run unfolds — new capacity is built, new firms enter — the supply curve flattens, prices come down, and the long-run equilibrium settles at a much lower price than the short-run peak. The COVID-era spikes in everything from face masks to ventilators to home gym equipment showed this pattern in compressed time: prices spiked when fixed capacity met surge demand, then fell as supply expanded.

The implication for policy is that interventions justified by short-run price spikes can be counterproductive in the long run. Price controls imposed during the spike suppress the price signal that would otherwise have drawn in new supply. The long-run adjustment that would have resolved the shortage is delayed because the signal has been muted.

## Why this matters in policy

Short-run interventions should anticipate the long-run supply response that would have happened naturally. Interventions that aim to manage the short-run pain (vulnerable-population supports, emergency rationing) without suppressing the price signal that drives long-run adjustment tend to outperform interventions that try to control prices throughout. Letting prices spike — while protecting the most-affected populations — usually produces faster aggregate resolution than prolonged price controls that keep the shortage going.
