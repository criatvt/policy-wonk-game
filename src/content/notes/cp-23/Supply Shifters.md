# Supply Shifters

The supply equivalent of demand shifters describes the things that, when they change, move the whole supply curve rather than just shifting position along it. The standard list includes *input prices*, *technology*, *number of producers*, *regulations and taxes*, and *expectations about future prices*.

Input prices are usually the largest mover. When the cost of labour, raw materials, energy, or capital rises, producers' marginal cost rises and supply at every price contracts. The curve shifts left. When input costs fall, the curve shifts right. This is why oil price shocks ripple through every supply chain that uses energy: every producer whose input bill has risen contracts their supply, and the cumulative effect is visible across many markets.

Technology is the largest long-run shifter. Productivity-improving technology lets producers produce more at any given price, pushing the supply curve right. The cumulative effect of technological improvement over decades is what produces long-run growth: the same goods become cheaper because the supply curves for them have shifted out.

The number of producers shifts supply directly. More firms in the market means more aggregate supply at every price; fewer firms means less. Entry and exit are the main long-run adjustment mechanisms in competitive markets.

Regulations and taxes typically shift supply left by adding to producers' costs. A new safety regulation that requires additional inspection raises production cost. A new tax raises the effective marginal cost. Conversely, subsidies, deregulation, and reduced taxes shift supply right.

Expectations about future prices shift current supply through inventory and timing decisions. If producers expect higher prices in the near future, they reduce current supply to store inventory for later sale (the egg and rescue-puppy stories during COVID illustrated this in real time).

## Why this matters in policy

Most supply-side policy operates through these shifters. Input subsidies, R&D support, infrastructure investment, regulatory reform, and tax adjustments all change where the supply curve sits. The effects can be large in the long run as the supply response accumulates — even though the short-run effect of any single intervention may be modest.
