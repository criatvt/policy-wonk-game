# Giffen and Veblen Goods

The textbook demand curve slopes downward: higher prices, lower quantity demanded. Two exceptional categories of goods violate this pattern in different ways, and understanding both helps anchor the general theory.

A *Giffen good* is an inferior good for which the income effect overwhelms the substitution effect, so that demand *rises* when price rises. The mechanism: when the price of a staple inferior good rises, low-income consumers face such a substantial reduction in real income that they can no longer afford the alternative-to-staple goods (meat instead of rice; vegetables instead of potatoes). They concentrate even more of their spending on the now-more-expensive staple because they cannot afford to switch. The classic case is potatoes in Ireland during the 1840s famine: potato prices rose, and potato consumption by the poorest households increased rather than fell because they could no longer afford the meat and dairy alternatives. Empirical Giffen behaviour is rare but documented in studies of staple foods among the very poor.

A *Veblen good* is a luxury for which the price itself is part of the appeal — the conspicuous-consumption signal. When the price of a designer handbag rises, demand may rise because the higher price makes ownership a more credible signal of wealth and status. Demand falls if the price falls because the signal weakens. The pattern violates the standard substitution effect (consumers should shift to substitutes when a good gets more expensive) because the substitutes don't carry the same status signal. Veblen behaviour is well-documented in luxury markets and some segments of consumer goods.

Both categories are exceptions, not refutations of the standard theory. The standard downward-sloping demand curve describes the great majority of goods accurately enough that policy can rely on it. Giffen and Veblen behaviour are useful reminders that the general pattern has limits and that some specific markets need specialised analysis.

## Why this matters in policy

Most policy doesn't have to worry about Giffen or Veblen behaviour. But food-aid programmes targeting staple goods among the very poor occasionally encounter Giffen-style dynamics: a subsidy on the staple may reduce demand for the staple if it raises real income enough to enable substitution toward preferred alternatives. Anti-luxury taxation may produce Veblen-style backfires if the higher price strengthens the consumption signal. Sensitivity to these exceptions is part of careful policy design in the specific sectors where they apply.
