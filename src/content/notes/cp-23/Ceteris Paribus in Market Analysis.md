# Ceteris Paribus in Market Analysis

Supply-and-demand analysis at its cleanest assumes that *ceteris paribus* — other things equal — only one or two variables change at a time. A change in the price of the good, with everything else constant. A change in consumer income, with everything else constant. A change in input costs, with everything else constant. The ceteris paribus assumption is what lets the analysis isolate the effect of each variable cleanly.

Real policies rarely change just one variable. A trade-liberalisation policy affects prices, but it also changes the variety of goods available, the input costs for firms that use the now-cheaper imports, the income of affected workers, and the price expectations of consumers. A monetary-policy intervention changes interest rates, but it also changes inflation expectations, exchange rates, asset prices, and the cost structure of every firm that borrows or lends. A tax reform changes prices in the affected sectors, but it also changes after-tax incomes, the relative attractiveness of taxed versus untaxed activities, and (eventually) the underlying behaviour of taxpayers in ways the ceteris paribus analysis can't fully capture.

The technical name for analysis that takes the interaction effects seriously is *general equilibrium* analysis, as opposed to the *partial equilibrium* analysis that holds everything but the variable of interest constant. General equilibrium analysis is much harder — it requires modelling the whole interlocking system rather than one market in isolation — and is correspondingly less precise. But for complex policies, it is the right level of analysis. Partial equilibrium analysis applied to a policy that violates ceteris paribus produces predictions that miss the second-order effects.

Most working policy analysis is somewhere between pure partial equilibrium and full general equilibrium. The analyst uses partial equilibrium for the main effect, then thinks through the most important second-order channels (cross-price effects on related goods, income effects on broad consumption, expectations effects on related markets) without trying to model the whole economy.

## Why this matters in policy

Pure partial-equilibrium predictions about complex policy changes tend to be wrong in the same direction repeatedly: they over-estimate the direct effect and miss the offsetting (or amplifying) second-order channels. Analysts who explicitly think through the second-order effects produce more accurate predictions even when they don't run formal general-equilibrium models. The discipline is partly habit: always ask "what else changes when this changes?"
