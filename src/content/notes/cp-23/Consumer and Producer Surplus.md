# Consumer and Producer Surplus

Consumer surplus and producer surplus are the two welfare measures used to evaluate market outcomes. Consumer surplus is the value consumers get from a purchase above what they paid: for each unit sold, the gap between the buyer's maximum willingness to pay and the actual price. Aggregated across all units sold, it is the area under the demand curve and above the market price. Producer surplus is the symmetric measure for sellers: the gap between the actual price and the seller's minimum acceptable price, summed across all units sold. It is the area between the market price and the supply curve.

At competitive equilibrium, total surplus (consumer surplus plus producer surplus) is maximised given the underlying demand and supply curves. Any deviation from equilibrium — caused by taxes, price controls, monopoly, or quantity restrictions — reduces total surplus. The portion of the loss that is *transferred* between parties (from consumers to producers, from market participants to government) is redistribution, not destruction. The portion that is *gone* — captured by no one — is the deadweight loss.

Policy interventions move the boxes around. A tax on a good transfers some consumer surplus and some producer surplus to the government as tax revenue; the remainder of the lost surplus (the deadweight loss) is gone entirely. A price ceiling that produces a shortage transfers surplus from producers to those consumers who can still buy at the lower price; the surplus lost to consumers who can no longer buy is the deadweight loss. A subsidy expands consumer and producer surplus directly but is paid for by taxpayers — whose tax-financed contribution exceeds the surplus expansion by the subsidy's deadweight loss.

The framework is the workhorse of cost-benefit welfare analysis. It lets analysts compare interventions by tracking who gains, who loses, and how much net welfare changes overall.

## Why this matters in policy

The consumer-producer surplus framework is what makes statements like "this policy is more efficient than that one" precise. It gives you a way to evaluate whether the redistribution a policy achieves is worth the efficiency cost it imposes — turning vague welfare debates into tractable empirical questions about magnitudes.
