# Cross-Price Elasticity

Cross-price elasticity measures how the quantity demanded of one good changes in response to changes in the price of another good. Formally, it is the percentage change in quantity demanded of good X divided by the percentage change in price of good Y. The sign of the elasticity tells you the relationship between the two goods.

*Positive cross-price elasticity* indicates substitutes. When the price of one good rises, demand for the other rises. Tea and coffee, butter and margarine, Coke and Pepsi, brand-name and generic drugs all have positive cross-price elasticity — they fill similar functions, and consumers shift between them in response to relative prices. The magnitude indicates how closely they substitute: highly positive elasticity means the goods are close substitutes (consumers shift readily); modestly positive elasticity means they substitute less perfectly.

*Negative cross-price elasticity* indicates complements. When the price of one good rises, demand for the other falls because they are consumed together. Cars and petrol, printers and ink, smartphones and apps, bread and butter all show negative cross-price elasticity. A rise in the price of petrol depresses demand for cars (especially fuel-inefficient ones); a rise in the price of printers depresses demand for ink cartridges; and so on.

*Zero or near-zero cross-price elasticity* indicates that the goods are unrelated. The price of bread does not meaningfully affect demand for laptops; their cross-price elasticity is essentially zero.

Cross-price elasticity is useful for several analytical tasks. Antitrust law uses it to define market boundaries: goods with substantial positive cross-price elasticity are competitors in the same market; goods with low cross-price elasticity are in different markets. Pricing strategy uses it to predict how price changes will affect demand for related products. Policy analysis uses it to predict spillover effects: a tax on one good affects demand for its substitutes and complements.

## Why this matters in policy

Excise taxes on tobacco have positive cross-price effects on substitutes (other forms of nicotine; informal-market tobacco) and negative cross-price effects on complements (smoking accessories). Subsidies for electric vehicles have negative cross-price effects on petrol consumption. Failing to account for these cross-effects when designing policy produces incomplete predictions of impact.
