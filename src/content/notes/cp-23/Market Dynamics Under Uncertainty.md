# Market Dynamics Under Uncertainty

Standard supply-and-demand analysis assumes that buyers and sellers know what the current conditions are. Under uncertainty — particularly during fast-moving crises — that assumption breaks. Buyers and sellers don't know what conditions will be like next week, let alone next month. The uncertainty itself becomes a driver of behaviour, in ways that the static framework doesn't fully capture.

The hand-sanitiser case during COVID early 2020 illustrated this. Consumers, uncertain how bad the pandemic would get and uncertain whether supply would hold up, bought far more than their immediate needs justified — *hoarding*. The hoarding produced an immediate demand spike beyond what the actual consumption needs would have generated, which contributed to the price spike. As supply rebuilt and the immediate panic faded, the hoarded inventories absorbed some of the new supply, prolonging the apparent shortage until the hoards were drawn down.

The same dynamic operates on the supply side. Producers, uncertain whether the high prices will persist or whether new entrants will flood the market, may reduce production rather than expand it — *withholding*. The expectation that prices might rise further encourages holding inventory back; the expectation that prices might collapse discourages investment in new capacity. The uncertainty produces hesitation rather than the textbook strong supply response to high prices.

Uncertainty-driven hoarding and withholding produce the kind of dramatic short-term shortage that critics of pure market analysis often point to — and that defenders of price intervention often cite as evidence that the market isn't working. The economic reading is that the market is responding rationally to uncertainty as well as to prices. The shortages are real but transient; as uncertainty resolves, the dynamics normalise.

## Why this matters in policy

Crisis-period policy that aims to reduce uncertainty — clear, credible communication about supply, transparent inventory levels, public commitment to maintaining supply chains — can substantially dampen the hoarding-and-withholding dynamics. Policy that adds to uncertainty (sudden announcements, inconsistent rules, contradictory signals) can amplify the dynamics in ways that make the underlying shortages much worse than they would have been otherwise.
