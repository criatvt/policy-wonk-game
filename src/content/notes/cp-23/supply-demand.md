# Supply and Demand: COVID Egg and Puppy Cases

Two recent cases from the COVID period make the supply-demand framework vivid: egg markets in early 2020 and rescue-puppy markets through 2020-21. Both ran through the textbook dynamics in compressed time, and the differences between them illuminate the role of elasticity in shaping how shocks affect prices versus quantities.

The egg case was a supply shock. Farm labour disruptions, transport problems, and flock-disease outbreaks reduced egg supply substantially in several regions. Demand for eggs (a staple food) remained roughly stable. The leftward supply shift produced sharp price spikes — eggs that had cost a few rupees a piece sold for substantially more — and lower quantities sold. Over months, as flocks recovered and logistics rebuilt, supply normalised and prices came back down. The price spike was the market's way of allocating reduced supply across stable demand.

The puppy case was a demand shock. With lockdowns leaving people working from home and seeking companionship, demand for rescue and adoption animals surged. Supply was sharply constrained — you cannot quickly produce more puppies than current breeding programmes are generating. The rightward demand shift produced sharp price spikes for adoption fees, breeding-related fees, and resale of animals. Over months, as supply gradually expanded (more breeders, more imports, eventually some normalisation as people went back to offices), prices came back down toward pre-pandemic levels.

The contrast illustrates how elasticity determines shock incidence. Both cases had inelastic short-run supply, which is why both produced large price effects. The egg case had stable demand; the puppy case had a demand surge. Both cases eventually resolved through supply expansion — but the timeline depended on how fast supply could rebuild, which was a feature of the underlying production technology rather than of the policy environment.

## Why this matters in policy

Cases like these are the empirical equivalents of the supply-demand graphs in textbooks. They illustrate that the standard framework continues to produce useful predictions about real markets, even in fast-moving crises that look chaotic from the inside. Practitioners who use the framework as a diagnostic tool — distinguishing supply shocks from demand surges, estimating elasticities, predicting the duration of price effects — tend to make more accurate forecasts than those who treat each crisis as sui generis.
