# Income and Substitution Effects

When the price of a good changes, the consumer's response can be decomposed into two analytically distinct effects. The *substitution effect* captures the response to the change in *relative prices*: the good has become more expensive relative to its substitutes, so the consumer shifts some of their consumption toward those substitutes. The *income effect* captures the response to the change in *real purchasing power*: with the same nominal income, the consumer can now afford less of everything, so consumption of normal goods falls.

For typical (normal) goods, both effects pull in the same direction when prices rise. Consumers substitute away from the now-more-expensive good (substitution effect) and consume less of normal goods overall because their real income has fallen (income effect). Both contribute to the downward-sloping demand curve.

For *inferior* goods, the two effects pull in opposite directions. When the price of an inferior good rises, consumers substitute away from it (substitution effect, as for any good). But the real-income reduction caused by the price rise makes the consumer poorer, and poorer consumers buy *more* inferior goods (income effect). The two effects offset each other. In normal cases, the substitution effect still dominates and demand falls. In the rare Giffen-good case, the income effect dominates and demand for the good *rises* with price.

The decomposition is more than analytical bookkeeping. It explains why low-income consumers and high-income consumers respond differently to the same price changes. For low-income consumers spending a large share of their income on a good, the income effect of a price change is substantial; their demand is more sensitive to price. For high-income consumers spending a small share on the same good, the income effect is negligible and only the substitution effect matters; their demand is less sensitive.

## Why this matters in policy

Welfare effects of price changes are not uniform across consumers. A staple-good price increase hits low-income households much harder than high-income ones, both because they spend more on staples and because the income effect of any increase is larger for them. Honest distributional analysis of price-affecting policies accounts for these differential effects.
