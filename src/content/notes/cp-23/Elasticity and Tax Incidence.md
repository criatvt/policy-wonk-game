# Elasticity and Tax Incidence

Tax incidence is the question of who actually bears the burden of a tax — whose welfare gets reduced — as distinct from who is legally responsible for remitting it to the government. The two are often different. The legal liability is determined by the tax statute; the economic incidence is determined by relative elasticities.

The general principle: the more inelastic side of the market bears more of the tax. If demand is highly inelastic and supply is elastic, consumers bear most of the tax (they cannot easily reduce purchases, so producers can largely pass the tax through to consumer prices). If supply is highly inelastic and demand is elastic, producers bear most of the tax (they cannot easily reduce supply, and demand-side substitution prevents them from raising prices much).

Consider a tax on cigarettes. Demand for cigarettes is inelastic, especially in the short run (addiction reduces price sensitivity). Supply is more elastic (producers can adjust output). When a tax is imposed, retail prices rise by close to the full tax amount, and the consumers bear most of the burden. Producers' margins are largely unchanged. This is partly why tobacco taxes are politically attractive: the tax is paid by consumers, and the political coalition of producers does not mobilise strongly against it.

Now consider a property tax on rental housing. Supply of housing is very inelastic in the short run (you cannot quickly build more buildings). Demand is more elastic (renters can move, downsize, share). When property taxes rise, landlords cannot easily raise rents to pass on the tax (renters would leave), so landlords bear most of the short-run incidence. In the long run, as the inelasticity of supply diminishes (fewer buildings get built; the housing stock shrinks), some of the tax shifts to renters through tight markets.

The takeaway is that statutory incidence is a misleading guide to actual incidence. The economic question — who actually loses welfare to the tax — depends on elasticities and may be very different from the legal answer.

## Why this matters in policy

Politically attractive taxes — those framed as falling on producers — often actually fall on consumers if supply is more elastic than demand. Politically unattractive taxes — framed as falling on consumers — may actually fall on producers under the opposite elasticity pattern. Honest tax-design analysis tracks the actual incidence rather than the statutory framing.
