# Elasticity of Demand

Price elasticity of demand is the technical measure of how responsive quantity demanded is to changes in price. Formally, it is the percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price. When demand is *elastic*, quantity moves more than proportionally to price; small price changes produce large quantity changes. When demand is *inelastic*, quantity moves less than proportionally; even large price changes produce small quantity changes.

The intuition behind elasticity is about substitution and necessity. Goods with many close substitutes tend to have elastic demand: if the price of one brand of cola rises, consumers easily shift to another, and the quantity sold of the first brand falls sharply. Goods with few substitutes — particularly necessities — tend to have inelastic demand: when the price of insulin rises, diabetic consumers cannot easily switch, and quantity demanded falls only modestly even as the price rises a lot.

The level of aggregation matters. Demand for "Coca-Cola" is elastic because Pepsi and other colas are close substitutes. Demand for "cola" as a category is less elastic. Demand for "sugary beverages" is less elastic still. Demand for "beverages" is quite inelastic because everyone needs some liquid. The narrower the category, the more substitutes are available, the more elastic the demand.

Time horizon also matters. Short-run elasticity is typically smaller than long-run elasticity. When petrol prices rise, consumers adjust modestly in the short run (drive a bit less) but more substantially in the long run (buy more fuel-efficient cars, move closer to work, switch to public transport). Policy that aims to change behaviour through price has to recognise that the full response takes time.

## Why this matters in policy

Tax design depends heavily on elasticity. A tax on an inelastic good raises substantial revenue without changing behaviour much. A tax on an elastic good changes behaviour substantially but raises modest revenue. Sin taxes on tobacco (relatively inelastic in the short run) generate stable revenue; congestion charges on driving (moderately elastic) change behaviour. Knowing the elasticity matters for predicting whether the tax will primarily move behaviour or primarily raise money.
