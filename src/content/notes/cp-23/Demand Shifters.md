# Demand Shifters

A crucial distinction in demand analysis is between *movements along the curve* and *shifts of the curve*. A price change produces a movement along the curve — same demand relationship, different point on it. A change in something other than the good's own price produces a shift of the whole curve — different demand relationship at every price.

The standard demand shifters are: *consumer income*, *prices of related goods*, *tastes and preferences*, *number of consumers*, and *expectations about future prices*.

Income shifts demand depending on whether the good is normal or inferior. For *normal goods*, demand rises with income (you buy more cars, restaurant meals, holidays when you have more money). For *inferior goods*, demand falls with income (you buy less cheap instant noodles, less public transport, less budget-brand staple when you can afford alternatives). The direction of the income effect identifies the good's type.

Prices of related goods shift demand through substitution and complementarity. *Substitutes* (butter and margarine; tea and coffee) move opposite ways: a higher price for one increases demand for the other. *Complements* (cars and petrol; printers and ink) move together: a higher price for one decreases demand for the other.

Tastes and preferences shift demand directly. A health campaign that makes people prefer vegetables over fried foods shifts the demand curves for both. Fashion changes that make particular clothing popular shift demand for that clothing.

Expectations about future prices shift current demand. If consumers expect prices to rise next month, they buy more this month — pulling the demand curve rightward in the current period. Expectations of falling prices have the opposite effect.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy that affects any of the demand shifters — incomes (transfers, tax cuts), related goods (taxes on substitutes or complements), tastes (information campaigns), or expectations (announcements of future changes) — moves demand without changing the good's own price. Understanding which shifter a policy is hitting helps predict the demand response.
