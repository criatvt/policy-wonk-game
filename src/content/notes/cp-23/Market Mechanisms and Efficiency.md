# Market Mechanisms and Efficiency

A subtle point about the market mechanism is that the *existence* of supply and demand doesn't by itself guarantee efficient outcomes. The mechanism produces efficient outcomes only under certain conditions: prices must capture the full marginal cost and full marginal benefit of each transaction; information must be reasonably symmetric between buyers and sellers; and competition must be sufficient that no individual participant can move prices through their own actions.

Where these conditions fail, the market still has supply and demand and still clears at some equilibrium — but the equilibrium is not socially efficient. Externalities are the most common failure: when a transaction imposes uncompensated costs (or benefits) on third parties, the market participants ignore those costs in their decisions, and the equilibrium quantity is wrong. Air pollution from a factory imposes costs on neighbours that the factory and its customers don't pay; the market produces too much pollution because the polluting transactions look profitable to the parties involved even though they are net negative for society.

Information asymmetry is the next most common. Used-car markets, insurance markets, and credit markets have versions of this problem. The party with less information charges (or accepts) prices that reflect their uncertainty, which can produce adverse selection (only the worst risks accept) or moral hazard (insured parties take excessive risk). The market clears, but at quantities and prices that don't reflect underlying value.

Monopoly power produces a different failure. With insufficient competition, the single seller can profitably restrict output below the competitive level to raise prices. The market clears (at the monopoly price), but the quantity is below the efficient level and the consumer surplus is lower than it would have been in a competitive market.

Each failure mode points to a different policy response. Externalities call for prices that include the externality (Pigouvian taxes) or regulations that constrain quantity directly. Information asymmetries call for disclosure rules, certification, and liability rules. Monopoly calls for anti-trust enforcement or, for natural monopolies, regulation.

## Why this matters in policy

The market-failure framework is the standard analytical entry point for evaluating whether intervention is justified in a given sector. Intervention without a clear market failure to point to is hard to defend; intervention well-matched to a specific failure is easier to design and easier to defend.
