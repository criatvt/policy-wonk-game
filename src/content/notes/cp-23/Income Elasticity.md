# Income Elasticity

Income elasticity of demand measures how responsive quantity demanded is to changes in consumer income. Formally, it is the percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in income. The sign and magnitude classify goods into useful categories.

*Positive income elasticity above 1* indicates luxuries. Demand rises more than proportionally with income. Fine dining, air travel, premium consumer electronics, and second homes are typical examples. A 10% rise in income produces more than a 10% rise in demand. Economies that grow rapidly in income see disproportionate growth in luxury consumption — which is why emerging-market luxury markets often outpace overall growth.

*Positive income elasticity between 0 and 1* indicates necessities. Demand rises with income but less than proportionally. Basic food, utilities, and essential clothing fall in this category. People consume more of these as they grow richer, but the share of income spent on them falls. Engel's law — that the share of income spent on food falls with income — is an empirical regularity that fits this pattern.

*Negative income elasticity* indicates inferior goods. Demand falls as income rises. Cheap instant noodles, public transport, used clothing, and budget-brand staples often show this pattern: as people grow richer, they substitute toward more attractive alternatives, and demand for the inferior good falls.

Income elasticity has consequences for policy and business. A growing economy sees luxury sectors expanding fastest, with cumulative growth far above GDP growth, while inferior goods may decline absolutely. Tax incidence depends on income elasticity: taxes on necessities fall more heavily on the poor (who spend a larger share of income on them); taxes on luxuries fall more heavily on the rich. Welfare programmes targeted at the poor often work through inferior or necessity goods because that's where lower-income consumption is concentrated.

## Why this matters in policy

Distributional analysis of taxes and subsidies depends on income elasticity. A subsidy for an inferior good (rural public transport, basic foods) reaches lower-income households disproportionately. A subsidy for a luxury (private universities, premium services) reaches higher-income households. The income-elasticity pattern tells you who benefits.
