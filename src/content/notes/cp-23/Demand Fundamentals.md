# Demand Fundamentals

The law of demand states that, holding other things constant, the quantity of a good that consumers want to buy is inversely related to its price. When the price rises, quantity demanded falls; when the price falls, quantity demanded rises. The relationship is so general — across goods, across consumers, across cultures — that economics has built much of its analytical apparatus on it.

The standard graphical representation is a demand curve sloping downward from upper-left to lower-right, with price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal. Each point on the curve represents the quantity that consumers in aggregate would want to buy at the corresponding price, holding everything else constant. The slope captures price-sensitivity: steeper curves mean less price-sensitive demand; flatter curves mean more price-sensitive demand.

The mechanisms behind the law are two related effects. The *substitution effect*: at a higher price, consumers shift toward cheaper alternatives within the same broad category (less of the expensive item, more of its substitutes). The *income effect*: at a higher price, the consumer's real purchasing power is reduced, which typically reduces consumption of normal goods (more on inferior goods elsewhere in this module). Both effects pull in the same direction for typical goods, producing the standard downward slope.

The law has well-known exceptions — Giffen goods, where inferior-good income effects overpower substitution, and Veblen goods, where price itself is part of the appeal — but these are narrow categories. For the great majority of goods and services, the downward-sloping demand curve is a reliable empirical regularity.

## Why this matters in policy

Any policy that uses price as a lever — taxes, subsidies, fees, charges, congestion pricing — operates on the demand-curve mechanism. The magnitude of the policy effect depends on how steep the demand curve is in the relevant range, which is the elasticity question covered elsewhere in this module. The direction of the effect, however, is reliable enough that policy designs typically don't need to worry about getting it wrong.
