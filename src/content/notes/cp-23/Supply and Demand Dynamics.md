# Supply and Demand Dynamics

When only one curve shifts, the resulting change in equilibrium is fully determined: a rightward demand shift raises both price and quantity; a rightward supply shift raises quantity and lowers price; and so on. When *both* curves shift, the analysis becomes ambiguous in one dimension and definite in the other, depending on which way each shift goes.

If demand and supply both shift right (both increase), the equilibrium quantity definitely rises. The equilibrium price could rise, fall, or stay the same depending on the relative magnitudes. If demand grew more than supply, price rises. If supply grew more than demand, price falls. The change in price is therefore ambiguous from the qualitative analysis alone; quantitative estimates are needed to resolve it.

If demand shifts right and supply shifts left, the equilibrium price definitely rises. The quantity change is ambiguous. If the demand increase is larger than the supply decrease, quantity rises. If the supply decrease is larger than the demand increase, quantity falls. Same uncertainty in the other dimension as the first case, but mirrored.

The general principle is: when both curves shift, one dimension of the equilibrium change is determined by the directions of the shifts (always rises or always falls), and the other dimension is determined by the relative magnitudes. The qualitative analysis tells you which is which; quantitative analysis tells you the actual outcome.

Real markets typically have both curves moving simultaneously — incomes change, costs change, technology changes, preferences change. The ambiguity is the analytical norm rather than the exception. The discipline of identifying which dimension is determined and which is ambiguous from the shift pattern is part of how applied analysis works.

## Why this matters in policy

Policy interventions often shift both curves simultaneously. A subsidy that affects both consumer prices (raising demand) and producer costs (raising supply) can produce ambiguous price effects unless you know the magnitudes. Knowing what is and isn't determined by the qualitative pattern keeps the analysis honest about what it can and can't predict.
