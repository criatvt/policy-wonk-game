# Non-Price Allocation under Price Controls

When a binding price ceiling produces a shortage, the goods that are available have to be allocated to consumers somehow. The price mechanism, which would normally do this job by matching willingness to pay against supply, has been suppressed. Other mechanisms step in to allocate the limited supply among the larger group of consumers who want it.

*Queueing* is the most common substitute. The goods go to whoever is willing to wait the longest. This allocates by available time rather than by need or by willingness to pay. People with flexible schedules can queue; people with rigid work schedules cannot. The cost of acquisition shifts from money to time, and the welfare effects depend on whose time is more valuable.

*Formal rationing* allocates by some pre-defined criterion: per-household limits, ration cards, eligibility tied to income or other characteristics. The advantage is that the criterion can be tailored to the policy goal (more for households with young children; more for low-income households). The disadvantage is administrative cost and the risk that the criterion misses important variation in need.

*Seller discretion* allocates by who the seller chooses to favour. The seller may serve regular customers preferentially, employees and their families, or those willing to make side payments. This often produces patterns the policy was supposed to prevent (better-connected consumers getting served while less-connected ones do not).

*Informal markets* allocate to those willing to engage in transactions outside the formal regulated market. Goods originally sold at the regulated price end up in informal channels at higher prices. This redirects part of the supply away from the formal market the controls were trying to protect.

Each mechanism has its own efficiency and equity consequences. None of them is the same as the market-clearing price mechanism. None of them is unambiguously fairer or more efficient than market clearing. The choice among them — implicit or explicit — is itself a policy choice with consequences.

## Why this matters in policy

Imposing a price control without designing the non-price allocation mechanism leaves the allocation to be sorted out by queueing, seller discretion, and informal markets — none of which usually serve the original protective intent well. Combining a price control with a deliberate allocation mechanism (rationing rules, distribution channels, eligibility criteria) tends to produce outcomes that better match the policy's stated goals.
