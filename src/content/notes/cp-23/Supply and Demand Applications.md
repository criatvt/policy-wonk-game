# Supply and Demand: COVID Hand-Sanitiser Case

The Indian hand-sanitiser market during the early COVID period in 2020 ran through the standard textbook sequence in compressed time. Demand surged as the public became aware of the role of hand hygiene in preventing transmission. Supply was constrained in the short run by the existing production capacity for hand sanitiser, which had been modest in pre-pandemic India. Prices started to rise sharply at retail, and reports of price gouging — sanitiser sold at multiples of the pre-crisis price — generated political pressure.

The government responded by imposing price ceilings on hand sanitiser. The retail price was capped, and enforcement actions were taken against traders selling above the cap. From a supply-and-demand perspective, the intervention was a textbook case of binding price ceiling: the regulated price was below the new short-run equilibrium price, so quantity demanded exceeded quantity supplied, and shortages developed. Sanitiser became hard to find in retail outlets. Allocation shifted from price-based to whoever could find supply through informal channels, queue effectively, or know retailers personally.

The medium-term resolution came from supply expansion. Multiple new producers entered the market — distilleries pivoting to sanitiser production, chemical companies adding capacity, small-scale entrepreneurs setting up bottling operations. The supply curve shifted right substantially over weeks rather than months. By mid-2020, hand sanitiser was widely available, often at prices well below the original peak. The shortage resolved through supply expansion that the price spike had partly motivated, even though the price spike itself had been capped by the regulation.

The case illustrates several standard lessons. Price controls prevent price spikes but don't prevent the underlying shortage; the shortage shows up as availability problems instead. Supply responds substantially to higher prices over weeks-to-months horizons, much faster than typical capacity-construction timelines suggest, when the production technology is relatively simple. Targeted distribution channels (essential-services preference, hospital and frontline-worker priority) might have served the protective intent more effectively than general price controls.

## Why this matters in policy

The COVID supply-shock cases give policymakers a recent menu of real-world experiments in price-control policy. The standard predictions — shortages, non-price allocation, eventual supply response — all played out as the theory predicted. Future crises will repeat the patterns unless the policy approach itself shifts to target the underlying allocation question rather than the surface price signal.
